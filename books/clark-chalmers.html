Extended Cognition

The Extended Mind

Andy Clark & David Chalmers · 1998 · paper, free online
~1 hour to read Condensed read below: ~18 min read Where does the mind stop? Pairs with: Cognition in the Wild

Nine pages of Analysis, one notebook-carrying man named Otto, and a question most people have never thought to ask: why should the boundary of the mind be the boundary of the skull? This is the rare philosophy paper that is short enough to read over coffee and unsettling enough to reorganize how you see every tool you touch afterward—read it in full at consc.net/papers/extended.html before or after this page.

The authors were colleagues at Washington University in St. Louis when they wrote it, and they make an instructive pair. Andy Clark had spent the decade arguing, in books like Being There, that cognition is embodied and environmentally scaffolded—that brains are controllers for acting bodies, not sealed reasoning engines. David Chalmers had just become famous for naming the “hard problem” of consciousness. The paper distills Clark’s sprawling empirical case into a single crisp metaphysical claim and defends it with Chalmers-grade analytic discipline; it reportedly circulated for years, rejected by journal after journal, before Analysis published it in 1998. This is the paper—not Annie Murphy Paul’s 2021 popular-science book that borrowed its title.

Its afterlife has been enormous. “The Extended Mind” became one of the most-cited philosophy papers of recent decades, the founding text of what is now called 4E cognition (embodied, embedded, enacted, extended), and a standing provocation that every philosopher of mind has had to locate themselves relative to—for or against. It also turned out to be, quite by accident, an anticipatory theory of life with smartphones and language models: a rigorous framework for asking when a device stops being something you use and becomes part of how you think.

The core argument

When an external resource is coupled to a person in the right way—reliably present, fluently accessed, automatically trusted—it plays the same functional role that neurons playing it would make us call cognition. So the fair conclusion is that cognitive processes, and even mental states like belief, sometimes extend beyond the skin: the mind is whatever does the mind’s work, wherever it sits.

The engine of the argument is a demand for consistency. Cognitive science is officially functionalist: what makes something a memory or a belief is the role it plays—how it is formed, accessed, and used to guide behavior—not the material it is made of. Clark and Chalmers simply hold the field to its word. If we credit a process as cognitive when it runs on neurons, and an identically functioning process runs partly on paper or silicon, then refusing to credit the second is not science but prejudice—what the authors’ sympathizers would later call “neural chauvinism.” The parity principle is the formal statement of this challenge, and the paper’s thought experiments are engineered to make the double standard maximally visible.

What keeps the thesis from dissolving into absurdity—your mind is not the Library of Congress—is the coupling condition. The external resource must be bound into the agent’s cognitive routines the way biological memory is: carried everywhere, consulted without deliberation, trusted without re-verification. The claim is therefore not that tools are minds, but that a particular intimacy of interaction creates a single computational system spanning brain and world, and that drawing the scientific boundary at the skull cuts that system in half for no principled reason.

Notice the difference in scale from Hutchins. Where Cognition in the Wild redraws the unit of analysis around a whole team for explanatory purposes, Clark and Chalmers make a sharper and stranger claim about a single person: that Otto, walking to the museum, believes the museum is on 53rd Street in exactly the sense Inga does—before either of them consults anything. The radicalism is metaphysical, not merely methodological, and that is precisely what made the paper so productive to attack.

“The Extended Mind” is a fourteen-page paper published in Analysis 58:10–23 in 1998, and one of the most cited philosophy papers of its decade. In five short sections, Clark and Chalmers argue that cognitive processes — and, more radically, mental states like belief — are not confined to the brain: an external resource coupled to a person in the right way literally constitutes part of their mind. This condensation follows the paper’s five sections in order at near-walkthrough fidelity, keeping the Tetris triad, Otto’s notebook, Twin Otto, the exact parity principle, and every objection-and-reply pair. One charming front-matter detail: the authors are listed “in order of degree of belief in the central thesis” — Clark first.

Section 1 · Introduction

Where does the mind stop and the rest of the world begin?

The paper opens with that question in its first sentence, and notes that it invites two standard replies. Some accept the demarcations of skin and skull: whatever is outside the body is outside the mind — the default of common sense and most cognitive science. Others are impressed by Putnam- and Burge-style arguments that the meaning of our words “just ain’t in the head,” and hold that this externalism about meaning carries over into an externalism about mind.

Clark and Chalmers pursue a third position: in their words, “a very different sort of externalism: an active externalism, based on the active role of the environment in driving cognitive processes.” Where the older externalism made minds depend on distant, historical facts (which lakes you grew up near, what your community means by “arthritis”), active externalism is about external things doing cognitive work right now, in the here-and-now loop of thought and action. The whole paper unpacks that one move.

Section 2 · Extended Cognition

Three Tetris cases and the parity principle

The argument begins not with metaphysics but with three cases of human problem-solving. (1) A person at a computer screen must judge whether two-dimensional geometric shapes fit into depicted “sockets,” mentally rotating them to assess fit. (2) A person at a similar screen can rotate mentally or press a button that physically rotates the image on screen — and, not unrealistically, the physical rotation is faster. (3) Sometime in “the cyberpunk future,” a person has a neural implant that rotates as fast as the computer in case (2), and must still choose between implant and old-fashioned mental rotation, each making different demands on attention.

How much cognition is present in each? All three, Clark and Chalmers suggest, are on a par. Case (3), the implant, is clearly cognition — it is in the head. But case (2) displays exactly the same computational structure as (3), merely distributed across agent and computer rather than internalized. If the rotation in (3) is cognitive, by what right do we count (2) as fundamentally different? We cannot simply point to the skin/skull boundary — its legitimacy is precisely what is at issue — and nothing else seems different.

These cases are not exotic. Humans lean on environmental supports constantly: pen and paper for long multiplication, rearranging letter tiles to prompt word recall in Scrabble (Kirsh 1995), the nautical slide rule (Hutchins 1995), and the general paraphernalia of language, books, diagrams, and culture. In each, the brain performs some operations while others are delegated to manipulations of external media; had our brains been different, the division of labor would have differed too.

Even the rotation cases are real: they describe options available to Tetris players. David Kirsh and Paul Maglio (1994) measured the trade-off: physically rotating a falling shape through 90 degrees takes about 100 milliseconds, plus about 200 to select the button; the same result by mental rotation takes about 1,000. More telling, players use physical rotation not just to position a piece but often to help determine whether shape and slot are compatible — using the world to think with. Kirsh and Maglio call this an epistemic action.

Epistemic action (Kirsh & Maglio 1994)

An action that alters the world in order to aid and augment cognitive processes such as recognition and search. Contrast a merely pragmatic action, which alters the world because the physical change is desirable for its own sake — Clark and Chalmers’s example is putting cement into a hole in a dam.

Epistemic action, the authors say, “demands spread of epistemic credit.” And here comes what the literature now calls the parity principle, stated in the paper exactly as follows:

“If, as we confront some task, a part of the world functions as a process which, were it done in the head, we would have no hesitation in recognizing as part of the cognitive process, then that part of the world is (so we claim) part of the cognitive process. Cognitive processes ain’t (all) in the head!”— Clark & Chalmers, “The Extended Mind,” §2

Notice the test’s structure: it does not say everything causally helpful is cognition; it says the skin may not do classificatory work by itself. Judge a process by its functional role, imagining it relocated inward — if it would count as cognitive there, location alone cannot disqualify it out here.

Section 3 · Active Externalism

Coupled systems, Twin Earth, and the portability objection

What makes these cases cognitive rather than mere tool use? In each, the human organism is linked with an external entity in a two-way interaction, creating a coupled system that is a cognitive system in its own right. All components play an active causal role and jointly govern behavior the way cognition usually does; remove the external component and behavioral competence drops, just as if you removed part of the brain. The thesis: such coupled processes count equally well as cognitive, whether or not wholly in the head.

Now the promised contrast with Putnam (1975) and Burge (1979). On Twin Earth, I believe water is wet and my twin believes twin-water (XYZ) is wet; the external features responsible for the difference are distal and historical, at the far end of a lengthy causal chain. If I teleported to Twin Earth right now, my beliefs would still concern ordinary water, because of my history. Those features are passive — they play no role in driving cognition in the here-and-now, which is why the twins behave in physically indistinguishable ways. In Clark and Chalmers’s cases the external features are active: coupled to the organism, in the loop, with direct impact on behavior. Critics have long complained that Putnamian content is explanatorily idle — vary the distal environment with internal structure fixed and behavior never changes. Active externalism is immune: change the external features of a coupled system and behavior may change completely. (A footnote sharpens the diagnosis: debate has fixated on whether mind must be in the head, but the better question is “is mind in the present?”)

Active externalism also buys simpler explanations. You could explain my Scrabble play purely via inner processes plus a long series of “inputs” and “actions,” but if an isomorphic process were running in the head we would feel no urge to describe it so cumbersomely. “In a very real sense, the re-arrangement of tiles on the tray is not part of action; it is part of thought.” And the view aligns with a growing research program — situated cognition (Suchman 1987), real-world robotics (Beer 1989), dynamical approaches to child development (Thelen and Smith 1994), Hutchins’s (1995) cognitive collectives. Seeing cognition as extended is methodological, not terminological: explanatory methods once reserved for “inner” processes get adapted for the outer.

Two objections are handled here. First, consciousness: many identify the cognitive with the conscious, and consciousness plainly does not extend outside the head. Reply: not every cognitive process is conscious on standard usage — memory retrieval, linguistic processing, skill acquisition all run beyond consciousness’s borders — so externality is no ground for denying cognitive status. Second, the more interesting portability objection: real cognition must be portable — the vision of the Naked Mind, a constant package of resources brought to bear regardless of local environment. The trouble with coupled systems is that they are “too easily decoupled”; the true cognitive processes are the constant core, everything else an add-on extra.

There is something to this, they concede: the brain-and-body package is of interest in its own right, incorporating bodily tricks like counting on our fingers without encompassing contingent props like a pocket calculator. But mere contingency of coupling does not rule out cognitive status — a future plug-in memory module would be cognitive the moment it was plugged in. And once finger-counting is let in the door, it is easy to push further: think of the engineer with a slide rule hanging from his belt wherever he goes. The real moral of the portability intuition is that reliable coupling is what matters — it mostly happens within the brain, but not necessarily: “If the resources of my calculator or my Filofax are always there when I need them, then they are coupled with me as reliably as we need.” Nor does the danger of loss, malfunction, or occasional decoupling impugn such systems — the brain faces the same dangers, losing capacities in sleep, intoxication, and emotion. “If the relevant capacities are generally there when they are required, this is coupling enough.”

The section closes by arguing that extended cognition is not even optional for creatures like us: evolution and development have factored in a manipulable environment — vision, for instance, exploits contingent regularities of natural scenes and the shortcuts of bodily motion. The closing analogy is the fish, whose extraordinary swimming efficiency owes partly to an evolved capacity to couple its strokes to external pools of kinetic energy — swirls, eddies, and vortices, some natural and some self-induced by well-timed tail flaps (Triantafyllou & Triantafyllou 1995): fish plus vortices constitute a unified, remarkably efficient swimming machine. Likewise the human infant is enveloped from birth by a “sea of words,” and the plastic brain factors that surround into its routines — where the fish flaps its tail to create the eddies it exploits, we intervene in linguistic media, creating structures whose reliable presence drives our internal processes. “Words and external symbols are thus paramount among the cognitive vortices which help constitute human thought.”

Active externalism

The thesis that external features which are coupled to an organism in a two-way interaction — in the loop, driving cognition in the here-and-now — count as part of the cognitive process itself. Opposed both to skin-and-skull internalism and to the passive externalism of Putnam and Burge, on which the mind-fixing environment is distal and historical.

Section 4 · From Cognition to Mind

Otto, Inga, and the museum on 53rd Street

So far the paper has argued about “cognitive processing.” But some will think the conclusion was bought too cheaply: perhaps some processing happens in the environment, but what of mind? Everything said so far is compatible with all truly mental states — experiences, beliefs, desires, emotions — being determined by brain states. Clark and Chalmers take a step further: while some mental states such as experiences may be determined internally, beliefs can be constituted partly by features of the environment, when those features play the right role in driving cognition. If so, the mind extends into the world.

Enter the most famous pair in recent philosophy of mind. Inga hears from a friend that there is an exhibition at the Museum of Modern Art and decides to go. She thinks for a moment, recalls that the museum is on 53rd Street, walks there, and goes in. Clearly Inga believed the museum was on 53rd Street even before consulting her memory — not an occurrent belief, but then neither are most of our beliefs; it sat in memory, waiting to be accessed. Otto has Alzheimer’s disease and, like many patients, relies on the environment to structure his life. He carries a notebook everywhere; when he learns new information he writes it down, and when he needs old information he looks it up. The notebook plays the role usually played by biological memory. Otto hears about the same exhibition, consults the notebook — the museum is on 53rd Street — walks there, and goes in.

Clearly Otto walked to 53rd Street because he wanted to see the exhibition and believed the museum was on 53rd Street. And just as with Inga, it seems reasonable to say he believed this before consulting the notebook: the cases are entirely analogous in all relevant respects — the notebook plays for Otto exactly the role memory plays for Inga; the information just happens to lie beyond the skin. The alternative — Otto has no belief until he looks, believing at best that the museum is at the address written in the notebook — looks unnatural once you follow Otto around: he uses the notebook constantly, as a matter of course, central to his actions the way ordinary memory is central to an ordinary life. Saying his beliefs disappear when the notebook is filed away misses the big picture exactly as saying Inga’s beliefs disappear whenever she stops being conscious of them. In both cases the information is reliably there when needed, available to consciousness and to guide action — just what we expect of a belief.

The argument is then put in terms of explanatory role. We happily explain Inga’s action by her occurrent desire plus her standing belief that the museum is on 53rd Street; we should explain Otto’s the same way. The alternative — occurrent desire, plus a standing belief that the location is written in the notebook, plus the accessible fact that the notebook says 53rd Street — complicates the explanation unnecessarily, and the extra notebook-term would recur in countless other explanations of Otto’s behavior. “We submit that to explain things this way is to take one step too many” — as pointlessly complex as explaining Inga’s actions via beliefs about her memory. The notebook is a constant for Otto as memory is for Inga; “in an explanation, simplicity is power.” There is even a Twin Otto, identical from the skin in, who long ago miswrote that MoMA is on 51st Street: Otto walks to 53rd, Twin Otto walks to 51st. A belief, in such cases, is simply not in the head — and unlike the Putnam/Burge twins, these twins behave differently: the cases differ not just in reference and truth-conditions but in the very dynamics of cognition. The moral: “when it comes to belief, there is nothing sacred about skull and skin. What makes some information count as a belief is the role it plays.”

Then the objections. An opponent might dig in on the word “belief” and insist that, as she uses the term, Otto simply does not believe. The authors decline to debate usage; their point is normative — the notion of belief ought to be used so that Otto qualifies, because used that wide it picks out something more like a natural kind: deeper, more unified, more useful in explanation. Substantial resistance requires a deep, relevant difference between Otto and Inga — and merely pointing out that one store of information is in the head begs the question. Candidate differences: (i) Reliability — someone might steal the notebook. Reply: constancy is indeed relevant (a one-off guidebook consultation would not ground a standing belief), but Otto’s access is very reliable, and Inga’s is not perfect either — a surgeon might tamper with her brain, or she might simply have too much to drink. (ii) Intermittent access — Otto showers without the notebook and cannot read it in the dark. Reply: when Inga is asleep or intoxicated her beliefs do not disappear; what counts is that the information is easily available when needed, a constraint both satisfy. (iii) Bandwidth — Inga’s link to her memory is high-grade, Otto’s to his notebook low-grade. Reply: Inga’s friend Lucy, whose biological memory has only a low-grade link to her central systems, processes less efficiently but clearly still believes the museum is on 53rd Street. (iv) Perception versus introspection — Otto reaches the information only by perceiving the notebook. Reply: this begs the question; on the view being advocated, Otto-plus-notebook is a single cognitive system, and from that system’s standpoint the flow of information between notebook and brain is not perceptual at all — nothing impacts the system from outside; it is more akin to information flow within the brain. The residue is a perceptual phenomenology of retrieval — but why should phenomenology settle a belief’s status? The Terminator recalls stored information by having it displayed in his visual field; that makes no difference to its status as standing belief.

All these differences, the authors conclude, are shallow; fixating on them misses how completely the notebook entries play the role beliefs play in guiding most people’s lives. The last refuge is to hold that only occurrent beliefs are true beliefs — but that rules out Inga’s belief too. On any less extreme view — say, that a belief must be available for consciousness — Otto’s notebook entry qualifies as well as Inga’s memory. Once dispositional beliefs are let in the door, it is hard to resist the conclusion that the notebook has all the relevant dispositions.

Section 5 · Beyond the Outer Limits

Puzzle cases, other people, and the extended self

If the thesis is accepted, how far does it go? Puzzle cases multiply: the amnesic villagers in One Hundred Years of Solitude, who forget the names of everything and hang labels everywhere; the Filofax; a tampered notebook; the unread page in front of me; the Internet — is my cognitive state somehow spread across it? Clark and Chalmers decline categorical answers, and instead extract the features that make “belief” so clearly applicable in the central case — the conditions the later literature calls glue and trust.

The “glue and trust” conditions

What makes Otto’s notebook belief-grade: (1) it is a constant in his life — he rarely acts on relevant matters without consulting it; (2) its information is directly available without difficulty; (3) upon retrieval he automatically endorses it; (4) the information was consciously endorsed in the past and is there as a consequence. The fourth is flagged as arguable; the first three “certainly play a crucial role.”

As exotic cases shed these features, the applicability of “belief” gradually falls off. If I rarely act without consulting my Filofax, it resembles Otto’s notebook; if I often answer relevant questions with “I don’t know,” its contents count less clearly as my beliefs. The Internet “is likely to fail on multiple counts, unless I am unusually computer-reliant, facile with the technology, and trusting” — though certain files on my own computer may qualify. Intermediate cases may be indeterminate or context-dependent; none of that clouds the central ones.

What about socially extended cognition — mental states partly constituted by the states of other thinkers? “We see no reason why not, in principle.” In an unusually interdependent couple, one partner’s beliefs may play for the other just the role the notebook plays for Otto; what is central is a high degree of trust, reliance, and accessibility. A delightful footnote cites the New York Times of March 30, 1995, on UCLA coach John Wooden: across 36 straight Final Fours, Nell Wooden — who rarely forgot a name, where her husband rarely remembered one — “invariably served as his memory bank,” recognizing people for him in the lobbies. The waiter at my favorite restaurant might be a repository of my beliefs about my favorite meals (perhaps even extended desire); one’s beliefs might be embodied in one’s secretary, accountant, or collaborator. In each case the burden of coupling is carried by language: without it we might be much more akin to discrete Cartesian inner minds; with it, we spread cognition into the world. Language is not a mirror of inner states but a complement to them, and the intellectual explosion in recent evolutionary time may owe as much to this linguistic extension as to any independent inner development.

Finally, the self. Does the extended mind imply an extended self? “It seems so.” Most of us already accept that the self outstrips the boundaries of consciousness — my dispositional beliefs are part of who I am — so those boundaries may fall beyond the skin too. The information in Otto’s notebook is a central part of his identity as a cognitive agent: Otto himself is best regarded as an extended system, a coupling of biological organism and external resources; resisting consistently would shrink the self to a bundle of occurrent states, threatening its deep psychological continuity. The reconception has moral consequences: interfering with someone’s environment may carry the same moral significance as interfering with their person, and some social activity might be reconceived as less akin to communication and action, more akin to thought. The paper ends on its most quoted line:

“In any case, once the hegemony of skin and skull is usurped, we may be able to see ourselves more truly as creatures of the world.”— Clark & Chalmers, “The Extended Mind,” closing sentence

Reading it critically: the paper’s afterlife

Famously rejected by three journals before Analysis took it, the paper became the founding document of one wing of 4E cognition — cognition as embodied, embedded, enacted, and extended — and the centerpiece of Richard Menary’s 2010 anthology The Extended Mind. The sharpest attack came from Fred Adams and Ken Aizawa (“The Bounds of Cognition,” 2001; book, 2008), who charge the argument with the coupling-constitution fallacy: that the notebook is reliably coupled to Otto’s cognition does not show it constitutes part of it — pencils are coupled to mathematicians without thinking — and they propose a “mark of the cognitive” (roughly, intrinsic, non-derived content) that notebooks fail. Robert Rupert (2004) pressed a rival framing: the cases are captured by the tamer hypothesis of embedded cognition (HEMC) — cognition leans heavily on environmental scaffolding — without the radical hypothesis of extended cognition (HEC); and since inner memory and notebook storage have very different fine-grained profiles (no interference effects, no generation effect), lumping them into one kind is bad science. Defenders reply that the parity principle was always an intuition pump against prejudice, not a similarity claim. Clark answered his critics at book length in Supersizing the Mind (2008, foreword by Chalmers), after Being There (1997) and Natural-Born Cyborgs (2003); Surfing Uncertainty (2016) recasts the brain itself as one node in rolling brain-body-world loops. Meanwhile smartphones made Otto’s notebook feel less like science fiction than like a Tuesday — a 1998 thought experiment about a Filofax now reads as the first philosophy of wearable, searchable, AI-augmented memory.

If you remember ten things

  1. The paper’s question is its first sentence — “Where does the mind stop and the rest of the world begin?” — and its answer is that skin and skull mark no principled boundary.
  2. The three Tetris cases (mental rotation, rotate button, neural implant) show one computational structure differently located; if the implant is cognition, the button has equal claim.
  3. Kirsh and Maglio’s numbers carry the empirical weight: ~100 ms physical rotation plus ~200 ms button-selection versus ~1,000 ms mental rotation — and players rotate in order to think, an epistemic rather than pragmatic action.
  4. The parity principle: if part of the world functions as a process that, were it done in the head, we would unhesitatingly call cognitive, then it is part of the cognitive process.
  5. Active externalism differs from Putnam/Burge passive externalism because the external features are in the loop here-and-now: Twin Earth twins behave identically, but Otto walks to 53rd while Twin Otto walks to 51st.
  6. The portability objection is answered with reliable coupling: occasional decoupling no more disqualifies a notebook than sleep or drunkenness disqualifies a brain — “this is coupling enough.”
  7. Otto’s notebook entries are standing beliefs because they play the belief role: denying this is “one step too many,” and in an explanation, simplicity is power.
  8. Every proposed Otto/Inga difference — reliability, intermittent access, bandwidth (Lucy), perceptual access (the Terminator) — is shallow; once dispositional beliefs are admitted, the notebook has all the relevant dispositions.
  9. The glue-and-trust conditions tame the puzzle cases: constant use, direct availability, automatic endorsement, and (arguably) past conscious endorsement — so the 1998 Internet mostly fails while a spouse like Nell Wooden may succeed.
  10. The conclusion extends from mind to self and morals: Otto is a coupled system of organism and resources, interfering with an environment can be interfering with a person, and once the hegemony of skin and skull is usurped we are “creatures of the world.”

Concepts to keep

Active externalism

The environment can play an active, real-time role in driving cognitive processes—unlike the passive externalism of Putnam and Burge, where external facts fix meaning but make no difference to how thinking unfolds in the moment. The world is in the loop, not just in the background.

The parity principle

If part of the world functions as a process which, were it done in the head, we would unhesitatingly call cognitive, then that part of the world is part of the cognitive process. A consistency test: judge processes by function, not by location.

Epistemic vs. pragmatic action

Kirsh and Maglio’s distinction: pragmatic actions change the world because you want it changed; epistemic actions change the world to make your computation cheaper—rotating the Tetris piece on screen, scribbling intermediate sums. Expert performance is saturated with the epistemic kind.

Coupled systems

When brain and external resource interact in a two-way loop such that removing the resource degrades competence like a lesion would, the ensemble constitutes a single cognitive system. Coupling, not containment, is what individuates the thinker.

The glue-and-trust criteria

What promoted Otto’s notebook from tool to memory: constant availability, direct and easy access, automatic endorsement of retrieved content, and past conscious endorsement. The operational checklist for asking whether any given technology has become part of someone’s mind.

The cognitive bloat objection

If notebooks count, why not the phone book, Google, or the whole internet—does Otto believe everything searchable? The standard pressure test on the thesis; the glue-and-trust criteria are the standard answer, and how well they hold is still contested.

Socially extended cognition

The closing speculation that other people can serve as components of one’s cognitive system—a spouse as one’s memory for names, a team as one’s working memory. The hinge that connects this paper to Hutchins’s shipboard ethnography.

Threads to other books

Cognition in the Wild — Edwin Hutchins
The empirical companion: Hutchins documented brain–artifact–team systems doing real cognition aboard a Navy ship three years before Clark and Chalmers argued that such systems are not just useful descriptions but literally minds.
Machines of Loving Grace — Dario Amodei
Amodei’s hopeful future turns on whether AI extends human cognition or substitutes for it—and the glue-and-trust criteria are the sharpest available test for telling, person by person and task by task, which of the two is actually happening.
Nexus — Yuval Noah Harari
If minds extend into shared external media, then Harari’s information networks—stories, bureaucracies, databases—are not merely channels between minds but constituents of them, which raises the stakes of who controls the media considerably.
Influence — Robert B. Cialdini
Automatic endorsement is the load-bearing criterion of the extended mind—and Cialdini’s catalog of click-run compliance shows precisely how dangerous unexamined automatic endorsement becomes when someone engineers what gets retrieved.
The Book of Why — Judea Pearl
A causal diagram on paper is rung-three reasoning offloaded into the world—an epistemic artifact in exactly the Clark–Chalmers sense, doing inferential work that the unaided head demonstrably cannot.
For the AI researcher

Otto’s notebook stored only what Otto had once consciously endorsed; it never wrote itself. A language model is something stranger—an external resource that generates content—and that makes it both the most literal extended mind ever built and a case the 1998 criteria do not cleanly cover. Run the checklist honestly: constant availability, yes; direct accessibility, yes; automatic endorsement—increasingly yes, and that is exactly the unsettling part, because the past-endorsement condition that anchored Otto’s notebook to Otto’s own epistemic history has no analogue. A user who automatically endorses model outputs satisfies the coupling profile of extended belief while skipping the step that made the contents theirs. Whether that constitutes cognitive extension, cognitive outsourcing, or something needing a new name is a live and genuinely important question—and the paper gives you the cleanest vocabulary for asking it, including for empirical work on what reliance on models does to the biological remainder of the system.

The framework also reads directly onto agents. An LLM with tool use is a coupled system in precisely the paper’s sense: scratchpads and chains of thought are epistemic actions (restructuring the external medium to simplify the next computation step); retrieval stores, code interpreters, and memory files are candidate cognitive parts of the agent rather than mere peripherals. This suggests evaluating and interpreting at the level of the coupled system—model plus tools plus memory—since, as the Tetris case teaches, the competence under study may not reside in the weights at all. Adams and Aizawa’s coupling-constitution objection is worth keeping in your pocket here as a discipline: causal reliance on a tool is not yet constitution, and the difference matters for where you draw the boundary of the thing you are aligning.

Finally, the paper quietly reframes what is at stake in deployment. If minds extend into reliably coupled artifacts, then changing the artifact is changing the mind—deprecating a model someone has woven into their daily cognition is closer to a lesion than to a discontinued product, and an interface that shapes what gets retrieved shapes what its users believe in the paper’s full-blooded sense. Clark and Chalmers wrote nine pages about a man and his notebook; you are building the notebooks now, at population scale, and they talk back.
