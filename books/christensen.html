Firms & Disruption

The Innovator’s Dilemma

Clayton Christensen · 1997
~8 hours to read Condensed read below: ~40 min read Why good management kills great firms Pairs with: Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital

This is the rare business book with an actual theory in it — a falsifiable causal claim about why excellent, well-run companies get destroyed by inferior products. The word it gave the world, “disruption,” has been worn smooth by misuse; the argument underneath remains sharp, strange, and worth recovering from the buzzword.

Clayton Christensen came to the question late and sideways: a Rhodes scholar and former BCG consultant who co-founded a materials-science company before arriving at Harvard Business School in his late thirties for a doctorate. His dissertation subject was the hard disk drive industry — chosen, he liked to say, for the same reason geneticists study fruit flies: with product generations turning over every few years, you could watch entire cycles of industrial birth and death inside a single career. The 1997 book generalized what the disk drives revealed.

The puzzle that provoked it was an affront to management orthodoxy. The leading firms in industry after industry were not complacent, badly run, or deaf to customers — they were paragons, the companies the case studies celebrated. Yet they failed with eerie regularity, and they failed precisely when a technically simpler product appeared at the bottom of their market. Christensen’s heresy was to argue that good management itself was the cause: the same disciplined practices of listening to your best customers and investing where margins are highest systematically steer great firms away from the innovations that eventually kill them.

The book’s influence is hard to overstate. Andy Grove publicly credited it; Steve Jobs absorbed it; The Economist ranked it among the most important business books ever written, and Christensen — who died in 2020 — spent the rest of his career extending and defending the theory against both dilution and critique (Jill Lepore’s 2014 broadside in The New Yorker being the most famous). Read it as the original, before the photocopies.

The core argument

Technologies come in two kinds. Sustaining technologies improve products along the dimensions existing customers already value, and incumbents almost always win those battles. Disruptive technologies are initially worse on those dimensions but cheaper, simpler, or more convenient, creating footholds in markets the incumbents’ best customers don’t care about. Because rational, well-managed firms allocate resources toward their most profitable customers, they cede those footholds — and when the disruptive technology improves enough to invade the mainstream, it is too late.

The mechanism is resource allocation, not stupidity. A firm is embedded in a value network — a web of customers, suppliers, and margin structures that defines what “performance” and “profit” mean. Inside that network, every signal a good manager attends to says the same thing: the disruptive product has worse specs, your customers explicitly reject it, the addressable market looks tiny, and the margins are thin. Killing the project is the correct decision by every metric the firm possesses. Christensen’s point is that the metrics themselves are the trap; firms are held captive by their customers as surely as by their competitors.

The trap springs because of two trajectories. Technology improves faster than customers’ ability to absorb improvement, so products that begin inadequate for the mainstream eventually overshoot it — at which point competition shifts from raw performance to reliability, convenience, and price, the disruptive entrant’s home turf. Meanwhile the entrants, having grown up in low-margin niches, march upmarket where the money is, and incumbents rationally retreat upward too, because fleeing to higher margins always looks smarter than defending lower ones. The retreat works beautifully until there is nowhere left to go.

The prescriptive half of the book follows from the diagnosis: since the failure is organizational physics rather than personal error, the remedy is structural. Incumbents can survive disruption only by creating separate organizations — small enough to be excited by small markets, with cost structures that find the new margins attractive, and with the freedom to discover their customers rather than inherit them.

Christensen’s question is deliberately uncomfortable: why do well-run companies—firms with attentive managers, satisfied customers, disciplined investment processes—get destroyed by technologically simpler products from smaller firms? His answer, built from a near-complete census of the hard-disk-drive industry and tested against excavators and steel, is that failure follows directly from good management. The book has two parts. Part One (chapters 1–4) builds the theory of why great companies can fail; Part Two (chapters 5–11) derives five principles of disruptive innovation and shows how a manager can work with them rather than against them, ending with a thought experiment on electric vehicles. This condensation follows the book’s own chapter order, merging the closing summary chapter into the electric-vehicle section; the statistical appendices and some repeated drive-industry detail are omitted.

Part One · Chapter 1

How can great firms fail? Insights from the hard-disk-drive industry

Christensen opens with the puzzle in its sharpest form. Sears was once the paragon of retailing and missed discount stores and home centers. IBM dominated mainframes and missed minicomputers; Digital Equipment dominated minicomputers and missed the desktop PC—and DEC failed while business pundits were still naming it one of the best-managed companies in the world. The conventional explanations—arrogance, bureaucracy, tired executives, short-termism—don’t survive scrutiny, because the same firms that fumbled these transitions were simultaneously executing brilliantly on hard technical problems. Something more systematic is going on.

Precisely because these firms listened to their customers, invested aggressively in new technologies that would provide their customers more and better products of the sort they wanted, and because they carefully studied market trends and systematically allocated investment capital to innovations that promised the best returns, they lost their positions of leadership.

— Clayton Christensen, The Innovator’s Dilemma, Introduction

To study failure rigorously you need an industry where companies are born and die fast enough to observe many complete life cycles—what geneticists get from fruit flies. Christensen’s fruit flies are hard disk drives. The industry began in 1956, when an IBM team in San Jose built the RAMAC: a machine the size of a large refrigerator that stored five megabytes on fifty 24-inch platters. Over the following decades the industry improved at a pace nothing else in industrial history matched: the density of information packed onto a square inch of disk rose roughly 35 percent per year, and the cost per megabyte fell on the order of five percent per quarter for more than twenty years. Yet the firms making the drives kept dying. Of the seventeen companies in the industry in 1976—all but IBM’s captive operation making 14-inch drives for mainframes—essentially every independent one had failed or been acquired by the mid-1990s. Christensen assembled data on every model introduced by every firm in the industry’s history and asked: which innovations killed the incumbents?

The answer is the book’s central empirical surprise. It was not the technologically difficult innovations. The industry saw a long series of radical component advances—thin-film heads replacing ferrite heads, thin-film disks replacing oxide-coated disks, later magnetoresistive heads—some of which cost incumbents like IBM upward of a hundred million dollars and a decade of work. The established firms led nearly all of them. Across the dozens of new technologies in the industry’s history, almost all were sustaining—they improved the performance that existing customers already valued—and incumbents led them regardless of how technologically heroic they were.

What killed incumbents was a handful of architecturally simple innovations: new drive form factors, each smaller than the last. The 14-inch Winchester drives sold to mainframe makers were displaced by 8-inch drives (pioneered around 1978–80 by entrants such as Shugart Associates, Micropolis, Priam, and Quantum), which initially held only 10 to 40 megabytes when mainframe customers needed 300 or more—but which fit inside the new minicomputers from DEC, Data General, Wang, and Hewlett-Packard. The 8-inch makers were displaced in turn by the 5.25-inch drive, introduced in 1980 by Seagate Technology—founded by Alan Shugart and Finis Conner—whose first ST-506 held a meager 5 megabytes but suited the desktop PC. Then Seagate itself stumbled on the 3.5-inch drive: a small Scottish firm, Rodime, introduced it in 1984, and Conner Peripherals—founded by Finis Conner in 1986 with backing from Compaq—rode it into portable computers, booking on the order of $113 million in its first year, then the fastest first-year ramp in U.S. manufacturing history. The pattern repeated with the 2.5-inch drive (pioneered by PrairieTek in 1989) and the 1.8-inch drive after that. In each generation, the new drive was worse on the attribute the mainstream cared about—capacity—and better on attributes the mainstream didn’t: physical size, weight, power draw, and price per unit.

The crucial detail is that incumbents were rarely beaten on R&D. Engineers at Seagate had working 3.5-inch prototypes by around 1985. Marketing showed them to the firm’s flagship customers—IBM’s PC division and other desktop makers—who shrugged: they wanted more megabytes in the 5.25-inch slot they already had, at lower cost per megabyte. Senior managers, looking at small forecast volumes and thin margins, shelved the program in favor of higher-capacity 5.25-inch models. Seagate eventually shipped 3.5-inch drives in 1988—but defensively, to its existing desktop customers, not to the laptop makers where the new market grew. Christensen’s diagnosis: the prototypes were not killed by incompetence. They were killed by a rational resource-allocation process responding faithfully to the best customers the company had.

This is one of the innovator’s dilemmas: Blindly following the maxim that good managers should keep close to their customers can sometimes be a fatal mistake.

— Clayton Christensen, The Innovator’s Dilemma, chapter 1

Sustaining vs. disruptive technology

A sustaining technology improves the performance of established products along the dimensions that mainstream customers in major markets have historically valued—whether the improvement is incremental or radical. A disruptive technology is, at first, worse on those dimensions: it brings a different value proposition—typically simpler, smaller, cheaper, more convenient—valued by new or marginal customers, and then improves until it satisfies the mainstream too.

The mechanism becomes visible in Christensen’s signature chart, which he asks the reader to hold in mind for the rest of the book. Picture time on the horizontal axis and performance—here, drive capacity—on the vertical. Draw a set of gently rising dashed lines: the capacity demanded by each class of computer user. Mainframe users’ demand for capacity rose roughly 15 percent a year; desktop users’ somewhat faster. Now draw the solid lines: the capacity supplied by each drive architecture, rising far more steeply—the 14-inch line improving on the order of 22 percent a year, the 5.25-inch line at something like 50 percent a year in its early life. Because the technology lines climb more steeply than the demand lines, two things happen. First, each architecture eventually overshoots its original market. Second—and this is the engine of disruption—a new architecture that starts below a market’s demand line, serving some humbler market, will reliably climb up through it. The 5.25-inch drive that could only serve desktops in 1981 could serve minicomputers by the mid-1980s. The entrant doesn’t have to out-innovate the incumbent; it just has to ride the steeper-than-demand slope of its own improvement until the mainstream falls within reach. The disruptors then carry their lower cost structures into the incumbent’s market and win.

Part One · Chapter 2

Value networks and the impetus to innovate

Chapter 2 asks why incumbents could lead hundred-million-dollar component revolutions yet fumble architecturally trivial form-factor changes. Christensen first tests the standard academic explanations—that firms fail at “radical” change, or at “architectural” change that rearranges how components fit together (Rebecca Henderson and Kim Clark’s influential account). Neither fits the drive data: incumbents managed radical component change and several architectural changes fine. The variable that predicts failure isn’t the nature of the technology at all. It’s the nature of the market the technology initially serves.

Value network

The context within which a firm identifies and responds to customers’ needs, solves problems, procures inputs, reacts to competitors, and strives for profit. Each value network has its own ranking of product attributes and—critically—its own characteristic cost structure and gross margins. Firms’ perceptions of what is worth doing are formed inside their network.

Borrowing from Giovanni Dosi’s idea of technological paradigms, Christensen describes each computer class as a nested system—drives inside cabinets inside machines inside the buyer’s business—and each nesting as a value network. In the mainframe network of the late 1970s, the attributes that mattered, in order, were capacity, speed, and reliability; drives there earned gross margins on the order of 50 to 60 percent, which is what it took to support the sales engineering and overhead of selling to mainframe makers. In the desktop network, the ranking was different—ruggedness, low price per unit, small size—and the margins were much thinner, roughly half. In the portable-computer network, thinner still, with weight and power consumption near the top of the list. A megabyte was not worth the same number of dollars in different networks.

This explains the asymmetry of motivation that drives the whole book. When a firm embedded in the mainframe network evaluated the 8-inch drive, it saw a product its customers couldn’t use, aimed at a market that was tiny (the entire minicomputer-drive market in its early years was smaller than a single year’s growth in 14-inch demand), with margins below its cost structure. Every rational signal—from customers, from the income statement, from the resource-allocation process—said wait. Entrants, with no installed customer base and cost structures built to be profitable at the new network’s margins, faced no such signal. Christensen traces the typical sequence: (1) the disruptive technology is developed inside an established firm; (2) marketing seeks reactions from lead customers, who reject it; (3) the firm accelerates sustaining development instead; (4) frustrated engineers leave and found new companies; (5) the entrants find new markets by trial and error; (6) the entrants improve and invade the established market from below; (7) the incumbents finally respond, too late, defending the high end as the floor collapses.

The chapter closes with the data on what leadership was worth. Leading in sustaining technologies conferred almost no survival advantage—followers caught up quickly, because the customers were waiting for them. Leading in disruptive form-factor generations was close to a matter of life and death: the firms that led disruptive generations dominated the new markets those generations created, while followers got almost nothing. The dilemma is now precise: the innovations that matter most are exactly the ones the firm’s own customers and financial logic reject.

Part One · Chapter 3

Disruptive change in the mechanical excavator industry

Could the disk-drive pattern be an artifact of a fast, frothy industry full of venture capital and job-hopping engineers? Chapter 3 deliberately picks the opposite kind of industry—slow-moving, capital-intensive, century-old—and finds the same anatomy. Mechanical earthmoving equipment descends from William Otis’s steam shovel of the 1830s. By the early twentieth century, more than thirty manufacturers—Bucyrus Erie, Marion, Thew, Insley, Koehring, Northwest, P&H, Link-Belt and others—built cable-actuated machines: the bucket was hoisted, swung, and dragged by steel cables wound on drums.

The industry first shows that incumbents handle even wrenching change when it is sustaining. The shift from steam power to gasoline engines in the 1920s was technologically radical—it changed the core of the machine—but it improved exactly what excavation contractors already valued, and the leading steam-era firms led the gasoline-era industry too. The same happened with the later moves to diesel and electric power, and with sustaining architecture changes like the arched boom that allowed longer reach.

Hydraulics was different. The first hydraulically actuated excavators appeared just after World War II—J. C. Bamford (JCB) in Britain and small American firms such as Sherman Products around 1947–48. The early machines were pitiful by incumbent standards: bucket capacities of roughly a quarter of a cubic yard, short reach, and seals that leaked under high pressure, at a time when mainstream sewer, general-excavation, and mining contractors were demanding buckets of one to four cubic yards. No general contractor could use one. So the entrants found customers who could: the machines were built as “backhoes,” attachments mounted on the back of industrial and farm tractors, sold through tractor dealerships—Ford, J. I. Case, John Deere, International Harvester, Massey Ferguson, JCB—to small residential contractors digging narrow trenches from water and sewer mains to house foundations. These customers had previously dug by hand. For them, a small, precise, cheap machine was a miracle; bucket capacity ranked far below maneuverability and price. A new value network had formed.

Then the trajectory did its work. Hydraulic actuation improved year by year as seal, pump, and cylinder technology matured, while the bucket sizes mainstream contractors needed grew only slowly. Through the 1950s and 1960s hydraulic machines climbed into general excavation and sewer work, and by roughly 1974 hydraulics had captured nearly everything except enormous mining applications. Of the some thirty established cable-shovel makers, only four—Insley, Koehring, Little Giant, and Link-Belt—successfully made the transition into sustainable hydraulic positions. The new leaders—JCB, Case, Deere, Caterpillar (which entered hydraulic excavators only in the early 1970s, as an entrant from the adjacent tractor business), Komatsu, Hitachi, Poclain—came from outside the cable industry.

The most instructive failure is Bucyrus Erie, the strongest of the incumbents—because it saw hydraulics and responded. In 1950–51 it acquired a small hydraulic-machine line and launched the “Hydrohoe,” a hybrid that used hydraulic cylinders for some motions but still relied on cable where hydraulics was weak. Crucially, Bucyrus did with the Hydrohoe what its processes knew how to do: it tried to sell it as a sustaining product, through its existing dealers, to its existing excavation contractors—who, predictably, found it inferior to cable machines on every attribute they valued. The product languished. Bucyrus and Marion ultimately retreated up-market into giant stripping shovels and draglines for mining, a profitable but shrinking refuge. The lesson Christensen draws is exact: the incumbents did not fail for lack of the technology—they cradled it into the wrong value network. Meanwhile their flight up-market felt like success, with healthy margins, right up until there was nowhere left to go.

Part One · Chapter 4

What goes up, can’t go down

Chapter 4 names the asymmetry that the first three chapters kept exposing: companies can migrate up-market toward higher-performance, higher-margin tiers with remarkable agility, and find it almost impossible to move down. Christensen calls the pattern the northeastern migration—on his trajectory chart, every firm’s product line drifts up and to the right. Three forces produce it. First, gross margins are simply better one tier up: a disk maker earning 25 percent margins in desktops sees 40 percent margins in minicomputer drives above it and 18 percent in portables below; every individual product decision that compares the two will favor up. Second, the firm’s best customers are themselves moving up-market and pull their suppliers along. Third, cost structures ratchet: once a firm builds the sales, engineering, and overhead apparatus to serve a high-end network, low-end business literally cannot cover its costs, so dropping down looks like value destruction in every spreadsheet. Rational managers therefore cede the lowest tier, again and again, to whoever is attacking it—and each retreat improves reported profitability, which is why boards applaud it.

He illustrates with the 1.8-inch drive, then the industry’s newest generation. A drive-company CEO told him, in effect: we developed the 1.8-inch drive, it’s sitting on the shelf, but there’s no market. Meanwhile the early demand for 1.8-inch drives was real but invisible to a computer-industry sales force, coming from outside computing altogether—applications like portable heart-monitoring devices. The drive existed; the company’s resource-allocation system, tuned to existing customers, could not see anyone to sell it to. “No market” meant “no market among the customers we already serve.”

The chapter’s great case is steel. Minimills melt scrap in electric arc furnaces, skipping the blast furnaces, coke ovens, and basic-oxygen converters of integrated mills; a competitive minimill can make steel at something like 15 percent lower cost, with vastly lower capital intensity. When minimills emerged in the mid-1960s, scrap-based steel had quality problems, so the only market open to them was the very bottom of the industry: concrete reinforcing bar—rebar—a commodity with loose specifications, sold almost purely on price, carrying the industry’s worst margins (on the order of 7 percent, versus roughly 25 to 30 percent for premium sheet at the top). The integrated mills—U.S. Steel, Bethlehem and the rest—were not just willing but relieved to exit rebar: shedding the lowest-margin tonnage raised their average profitability. Each side did the rational thing.

Then the minimills—Nucor under Ken Iverson, Chaparral, and others—marched. From rebar they moved up to larger bars, rods, and angle iron; the integrated mills ceded that tier too, and their margins improved again. Then structural beams; same response. By 1989 Nucor had committed the decisive blow: its plant at Crawfordsville, Indiana adopted thin-slab continuous casting (the CSP process from the German equipment maker SMS Schloemann-Siemag), which let a minimill pour flat-rolled sheet steel—the integrated mills’ last and richest stronghold—at a capital cost per ton on the order of a tenth of a conventional integrated sheet plant. The integrated mills had all evaluated thin-slab casting and passed, for an impeccable reason: its surface quality wasn’t good enough for their most demanding, most profitable customers—auto bodies, appliances, beverage cans—so it could serve only low-margin sheet, which wasn’t worth their capital. Nucor, attacking from below, had no such customers to disappoint. By the mid-1990s minimills held roughly 40 percent of North American steelmaking, and the integrated firms’ decades of profitable up-market retreat had run out of “up.” Christensen’s framing of the whole of Part One: in every one of these industries, well-managed retreat looked like victory until the moment it became extinction.

Part Two · Chapter 5

Give responsibility for disruptive technologies to organizations whose customers need them

Part Two turns from diagnosis to management. Christensen’s stance is distinctive: the forces that defeat incumbents are like gravity—managers who fight them lose, and managers who understand them can harness them, the way an engineer harnesses gravity rather than repealing it. He organizes Part Two around five principles of disruptive innovation, one per chapter, each stated first as the law that defeated the incumbents and then as a design rule.

The five principles of disruptive innovation

(1) Companies depend on customers and investors for resources—resource allocation follows them. (2) Small markets don’t solve the growth needs of large companies. (3) Markets that do not exist cannot be analyzed. (4) An organization’s capabilities define its disabilities. (5) Technology supply may not equal market demand—performance can overshoot what customers can absorb.

Principle one comes from resource-dependence theory (Pfeffer and Salancik): however much rhetoric flows top-down, the real direction of a company is set by who provides its resources—customers and investors. Proposals that serve them get staffed and funded; proposals that don’t get starved, no matter what the CEO announces. The well-run company is, in a meaningful sense, run by its customers. The managerial corollary is that you cannot make a mainstream organization prioritize a disruptive product by exhortation; you must place the project inside an organization whose own customers need the disruptive product, so the resource-dependence machinery works for it instead of against it.

The disk industry supplies a controlled comparison. Quantum, a leading 8-inch maker, had missed the 5.25-inch generation; when its engineers saw the 3.5-inch opportunity, Quantum in 1984 financed them as a separately housed spinout, Plus Development Corporation, which built the “Hardcard”—a 3.5-inch drive on an add-in card—for a brand-new customer set. Plus thrived while Quantum’s old 8-inch business withered; Quantum eventually bought out the venture, effectively refounded itself on Plus’s products and managers, and by 1994 was the largest unit-volume drive maker in the world. Control Data, the dominant 14-inch OEM supplier, had missed the 8-inch wave while its Minneapolis organization served mainframe customers; for the 5.25-inch generation it set up a dedicated unit in Oklahoma City, deliberately away from headquarters, and captured a healthy share of the high-capacity 5.25-inch market. The contrast case is Micropolis, whose CEO Stuart Mabon tried to manage the 8-inch-to-5.25-inch transition inside the mainstream organization. He succeeded—Christensen counts it among the only such successes in the industry’s history—but only by personally riveting the organization’s attention on the new drive while the old business starved, an effort Mabon remembered as utterly exhausting; the firm had to abandon essentially 100 percent of its old customers to survive. The rule: fighting resource dependence is possible and nearly lethal; aligning with it—via an organization whose customers pull the disruption forward—is reliable.

Part Two · Chapter 6

Match the size of the organization to the size of the market

Principle two: small markets cannot solve the growth problems of big companies. A $40 million company needs $8 million of new revenue to grow 20 percent next year; a $4 billion company needs $800 million. Disruptive markets in their formative years are small by construction—that is why incumbents ignore them—so the bigger and more successful a firm becomes, the harder it is for any emerging market to clear its materiality bar. Waiting until the market is “big enough to be interesting” is not a safe option either: Christensen’s drive data show that leadership matters enormously in disruption even though it barely matters in sustaining technology. Firms that led in sustaining advances gained no lasting edge—customers were waiting for the followers. But the firms that led disruptive generations accumulated, by his count over 1976–1994, a cumulative total on the order of $62 billion in revenue; the firms that entered those markets after they were established logged only about $3.3 billion. First movers in disruption got the growth; followers got the leftovers, because the early years are when a firm learns the new customers and builds the low-cost structure the network demands.

If you must be early, and the market must be small, the answer is to make small markets meaningful by giving them to small organizations. Christensen’s cautionary tale is Apple’s Newton. Apple in the early 1990s, a multibillion-dollar company needing big growth, decided personal digital assistants were the next big thing, and managed the Newton like a future flagship: heavy market research, large development spending under CEO John Sculley’s personal sponsorship, a high-profile 1993 launch. The Newton sold around 140,000 units in its first two years—numbers that would have electrified a startup (Apple’s own Apple II sold far fewer in its first two years, and was judged a triumph)—but against Apple’s size and expectations it was scored a humiliating flop, and the retreat poisoned the company’s appetite for the category. The same units, booked by a five-person venture, would have been a rocket. Options that keep the organization matched to the market: spin ventures out, as Quantum did; buy small companies already in the new value network; or run the project in a unit small enough to get excited by small wins. What a big company must not do is force an embryonic market to deliver mainstream-scale revenue on a schedule.

Part Two · Chapter 7

Discovering new and emerging markets

Principle three: markets that do not exist cannot be analyzed. For sustaining innovations, planning works—the customers are known, the trajectory is known, forecasts are decently accurate. For disruptive innovations, expert forecasts are reliably and sometimes comically wrong, because the information a plan requires does not exist yet. Christensen’s evidence from the drive industry: market forecasts for established applications hit within reasonable bounds, while forecasts for disruptive products missed by integer multiples. The failure is not analytic sloppiness; it is epistemic. Suppliers and customers have to discover the market together.

Hewlett-Packard’s Kittyhawk is the canonical self-inflicted wound. In 1991–92, HP’s disk division built the Kittyhawk, a 1.3-inch drive holding 20 megabytes—a marvel. Because HP was huge and wanted the project to matter, the team committed to an aggressive revenue ramp (on the order of $100–150 million within a couple of years) and therefore needed a big application fast. The plan anointed one: pen-based personal digital assistants. The team designed for it—including expensive ruggedness, since PDAs get dropped—and PDAs flopped. Meanwhile real, unforecast demand trickled in from places nobody had planned: Japanese makers of portable word processors, cash registers, and electronic cameras, and then video-game makers who wanted huge volumes of a cheaper, less rugged Kittyhawk. HP couldn’t serve them—the design and cost structure were locked to the dead plan—and the project was killed within about two years. The product was right; the requirement that the first guess be right was wrong.

Honda’s conquest of the North American motorcycle market shows the alternative, which Christensen calls agnostic marketing. In 1959 Honda set up shop in Los Angeles intending to sell big bikes against Harley-Davidson, Triumph, and BMW—that was the market that existed. The big Hondas failed in American conditions: sustained high-speed highway riding leaked oil and burned out clutches. Nearly broke, the team got around LA on the 50cc Supercubs they’d brought from Japan for errands—and strangers kept asking where to buy one. An inquiry from a Sears buyer forced the question. Reluctantly—fearing the little bike would embarrass the brand among “real” motorcycle buyers—Honda began selling Supercubs, not through motorcycle dealers (most refused them) but through sporting-goods retailers, to people who had never wanted a motorcycle: off-road, dirt-path recreational riders. The market named itself. In 1963 a UCLA undergraduate’s ad concept became the “You Meet the Nicest People on a Honda” campaign, cementing a value network Harley couldn’t enter—its dealers, tasting the margins, wouldn’t push small bikes, and Harley (which tried, via the Italian maker Aermacchi’s small machines) retreated up-market to the heavyweight niche it still occupies. Honda then rode its trajectory up through middleweight and big bikes. Nothing in the outcome was in the plan; what Honda did right was survive its first guess being wrong.

Intel’s microprocessor provides the in-house version. Intel was a DRAM company; the microprocessor arrived as a side effect of a calculator-chip contract with the Japanese firm Busicom. Intel did not foresee the microprocessor’s markets—nobody did—but its resource-allocation rule saved it: fab capacity was allocated by gross margin per wafer, and as Japanese competition crushed DRAM margins in the early 1980s, wafer starts drained automatically toward the higher-margin microprocessors, repositioning the company well before top management formally exited DRAMs in 1985. Christensen’s prescriptions follow: treat the first plan as a hypothesis; budget to be wrong; use what Rita Gunther McGrath and Ian MacMillan call discovery-driven planning, in which the plan’s assumptions, not its projections, get tested first and cheaply; conserve enough resources for the second and third try, since the odds that the first targeting is correct are low; and watch what customers do—like the Supercub errand-runners—rather than what they say.

Part Two · Chapter 8

How to appraise your organization’s capabilities and disabilities

Principle four: an organization’s capabilities define its disabilities. Managers staff a disruptive project with capable people and assume the organization is therefore capable. But capabilities reside in three distinct places, and only one of them is portable.

Resources, processes, values (RPV)

Resources—people, cash, technology, equipment, brands, customer relationships—are visible and flexible; they can be hired, bought, moved. Processes—the patterns of interaction, coordination, and decision-making by which the organization turns inputs into outputs—are tuned to specific recurring tasks, and by design resist change. Values—the criteria by which everyone in the firm prioritizes: which orders matter, which margins are acceptable, which opportunities count as big—are the least flexible of all, and in a well-run firm they are pervasive. A process that is a capability for one task is a disability for another; values that make a firm great at its business make it incapable of a business with lower margins or smaller markets.

The diagnostic example is Digital Equipment’s failure in personal computers. DEC had every resource the PC business required: brilliant engineers, cash, brand, technology. But its processes had been honed to design minicomputers—largely proprietary, internally developed components, two-to-three-year design cycles—while the PC business demanded machines assembled from outsourced, modular components on one-year cycles. And its values had been trained by minicomputer economics—gross margins of 50 percent or more—so that a 25-to-30-percent-margin PC proposal could never win the internal contest for resources; the company lurched in and out of the PC business four times as the mainstream organization kept reasserting its priorities. The people were capable; the organization, as an organization, was not.

The managerial tool is a two-question test for any innovation: do the organization’s existing processes fit this task, and do its values give the task priority? Four answers, four structures. If both fit, run it in the mainstream organization—this is why incumbents win sustaining battles. If the values fit but the processes don’t, create a heavyweight team—a colocated group pulled out of the functional structure, free to invent new ways of working, as Chrysler did with its platform teams. If the processes fit but the values don’t—the classic disruptive case—spin the project out into an autonomous organization whose cost structure makes the small, low-margin market attractive, as Quantum and Control Data did, and as IBM did when it built its PC business in a deliberately independent unit in Boca Raton, Florida, free to source components outside, sell through retail channels, and accept different margins. If neither fits, both spin out and develop new processes. A corollary on acquisitions: if you buy a company for its resources, integrate it; if you buy it for its processes and values—as is usually the case with a disruptive attacker—integration destroys precisely what you paid for, so it must be kept standing alone and resources flowed into it.

Part Two · Chapter 9

Performance oversupply and the shifting basis of competition

Principle five: technology supply may not equal market demand. Because technology trajectories are steeper than demand trajectories, products eventually give mainstream customers more performance than they can use—performance oversupply—and at that moment the basis of competition changes underneath the incumbent. Christensen dates the phenomenon precisely in disk drives: by around 1988 the capacity of 3.5-inch drives had satisfied the mainstream desktop, and customers stopped paying premiums for more megabytes; capacity became a hygiene factor, the price per megabyte of the once-“inferior” smaller drives crossed below the larger ones, and demand snapped to the smaller form factor.

The buying hierarchy

A pattern (which Christensen credits to the consulting firm Windermere Associates) describing how the basis of competition evolves once each layer of demand is satisfied: customers choose first on functionality; when rival products all function well enough, on reliability; then on convenience; and finally on price. Each disruptive generation typically enters as a cheaper, more convenient, more reliable product whose functionality is merely adequate—exactly the profile that wins as a market descends the hierarchy.

Two case studies show the pattern outside hardware. In accounting software, the sophisticated packages competed on ever-richer features and reports—functionality the small-business owner, who is not an accountant, could not absorb. Scott Cook’s Intuit attacked with QuickBooks in the early 1990s: built on the easy Quicken interface, stripped of much of the conventional double-entry reporting, doing less than rival products—and within roughly two years it had taken the large majority of the small-business segment, because the basis of competition there had already shifted from functionality to convenience. In insulin, Eli Lilly spent on the order of a billion dollars developing Humulin, biosynthetic human insulin of essentially perfect purity—the culmination of a decades-long trajectory that had driven impurities from tens of thousands of parts per million in the 1920s to about 10 ppm in animal insulins by 1980. Priced at a roughly 25 percent premium, Humulin met a shrug: for most patients, 10 ppm pork insulin was already more than good enough; purity was oversupplied. Meanwhile Denmark’s Novo prospered with insulin pens—a convenience innovation that cut a multi-step syringe ritual to seconds—and earned its premium. The deep warning of the chapter: sustaining trajectories are how great firms overshoot; racing up-market toward higher performance and higher margins feels like excellence precisely while it hands the broadening, good-enough tiers of the market to simpler, cheaper, more convenient disruptors. Companies can either ride the up-market escalator and accept eventual confinement to a shrinking summit, or deliberately descend and fight the disruptors with their economics—but they cannot stand still on the chart.

Part Two · Chapters 10–11

Managing disruptive change: the electric-vehicle case study, and the dilemmas summarized

Chapter 10 is a thought experiment: Christensen imagines himself a program manager at a major automaker, charged with the electric vehicle in the shadow of the California Air Resources Board mandate, which then required that zero-emission vehicles be 2 percent of a carmaker’s California sales by 1998, rising to 10 percent by 2003. He works the problem with the book’s tools, step by step, as a worked example the reader can apply to any candidate disruption.

First question: is the technology actually disruptive? Test: do mainstream customers want it? No—surveyed car buyers demand roughly 125 to 150 miles of range and acceleration to 60 mph in under about 10 seconds, while electric vehicles of the mid-1990s managed perhaps 50 to 80 miles and far slower acceleration. EVs cannot be sold into the mainstream value network; any plan that assumes so will fail. Second test: is the technology improving faster than mainstream demand is rising? Plotting the trajectories, EV range and acceleration had been improving at rates that—if sustained—would intersect mainstream demands in the foreseeable future, while the mainstream’s minimum requirements crept up slowly. Conclusion: the electric vehicle fits the disruptive profile—unusable by current customers, on a steeper improvement slope than demand. Note the posture: he does not claim to forecast when trajectories cross, only that the trajectory comparison, not customer surveys, is the right instrument.

Then the program design, principle by principle. Marketing: since markets that don’t exist can’t be analyzed, no market research can identify the first EV customers; the plan must be a plan for discovery, cheap enough to be wrong twice. He refuses to bet on the obvious guess (commuter cars for environmentalists) and instead reasons about who might value an EV’s actual attributes—quiet, simple, short-range—speculating, for instance, about parents buying safe, range-limited cars for high-school students, or fleet and dense-urban uses in congested Asian cities; but he insists these are hypotheses to test with real products, not forecasts to build a factory on. Product design: because the basis of competition is unknowable in advance, the vehicle must be simple, cheap, and fast to iterate—a design-cycle measured in months, a low break-even, and a platform that can be re-aimed when the first market guess is wrong. Pricing and cost structure must accept low margins and small volumes happily. Distribution: mainstream dealer networks, whose economics live on high-ticket sales and service, will not push a strange low-margin product, so new channels must be found. And organization: the whole effort belongs in an autonomous unit—he points to GM’s Saturn as evidence the industry knew how to build separate organizations, while criticizing the industry’s actual EV programs (like the mainstream-targeted, technology-first launches of the mid-1990s) for aiming expensive, heavily subsidized cars at the customers least able to use them. He explicitly declines to bet the program on a battery breakthrough: waiting for sustaining technology to make the disruptive product acceptable to the mainstream is exactly the trap the book has documented; the breakthrough, if it comes, will be found by firms already accumulating miles in whatever odd niches EVs first serve.

Chapter 11 gathers the findings as a list of the dilemmas’ resolutions, which double as the book’s summary. The trajectories of market demand and technology improvement differ, so products that miss the market today may be squarely mainstream tomorrow. Managing innovation mirrors the resource-allocation process: ideas that customers and margins favor get funded, so disruptive projects must be lodged where customers create that pull. Disruptive markets are small and unknowable in advance, so the first entrants’ advantage comes from learning cheaply and early, in organizations small enough to celebrate small wins. Organizations have capabilities—processes and values—independent of the people inside them, and creating new capabilities means creating new organizational space. And the attribute profile that makes disruptive products worthless in mainstream markets—simple, cheap, convenient—is precisely what makes them win in the new markets where they incubate. The dilemma is real, Christensen ends, but it is not a death sentence: managers who understand these laws can stand in the path of disruption and steer it—harnessing, as he puts it, the forces that defeat firms that ignore them.

Reading it critically

The book’s influence is hard to overstate—“disruption” became, for better and worse, the governing cliché of two decades of technology strategy—and the criticisms are correspondingly well developed. Jill Lepore’s 2014 New Yorker essay “The Disruption Machine” re-examined the founding cases and argued the histories are messier than the theory: Seagate was not destroyed by the 3.5-inch transition, several “disrupted” incumbents prospered for decades, and the disk-drive industry ultimately consolidated around incumbents. Andrew King and Baljir Baatartogtokh (MIT Sloan Management Review, 2015) surveyed experts on 77 cases Christensen and his collaborators had labeled disruption and found only a minority fit all four elements of the theory. The theory’s predictive record is mixed—Christensen famously predicted in 2007 that the iPhone, which he read as a sustaining improvement on phones rather than a disruption of laptops, would not succeed. Later work refined the framework substantially: The Innovator’s Solution (2003) distinguished low-end from new-market disruption and added the jobs-to-be-done lens, and Christensen himself spent his last years protesting how loosely the word “disruption” was applied. None of this erases the book’s core, still-standing contributions: the sustaining/disruptive asymmetry, the trajectory-intersection chart, resource dependence as the real seat of strategy, and the demonstration that failure can be the deductive consequence of doing everything right by the customers you already have.

If you remember ten things

  1. Great firms fail not despite good management but because of it: listening to the best customers and funding the highest-return investments systematically rejects disruptive technologies.
  2. Sustaining innovations—however radical technologically—improve what existing customers value, and incumbents almost always lead them; disruptive innovations are initially worse on those dimensions, and entrants almost always win them.
  3. Technology improves on a steeper trajectory than market demand, so a product that undershoots a market today will pierce it from below tomorrow—and overshoot it the day after.
  4. In disk drives, every smaller form factor—14-inch to 8 to 5.25 to 3.5 inches—was rejected by incumbents’ customers, commercialized by entrants in a new market, and then carried up-market to kill the incumbents.
  5. A value network sets which attributes count and what margins are normal; firms can migrate up-market within and across networks easily and almost never down, because every rational signal points northeast.
  6. Hydraulic backhoes and steel minimills replayed the drive story in slow motion: of some thirty cable-shovel makers only four crossed to hydraulics, and integrated steelmakers profitably ceded rebar, then bars, then beams, then sheet to Nucor and its kin.
  7. Resource dependence means customers effectively control resource allocation, so disruptive projects survive only inside organizations whose own customers need them—spin out or place the project where the pull exists.
  8. Small markets cannot solve big companies’ growth needs, yet leadership in disruption is decisive ($62 billion for leaders versus $3.3 billion for followers in drives), so match small organizations to small markets early.
  9. Markets that do not exist cannot be analyzed: plan to learn rather than to execute, conserve resources for the second and third guess, and watch what customers do—Honda’s Supercub found its market running errands, and HP’s Kittyhawk died of a confident forecast.
  10. Capabilities live in processes and values, not just people; when a task fits neither—and when performance overshoots and competition shifts from functionality to reliability, convenience, then price—only a separate organization with its own economics can do the disrupting for you.

Concepts to keep

Sustaining vs. disruptive technology

Sustaining innovations improve performance on dimensions existing customers value — and incumbents win them, even radical ones. Disruptive innovations are worse on those dimensions but cheaper, simpler, or more convenient, and they enter through customers the incumbent doesn’t want. The distinction is about trajectory and market, not technical sophistication.

Value networks

The nested context of customers, suppliers, and margin structures within which a firm operates, which silently determines how it ranks any innovation’s attributes. Firms don’t evaluate technologies in the abstract; their value network does it for them.

Resource dependence

In practice, customers and investors control a firm’s resource allocation more than its managers do: proposals serving the best customers get funded, others starve. This is why edict cannot beat disruption — the organization’s funding reflexes must be rehoused, not overruled.

Performance oversupply

Technology improves faster than customers can absorb, so products eventually overshoot mainstream needs. Once they do, competition shifts to reliability, convenience, and price — the moment a “worse” product becomes good enough, and the door through which every disruptor walks.

The upmarket ratchet

Moving toward higher margins always looks rational and moving down never does, so incumbents cede the low end repeatedly — and each retreat improves their reported profitability while shrinking their ground. Minimill steel is the canonical demonstration of rational extinction.

Small markets and large firms

A market big enough to matter to a giant is already too late to enter; a market small enough to enter early cannot move a giant’s needle. Matching organization size to opportunity size is the only way out of this arithmetic.

Agnostic marketing

For markets that don’t yet exist, no one — not the firm, not its customers — knows how the product will be used. Plan to learn rather than to execute: cheap probes, expectation of failure, resources reserved for the second and third try.

For the AI researcher

The instinctive application — “AI will disrupt everything” — is exactly the buzzword usage Christensen’s actual theory resists, so apply it with precision. The frontier-model race itself looks mostly sustaining: every lab is improving along the dimensions its best customers already value (reasoning, reliability, context, coding), which is the kind of fight Christensen predicts well-resourced incumbents win. Google’s response to OpenAI was not the paralysis of a disk drive maker but the mobilization of a giant on a sustaining trajectory it understands. The disruptive vector, if there is one, runs along the other axis: open-weight and distilled models that are worse on benchmarks but cheap, private, and locally deployable, colonizing users the frontier labs’ pricing and infrastructure ignore — and riding their own steep improvement curve upmarket.

Performance oversupply is the concept to watch most closely. For a large share of real workloads — summarization, extraction, routine coding, support — frontier models overshot “good enough” some time ago, and the basis of competition there is already tumbling down Christensen’s ladder from performance to reliability, convenience, and price. That is precisely the regime in which a vastly cheaper, slightly worse model takes the volume while the premium product retreats upmarket into ever-harder tasks — a retreat that, as the minimill story teaches, can look like healthy margin discipline right up until it doesn’t. The labs’ bet, implicit in every scaling roadmap, is that the hardest tier of tasks is economically deep enough that upmarket is where the value lives. That bet may well be right — agents and research-grade reasoning are not rebar — but it is a bet, and Christensen documents how rarely the incumbents holding it see the floor falling away beneath them.

There is also a personal reading for a researcher. Labs allocate attention the way firms allocate capital: toward the benchmarks and customers that define their value network. The ideas that reorder fields tend to begin as embarrassments by prevailing metrics — worse perplexity, weaker leaderboard scores, the wrong conference. Christensen’s deepest lesson for you is not about companies at all: it is to notice which evaluation regime is silently doing your resource allocation, and to keep a small, separate organization in your own head where unimpressive-looking trajectories are allowed to compound.
