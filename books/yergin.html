Geopolitics of Energy

The Prize: The Epic Quest for Oil, Money, and Power

Daniel Yergin · 1990
~30 hours to read Condensed read below: ~35 min read How oil made — and ruled — the twentieth century Pairs with: Chip War

Every story this library tells about compute — chokepoints, export controls, state-backed champions, cartel temptations, the entanglement of a strategic input with war and money — happened first, at planetary scale, with oil. The Prize is the master narrative of the master resource of the twentieth century, and reading it now is reading a preview: compute and energy are becoming to this century what oil was to the last.

Daniel Yergin is a Harvard- and Cambridge-trained historian who left academia to co-found Cambridge Energy Research Associates, the consultancy through which he watched the 1980s price collapse from inside the industry. He spent most of a decade writing this book and finished it under deadline pressure of the most dramatic kind: as the manuscript closed, Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait, and the history he had been writing became the front page. Published in late 1990, the book won the Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction in 1992 and became an eight-hour PBS series; thirty-five years later it is still the standard one-volume history of oil, the book every energy analyst, diplomat, and petro-state minister is assumed to have read.

Its method is narrative maximalism: roughly nine hundred pages, five parts, thirty-eight chapters, and a cast running from “Colonel” Edwin Drake, a railroad conductor with an invented title, to Winston Churchill, Stalin, Ibn Saud, Mossadegh, Armand Hammer, Yamani, and Saddam — told through deals, with the contract terms and the barrel counts left in. Yergin’s wager is that the specifics are the argument: you cannot understand why the modern state, the modern corporation, and the modern wars look the way they do without following the oil.

For this shelf it is the deep case study behind Miller’s one-line thesis that chips are “the new oil.” Before evaluating that analogy you need to know what the old oil actually did — and The Prize is where that knowledge lives.

The core argument

The history of oil is the history of the modern world, told through three intertwined themes. First, oil is the story of the rise and development of capitalism and modern business — the world’s biggest industry, the first great multinationals, the original trust and the original antitrust. Second, oil is a strategic commodity entwined with national power: from Churchill’s conversion of the Royal Navy onward, mastery of oil has meant mastery in war, and the great conflicts of the century were fought partly over and decisively with it. Third, the twentieth century transformed us into a “Hydrocarbon Society” and made us, in the language of anthropologists, “Hydrocarbon Man” — a civilization whose food, mobility, materials, and suburbs are built out of petroleum.

The engine of the narrative is a permanent, unresolved oscillation between shortage and glut. Oil is found in gushers — Spindletop, East Texas, Kirkuk, Ghawar — that crash prices, and every era of the industry has been an attempt to impose order on that chaos: Rockefeller’s monopoly, the As-Is cartel, the Texas Railroad Commission, the Seven Sisters, OPEC. Each ordering regime works for a while, breeds the investment and resentment that undermine it, and collapses into the next crisis. Yergin’s deeper point is that the “market” for oil has never been simply a market; it has always been a negotiation among companies, producing states, and consuming states over who captures the rent from the world’s most valuable trade.

The second through-line is the migration of power. For its first ninety years the industry was run by companies headquartered in New York, London, and The Hague, operating under concessions that treated sovereign territory as leasable acreage. The book’s long middle documents the slow inversion — 50-50 profit splits, nationalizations, OPEC — until by 1973 the producing states could embargo their own customers. The “prize” of the title, from Churchill’s phrase that in converting the navy to oil “mastery itself was the prize of the venture,” keeps changing hands; the quest for it never ends.

Yergin is neither a moralist nor a doomer. He writes as a sympathetic chronicler of an industry he plainly finds magnificent and dangerous in equal measure, and he ends in 1990 exactly where he began in 1859: with the world discovering, again, that it cannot look away from oil.

The book runs in five parts and thirty-eight chapters from the summer of 1859 to the autumn of 1990: Part I, “The Founders,” covers Drake, Rockefeller, and the first global contest; Part II, “The Global Struggle,” runs through Churchill, the First World War, and the interwar cartels and gushers; Part III, “War and Strategy,” is the Second World War; Part IV, “The Age of Hydrocarbon,” builds the postwar order from Aramco to the eve of the embargo; Part V, “The Battle for World Mastery,” runs from the oil weapon of 1973 through the 1986 collapse to Saddam’s tanks in Kuwait City. This condensation merges those thirty-eight chapters into eleven sections following the same arc, keeping Yergin’s deals, dates, and barrel counts. What is compressed most is texture — the wildcatter biographies, the boardroom set pieces — of which the book has hundreds; what is kept is everything you need to reconstruct the argument.

Part I · The Founders

Drake’s folly and the new light

The opening question is why anyone drilled for oil at all. The answer is illumination. Mid-nineteenth-century America was lit, expensively and badly, by whale oil, lard, and volatile camphene; the whales were getting scarcer and the price of good light was a luxury. A New York lawyer and promoter named George Bissell had the founding insight: the “rock oil” that seeped uselessly out of the ground in northwestern Pennsylvania — bottled and sold as patent medicine — might be refined into an illuminant. In 1855 his investors paid Yale’s Benjamin Silliman Jr. for a chemical analysis (the professor held the report hostage until his fee, $526.08, was paid in full), and Silliman delivered the industry’s founding document: rock oil could be distilled into a first-rate lamp fuel. The product would be called kerosene. The remaining problem was supply — skimming seeps yielded gallons, and the business needed barrels. Bissell’s second insight, borrowed from the salt-boring trade, was to drill for it.

The man sent to do it was Edwin Drake, an unemployed railroad conductor chosen partly because his rail pass made him cheap to ship to Titusville; the investors addressed letters to “Colonel” E. L. Drake to impress the locals, and the invented title stuck. Through 1858–59 Drake and his salt-borer driller, “Uncle Billy” Smith, fought collapsing boreholes (Drake’s improvisation, driving an iron pipe down to bedrock, became standard practice) while the town called the project Drake’s Folly and the money ran out. On August 27, 1859, at sixty-nine feet, the drill slipped into a crevice; the next day the well was full of oil. A letter from the investors ordering Drake to shut down and come home was already in the mail. It arrived after the strike.

What followed set the industry’s permanent pattern in miniature: the world’s first oil boom and the world’s first oil glut. The “rule of capture” — whoever pumps first owns the oil, regardless of whose land the pool lies under — turned the Oil Regions into a race to drain every reservoir as fast as possible. Flush production from flowing wells swamped demand: oil that fetched $10 a barrel in January 1861 sold for ten cents by the end of that year. (The 42-gallon barrel itself, standardized from old whiskey coopering, survives as the industry’s unit to this day.) Boomtowns materialized and evaporated — Pithole went from empty farmland to perhaps fifteen thousand people in 1865 and back to nothing within a few years — and speculators of every kind churned through the Regions, among them a young actor named John Wilkes Booth, who lost his stake and moved on to other plans. Yet through the chaos, kerosene conquered the world’s lamps within a decade: the new light was the first mass petroleum product, and oil was already, in the 1860s, one of America’s leading exports. The boom-bust violence of the Regions is the problem to which the rest of Part I — Rockefeller — is the terrifying solution.

Part I · The Founders

Rockefeller: “Our Plan” and the 1911 breakup

John D. Rockefeller, a Cleveland produce bookkeeper with a gift for ledgers and an iron Baptist composure, entered refining during the Civil War and bought out his senior partner at auction in 1865 for $72,500 — the moment, he later said, that determined his career. His diagnosis of the industry was clinical: drilling was a lottery, but refining and transport could be systematized, and the industry’s ruinous boom-bust cycle was a disease to be cured by consolidation — by what he and his partners simply called “Our Plan.” Standard Oil was incorporated in 1870, and its decisive weapon was scale bargaining with the railroads: secret rebates off the published freight rates and, more ruthlessly, “drawbacks” — payments to Standard on its competitors’ shipments. The notorious South Improvement Company scheme of 1872 collapsed in public scandal before carrying a single barrel, but in the panic it created Rockefeller executed the “Cleveland Massacre,” absorbing twenty-two of his twenty-six Cleveland rivals in about six weeks. By 1879 Standard controlled roughly 90 percent of American refining; when long-distance pipelines emerged as a threat, it captured those too. The Standard Oil Trust, formalized in 1882 and run from 26 Broadway, was the prototype of the modern corporation — vertically integrated, professionally managed, ruthlessly cost-minimizing. Rockefeller saw himself not as a predator but as a missionary of order: “We must ever remember we are refining oil for the poor man and he must have it cheap and good,” he insisted — and kerosene’s price did fall steadily under the monopoly.

Two forces broke the colossus. The first was a journalist. Ida Tarbell had grown up in the Oil Regions, the daughter of an independent producer ruined in Standard’s wake; her History of the Standard Oil Company, serialized in McClure’s across 1902–04, was investigative journalism’s founding masterpiece — meticulous, documented, and devastating. It made Standard the emblem of everything the Progressive Era feared, and gave Theodore Roosevelt’s trust-busters their central target. On May 15, 1911, the Supreme Court ordered the trust dissolved into more than thirty separate companies. The breakup’s irony is one of Yergin’s favorites: the successor companies — Jersey Standard (later Exxon), Standard of New York (Mobil), Standard of California (Chevron), Standard of Indiana (Amoco), and the rest — were soon worth far more apart than together, and Rockefeller, holding his pro-rata shares through the run-up, emerged with a fortune approaching $900 million, the richest man in America. Told the news on a golf course, he reportedly advised his partner to buy Standard stock.

The second force was technological, and it cut both ways. Edison’s electric light, spreading from 1882, doomed kerosene — the product on which the whole industry had been built. But at the same moment the automobile arrived: by 1910 gasoline, formerly a near-waste fraction, was on its way to becoming the industry’s reason for being. Oil’s history, Yergin notes, is punctuated by these reprieves — the industry has repeatedly been saved from obsolescence by a new use no one planned for.

Part I · The Founders

Baku, Shell, and Spindletop: the contest goes global

While Standard ruled America, the first challenge came from the Caspian. Baku, on the Absheron Peninsula, sat over oil so abundant it had fed Zoroastrian fire temples; under Russian rule it became, by the 1890s, the most prolific oil district on earth — around 1900, Russia briefly out-produced the United States. The architects were unexpected: Robert Nobel, sent south in 1873 to buy walnut for rifle stocks, bought a refinery instead, and his brother Ludwig built the Nobels into Baku’s dominant producers — inventing, along the way, the modern oil tanker (the Zoroaster, 1878). The Rothschilds of Paris financed the railway that carried Baku kerosene to Batum on the Black Sea, opening Russian oil to the world and triggering decades of price war with Standard for the markets of Europe and Asia.

Into that war stepped Marcus Samuel, a London merchant whose family firm had begun by selling seashell trinket boxes. Samuel’s 1892 coup was logistical: a purpose-built bulk tanker, the Murex, certified safe for the Suez Canal — which Standard’s ships could not transit — carrying Rothschild kerosene east at costs Standard could not match. The syndicate became Shell Transport and Trading in 1897, named for the trinkets. Its eventual partner and master was Royal Dutch, born in 1890 to work oil seeps in Sumatra and driven by Henri Deterding, a Napoleonic figure who combined a tanker-rate clerk’s arithmetic with imperial ambition. In 1907 the two merged as Royal Dutch/Shell on Deterding’s unsentimental terms — 60 percent Dutch, 40 percent British. The combination got its timing right twice over: it diversified away from Russia just as the 1905 revolution swept Baku — fields burned, ethnic massacres between Armenians and Tatars, a young agitator named Stalin organizing the oil workers — permanently breaking Russian oil’s momentum.

The third front opened in Texas. On January 10, 1901, on a salt-dome hill called Spindletop near Beaumont — drilled on the obsessive faith of a one-armed local promoter, Patillo Higgins, and the engineering of an Austrian-born captain, Anthony Lucas, with Pittsburgh money from the Mellons — a well blew in at perhaps 75,000 barrels a day, a flow rate the industry had never imagined. Spindletop’s heavy, sulfurous crude was poor for kerosene but ideal as fuel oil, and it announced oil’s second age: oil as energy — for ships, locomotives, and soon automobiles — rather than light. Because Texas law and politics kept Standard out, the gusher incubated new giants: Gulf Oil (the Mellons) and the Texas Company, later Texaco. By the time the Supreme Court dismembered Standard in 1911, the world industry was already multipolar — American successors, Royal Dutch/Shell, the Nobels and Rothschilds, and, gestating in Persia, the company that would tie oil to the state itself.

Part II · The Global Struggle

Churchill’s gamble and the First World War

Persia entered the story as a rich man’s speculation. William Knox D’Arcy, who had made a fortune in Australian gold, bought a sixty-year concession from the Persian Shah in 1901 covering some three-quarters of the country, for £20,000 cash, £20,000 in shares, and 16 percent of future profits. Seven years of drilling under his engineer George Reynolds — heat, disease, tribal politics, and money running out, with Burmah Oil brought in to keep the venture alive — culminated on May 26, 1908, at Masjid-i-Suleiman, where the drill struck oil at around 1,180 feet just as the syndicate was preparing to quit. The Anglo-Persian Oil Company floated in 1909 and built its refinery at Abadan; what it lacked was a customer big enough to matter.

The customer was the Royal Navy, and the agent of conversion was Winston Churchill, made First Lord of the Admiralty in 1911 amid the naval race with Germany. Oil-fired ships were faster, ranged farther, refueled at sea, and freed crews from the half-day misery of coaling — but Britain had Welsh coal and no oil, and switching the fleet’s lifeblood to a fuel from Persia was strategic vertigo. Prodded by the oil-obsessed Admiral Fisher, Churchill committed the new fast battleships to oil and resolved the supply problem with a then-radical act of state capitalism: on June 17, 1914, on Churchill’s motion, the House of Commons voted for the British government to buy 51 percent of Anglo-Persian for £2.2 million. Eleven days later the Archduke was shot at Sarajevo. Churchill later wrote of the conversion that the risks were enormous but “mastery itself was the prize of the venture” — the line that gives Yergin his title.

The war that followed was the first internal-combustion war, and oil decided more of it than contemporaries expected. In September 1914 the military governor of Paris, Gallieni, threw reinforcements into the Battle of the Marne in some six hundred commandeered taxicabs — a logistical footnote that became the emblem of motorized war. Trucks replaced rail and horse at the front; the tank (its name a security cover story) broke the trench deadlock from 1916; aircraft turned fuel into airpower. By late 1917 the Allies faced a genuine petroleum crisis — German U-boats were sinking tankers faster than they could be built — and Clemenceau cabled President Wilson that gasoline was as vital as blood in the coming battles, demanding American tankers. America supplied the margin: US fields furnished the great bulk of Allied oil. The other side of the ledger was German starvation. Britain’s “Empire Jack” Norton-Griffiths dynamited and burned Romania’s fields and refineries ahead of the German advance in 1916; Ludendorff’s 1918 lunge toward Baku was a fuel raid dressed as a campaign. Ten days after the Armistice, at a London dinner for the Inter-Allied Petroleum Conference, Lord Curzon pronounced the war’s epitaph: the Allied cause had floated to victory upon a wave of oil. Every general staff on earth took the lesson. From 1918 onward, oil was a state interest everywhere, forever.

Part II · The Global Struggle

Cartels and gushers between the wars

The 1920s opened in shortage panic — official American forecasts saw exhaustion within a decade, and the powers scrambled for Mesopotamia, with Britain and France carving out oil rights at San Remo in 1920 while Washington demanded an “open door” for its companies (at home, the scramble’s seamy side surfaced as Teapot Dome, the bribery scandal that wrecked the Harding cabinet). The Mesopotamian negotiation took eight years and produced two of the most consequential cartel documents ever signed. The Red Line Agreement of July 31, 1928 settled the Turkish Petroleum Company (soon Iraq Petroleum): Anglo-Persian, Shell, the French CFP, and an American group each took 23.75 percent, with 5 percent for the indestructible middleman Calouste Gulbenkian — “Mr. Five Percent” — who drew a red line around the former Ottoman Empire on a map and bound every partner to develop oil inside it only jointly. Weeks later, at Achnacarry Castle in the Scottish Highlands — nominally a grouse shoot — Deterding of Shell, Walter Teagle of Jersey Standard, and John Cadman of Anglo-Persian drafted the “As-Is” agreement: world market shares frozen as they stood, facilities shared, competition suspended. The great companies had concluded that the only cure for oil’s chronic disease — glut — was not to compete.

The earth itself made a mockery of them. In October 1930 a seventy-year-old promoter named Columbus “Dad” Joiner — drilling on land the majors’ geologists had written off, guided by the cheerfully fraudulent science of “Doc” Lloyd — brought in the Daisy Bradford No. 3 in East Texas and uncovered the largest field ever found in the lower forty-eight, some forty-five miles long. (Joiner, tangled in his own overlapping promissory notes, sold out to a poker-playing trader named H. L. Hunt, founding one of America’s great fortunes.) As the Depression deepened, East Texas flooded the market with up to a million barrels a day; the price fell from around a dollar to thirteen cents and, in moments, to two. Order came from an unlikely quarter: in August 1931 the governor of Texas sent the National Guard to shut the field by martial law, and thereafter the Texas Railroad Commission — backed by federal law against bootleg “hot oil” in 1935 — rationed production to match demand, state by state, well by well. Yergin’s point lands hard in retrospect: prorationing made Texas the world’s swing producer and the Railroad Commission the institutional ancestor of OPEC.

Meanwhile the map of reserves quietly revolutionized itself. Mexico, long a major exporter, nationalized its industry under Cárdenas on March 18, 1938 — the first great expropriation, and a forty-year warning. And in the Gulf, the future arrived on absurdly cheap terms: Standard of California found oil in Bahrain in 1932; in May 1933 it bought the Saudi concession from Ibn Saud’s treasury-starved kingdom for roughly £35,000 in gold up front; Gulf and Anglo-Persian jointly took Kuwait. In 1938 both bets paid — Dammam No. 7 in Saudi Arabia in March, the colossal Burgan field in Kuwait — and Socal, needing markets, folded its Eastern position into a joint venture with Texaco. Almost nobody noticed: the wells were small, the war was coming, and the meaning of Arabian oil would only become legible afterward.

Part III · War and Strategy

The Second World War ran on oil

Yergin reads the Second World War as, to a remarkable degree, a war about oil fought with oil — and both Axis powers as prisoners of the same deficiency. Japan imported nearly all its oil, about 80 percent of it from the United States, a dependence that made every step of its expansion a hostage negotiation with Washington. When Japan occupied southern Indochina in July 1941, Roosevelt froze Japanese assets — a freeze that hardliners in the bureaucracy administered as a total oil embargo. Tokyo’s planners calculated roughly two years of stockpiles and concluded that the choice was withdrawal from China or war for the oil of the Dutch East Indies; the attack on Pearl Harbor was the flank guard of an oil grab. Yergin’s ironies are precise: the Japanese strike force spared Pearl Harbor’s exposed fuel farm — some 4.5 million barrels — whose destruction, Nimitz later said, would have immobilized the Pacific Fleet far longer than the loss of the battleships; and Japan never solved the protection of its own tankers. American submarines strangled the supply line from the southern fields; by 1944–45 the Imperial Navy was positioning fleets to be near fuel rather than near the enemy, pilot training was cut to save gasoline, the kamikaze’s one-way mission was in part fuel arithmetic, and the home islands were reduced to digging pine roots to distill into aviation spirit.

Germany’s war had the same shape. Hitler, acutely oil-conscious, built the Reich’s mobility on I.G. Farben’s synthetic-fuel plants — coal hydrogenation at Leuna and its sisters — which at their peak in early 1944 supplied on the order of 124,000 barrels a day, more than half of Germany’s oil and almost all of the Luftwaffe’s aviation gasoline. Conquest was meant to fix the rest: Romania’s Ploiesti fields first, then the Caucasus. The 1942 summer campaign aimed at Maikop, Grozny, and Baku — “If I do not get the oil of Maikop and Grozny,” Hitler told his generals, “then I must end this war” — and died at Stalingrad, on the flank of the road to Baku; German troops planted a flag atop Mount Elbrus but captured only wrecked fields. Rommel’s Afrika Korps was throttled by fuel logistics across the Mediterranean (“Shortage of petrol!” he wrote home — enough to make one weep). Then, on May 12, 1944, American bombers finally turned on the synthetic plants; Speer wrote afterward that with those raids the technological war was decided. By autumn the Luftwaffe was grounded for fuel, and the December 1944 Ardennes offensive was built on the desperate premise of capturing American fuel dumps along the way. It didn’t.

The Allies, meanwhile, won the logistics war oil made necessary. Of the roughly seven billion barrels the Allies consumed, some six billion came from the United States. Against the U-boats off the Atlantic coast, America built the Big Inch and Little Big Inch pipelines from Texas to the Eastern Seaboard; for Normandy, PLUTO laid fuel lines under the Channel; American 100-octane aviation gasoline gave Allied aircraft a hidden performance edge. And in 1943–44 the geologist Everette DeGolyer, sent to survey the Gulf, returned with the sentence that organized the next half century of American foreign policy: the center of gravity of world oil production was shifting from the Gulf-Caribbean to the Middle East — to the Persian Gulf — and would keep shifting until it was firmly established there.

Part IV · The Age of Hydrocarbon

The postwar order: Aramco, 50-50, Mossadegh, Suez

The postwar boom turned DeGolyer’s prophecy into infrastructure. Demand exploded — cars, suburbs, plastics, oil-fired industry; the United States, the world’s perennial swing supplier, became a net importer by 1948 — and Saudi Arabia needed capital and markets on a scale Socal and Texaco couldn’t supply alone. In 1948 Jersey Standard and Socony bought into Aramco (30 and 10 percent respectively, beside the founders’ 30-30), tearing up the Red Line Agreement over CFP’s and Gulbenkian’s litigation to do it. Aramco became the greatest industrial prize on earth — and the model of the entangled company-state, complete with the Tapline pipeline to the Mediterranean and a foreign policy of its own.

The producing countries now moved to claim the rent. Venezuela under Juan Pablo Pérez Alfonzo pioneered the instrument in 1948: a 50-50 split of profits between company and state, enforced by sovereign tax law rather than renegotiated concession. The idea traveled — Yergin calls the 50-50 principle one of the most contagious formulas of the century — and in December 1950 Aramco conceded it to Saudi Arabia, sweetened by Washington’s “golden gimmick”: the US Treasury let the companies credit Saudi taxes against their American tax bill, quietly transferring the cost of subsidizing Ibn Saud from Aramco’s shareholders to the American taxpayer, with the National Security Council’s blessing. Riyadh’s take soared; Congress was barely consulted.

Where the formula was refused, catastrophe followed. Anglo-Iranian — Britain’s 51-percent-state-owned crown jewel, paying Iran less than it paid the British exchequer in tax — stonewalled until Mohammed Mossadegh, riding a wave of nationalist fury, nationalized the company in the spring of 1951. The Abadan crisis ran two years: the British evacuated the world’s largest refinery, organized a global boycott of “stolen” Iranian oil (the majors covered the lost barrels from Kuwait and Saudi Arabia, demonstrating the cartel’s power as a stabilizer), and Time made Mossadegh its Man of the Year for 1951. In August 1953, a CIA-MI6 operation — Kermit Roosevelt running money and crowds in Tehran — toppled Mossadegh and restored the Shah. The 1954 settlement was the high-water mark of the corporate order: Iranian oil remained nominally nationalized, but a consortium operated it — BP (as Anglo-Iranian was renamed) with 40 percent, Shell 14, five American majors 8 each, CFP 6. The lesson every producing country drew was double-edged: nationalization would be punished, and the punishment required the companies’ unity to administer.

Suez, 1956, taught the consuming countries their own lesson. Nasser nationalized the Suez Canal Company that July — two-thirds of Europe’s oil came through the canal — and the Anglo-French-Israeli invasion in the autumn closed it entirely: Nasser sank blockships, saboteurs cut the pipelines through Syria, and Europe faced a winter oil shortage. Eisenhower, furious at allies who hadn’t consulted him, withheld emergency oil and blocked support for a collapsing pound until Britain withdrew; Eden’s government fell. The crisis ended European pretensions to independent Middle East power, redirected tanker economics around the Cape (giving birth to the supertanker), and demonstrated that the oil weapon could be pointed at Europe — by Washington as easily as by Cairo.

Part IV · The Age of Hydrocarbon

OPEC, the independents, and the tightening market

OPEC was born not of strength but of insult, in a market drowning in oil. By the late 1950s new production — Soviet exports returning at cut prices, new Middle East capacity, eager newcomers — had glutted the world; in March 1959 Eisenhower imposed mandatory import quotas, walling the vast American market off and deepening the glut outside it. The companies responded by cutting the “posted price” — the reference price on which producing-state taxes were computed — first in 1959, then in August 1960, when Jersey Standard cut postings by up to fourteen cents a barrel without so much as warning the governments whose budgets it gutted. The reaction came in Baghdad on September 14, 1960: Saudi Arabia’s Abdullah Tariki and Venezuela’s Pérez Alfonzo — who had studied the Texas Railroad Commission closely — joined Iran, Iraq, and Kuwait to found the Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries. Its first achievement was negative but permanent: the posted price was never cut again. Otherwise OPEC spent the 1960s nearly powerless, because the surplus made every producer cheat-prone and every buyer mobile; market prices sagged through the decade even as volumes soared.

The companies’ deeper vulnerability was of their own making: the rise of the independents. Newcomers hungry for crude — J. Paul Getty paying record terms in the Neutral Zone, Japanese and Italian ventures (ENI’s Enrico Mattei hawking 75-25 deals against the “Seven Sisters,” his name for the majors), and above all Occidental’s Armand Hammer in Libya — bid up concession terms and, crucially, had no diversified portfolio to fall back on. Libya licensed independents deliberately, and by 1969 it pumped over three million barrels a day of low-sulfur crude sitting west of Suez. Then, on September 1, 1969, a twenty-seven-year-old colonel named Muammar Qaddafi seized power. He picked his victim with care: Occidental, wholly dependent on Libyan oil. Through 1970 Libya cut Occidental’s allowed production until Hammer capitulated — thirty more cents a barrel and a higher tax rate — and the precedent detonated the whole structure. The Shah demanded matching terms in the Gulf (the Tehran Agreement of February 1971), Libya then topped Tehran at Tripoli, and the “leapfrog” showed every producer that the companies would always yield. “Participation” followed — Yamani’s formula for creeping state ownership of the concessions themselves — and outright nationalizations began.

Underneath the politics, the physical market flipped. American crude production peaked in 1970 around 9.6 million barrels a day and began to decline; in March 1972 the Texas Railroad Commission, for the first time, allowed 100 percent production — meaning the spare capacity that had cushioned every crisis from 1951 to 1967 no longer existed. US imports surged past six million barrels a day by 1973, world demand was growing relentlessly, and the cushion was gone. James Akins’s April 1973 Foreign Affairs article said it plainly in its title: this time the wolf was here. All that was missing was the political trigger.

Part V · The Battle for World Mastery

1973: the oil weapon

The trigger was war. Anwar Sadat, planning the assault that began on Yom Kippur — October 6, 1973 — had secured what Nasser never had: King Faisal’s commitment to use Saudi oil as a weapon. The crisis then unfolded on two distinct tracks that the public ever after conflated. The first was price. OPEC’s Gulf members met the companies in Vienna in mid-October to demand a major increase in the posted price; when the companies stalled (asking, fatally, for time to consult their governments), the producers simply stopped negotiating — on October 16 they unilaterally raised the posted price of Arabian Light about 70 percent, from $3.01 to $5.12 a barrel. The era in which companies set the price of oil ended in that room. The second track was supply. On October 17 the Arab producers, meeting in Kuwait, decreed escalating monthly production cuts; after Nixon announced a $2.2 billion arms package for Israel, Saudi Arabia and its partners embargoed the United States (and the Netherlands) outright.

Panic did the rest. With perhaps a twentieth of world supply withdrawn but every buyer scrambling for stocks, spot cargoes went to astonishing levels — an Iranian auction in December fetched over $17 a barrel — and when OPEC met in Tehran on December 22–23, the Shah pushed the posted price to $11.65 effective January 1, 1974. Oil had quadrupled in under three months. In America the shortage arrived as gas lines, odd-even rationing days, a 55-mile-per-hour speed limit, and a winter of presidential addresses; Nixon proclaimed Project Independence (energy self-sufficiency by 1980 — a target no one met or could have). The companies, caught between governments, allocated their global supplies pro rata — “equal misery” — quietly blunting the embargo’s country-by-country targeting; Yergin is clear that the embargo’s economic effects were mostly symbolic while its psychological and political effects were epochal. The embargo was lifted in March 1974, but nothing went back. The greatest peacetime transfer of wealth in history was under way — OPEC oil revenues rose from about $23 billion in 1972 to roughly $140 billion by 1977 — and with it a transfer of sovereignty: through the mid-1970s the producing states completed the takeover of the concessions, converting the majors from owners of Middle East oil into buyers of it. The consuming countries built their own counter-institution, the International Energy Agency, in 1974; Kissinger shuttled; Hydrocarbon Man waited in line, engine idling, and discovered that “the postwar order” had been priced in cheap oil all along.

Part V · The Battle for World Mastery

Second shock, countershock: 1979 panic, 1986 collapse

The second shock began as a labor action and ended as a civilizational event. In the autumn of 1978 strikes spread through the Iranian oil fields; by late December exports had ceased, and on January 16, 1979, the Shah left Iran; Khomeini returned in February. The net loss to world supply was modest — other producers, the Saudis especially, made up much of Iran’s five-plus million barrels a day — but the market’s response was a panic Yergin anatomizes carefully: companies and governments, burned in 1973, hoarded; buyers bought not for consumption but for inventory, amplifying a real shortfall of a few percent into a buying frenzy that more than doubled prices. Spot prices tore loose from official ones; OPEC members leapfrogged each other’s surcharges; by 1980–81 the marker price stood at $34 against $13 before the revolution, with spot cargoes higher still. The political weather matched: Carter’s energy speeches (he had already declared the energy problem “the moral equivalent of war” in 1977), gas lines again in the summer of ’79, the embassy hostages in November, and then, in September 1980, Saddam Hussein’s invasion of Iran, which knocked out both belligerents’ exports and added a war premium to a panic price.

Then came the part almost everyone in the industry got wrong: the countershock. The high prices of 1974–81 had set three slow fuses. Demand collapsed — conservation (efficiency standards, smaller cars, insulated buildings), fuel-switching to coal, gas, and nuclear, and deep recession cut oil use in the non-communist world from about 52 million barrels a day in 1979 to under 46 by 1983, breaking the assumption of eternal growth. Non-OPEC supply surged — the North Sea, Alaska’s Prudhoe Bay through the new pipeline, Mexico’s giant fields — all profitable at the new prices and outside the cartel’s discipline. And oil became a commodity in the financial sense: with the majors’ integrated channels broken by nationalization, barrels moved to the spot market, and from 1983 the New York Mercantile Exchange traded crude futures — price discovery passed from ministries to trading screens. OPEC, defending $34 in a shrinking market, cut output from 31 million barrels a day in 1979 to around 17 by 1985, and Saudi Arabia, as designated swing producer, absorbed the cuts — sliding from about 10 million barrels a day to 2.2 million in the summer of 1985, defending a price that was destroying its own market share. In late 1985 Yamani reversed: netback pricing deals bought back market share, and the price war that followed took oil from over $31 in November 1985 to under $10 in 1986, with some Gulf cargoes near $6. The politics inverted overnight — Vice President George Bush, a Texas oilman, traveled to Riyadh to suggest that free-falling prices threatened American interests too — and in December 1986 OPEC re-fixed around an $18 reference. Yamani, the most famous oil minister in history, learned of his own dismissal from a television news bulletin. The pendulum Yergin began tracking in 1859 — glut, order, shortage, glut — had completed another full swing.

Epilogue

The Gulf, 1990: where the book closes

The book ends where the next war begins. Saddam Hussein emerged from eight years of war with Iran (ended 1988) victorious in name and bankrupt in fact, owing tens of billions to the very Gulf monarchies he believed owed him gratitude for holding back the Iranian revolution. Through the spring and summer of 1990 he converted grievance into ultimatum: Kuwait was overproducing its OPEC quota and depressing the price; Kuwait was, he charged, stealing oil from the shared Rumaila field; the loans must be forgiven and billions more paid. On August 2, 1990, Iraqi armor took Kuwait City in hours. The annexation put roughly a fifth of OPEC’s production under Saddam’s control and his army on the Saudi border, within reach of the fields that DeGolyer had identified as the world’s center of gravity half a century earlier; prices spiked from the mid-teens toward $40 as the UN embargo removed Iraqi and Kuwaiti barrels, and Saudi Arabia and others opened the taps to replace them.

Yergin finished the book with Desert Shield in the sand and the outcome unknown, and the ending is deliberately unresolved — because irresolution is the thesis. Every element of the 1990 crisis is a reprise the reader can now recognize: a producing state using oil as a weapon; a consuming world rediscovering a dependence it had spent the cheap-oil 1980s forgetting; the United States deploying force to keep the Gulf’s oil out of a single hostile hand; the price mechanism transmitting a regional quarrel to every gas pump on earth within days. The final pages return to the prologue’s three themes. Oil remained the world’s biggest business and the strategic commodity of national power, and Hydrocarbon Man — for all the new talk of environment and efficiency — showed no inclination to step down from the driver’s seat. The epic quest for oil, money, and power, Yergin closes, did not end with the twentieth century’s wars; the Gulf crisis was simply its latest chapter, written as the book went to press.

Reading it critically

The standard criticisms have hardened into three. First, vantage: the story is told overwhelmingly from the boardrooms and chancelleries of the West — reviewers and later historians have noted that producing-country societies appear mostly as concession-granters, nationalizers, and crisis-makers, and that the 1953 Iran coup, the Nigerian and Venezuelan experiences, and the human costs of the concession system get lighter moral scrutiny than the prose lavishes on company strategy. Relatedly, Yergin is not a disinterested chronicler: he built and ran a consultancy (CERA, later part of S&P Global) whose clients are the industry, and critics of his subsequent work read him as the industry’s most eloquent advocate. Second, climate: the book was completed in 1990, before climate politics existed in its modern form, and global warming is barely a whisper in nine hundred pages — the deepest consequence of Hydrocarbon Society is the one the book structurally cannot see. Later editions bolt on an epilogue carrying the story past 9/11, and his 2011 sequel The Quest addresses climate and renewables directly, but The Prize itself is a pre-climate book about the fuel that changed the climate. Third, aging: the book’s implicit horizon — Gulf supremacy, OPEC management, ever-deepening import dependence — was scrambled by the US shale revolution after 2008, which restored America to the role of swing-ish producer in a way no one in 1990 forecast. None of this dislodges the history; it does mean the book teaches the twentieth century, and the reader must do the twenty-first.

If you remember ten things

  1. Oil’s history runs on a permanent oscillation between gusher-driven glut and crisis-driven shortage, and every era’s institutions — Standard Oil, As-Is, the Texas Railroad Commission, OPEC — were attempts to manage that swing.
  2. Rockefeller built the prototype of the modern corporation by monopolizing refining and transport, not production; Tarbell’s journalism and the 1911 Supreme Court breakup created both modern antitrust and, accidentally, the supermajors.
  3. Churchill’s conversion of the Royal Navy to oil — and Britain’s purchase of 51 percent of Anglo-Persian eleven days before Sarajevo — fused oil to state power permanently; “mastery itself was the prize of the venture.”
  4. The First World War was won partly on American oil (“floated to victory upon a wave of oil”), and every postwar government treated petroleum as a strategic commodity thereafter.
  5. The interwar order was a cartel — the Red Line and Achnacarry As-Is agreements of 1928 — undone by East Texas, whose ten-cent oil forced state prorationing that became OPEC’s template.
  6. The Second World War was decided in significant part by fuel: Japan went to war over an oil embargo and was strangled by tanker losses; Hitler’s armies died on the road to Baku; the Allies ran on six billion American barrels.
  7. The postwar bargain — Aramco, the contagious 50-50 principle, the golden gimmick, the punishment of Mossadegh — held only as long as surplus capacity and company unity lasted; Qaddafi’s 1970 squeeze of Occidental showed both were gone.
  8. The 1973 shock was two events conflated: producers seizing the power to set prices (October 16) and the Arab embargo (October 17 onward) — the price change was permanent, the embargo largely symbolic.
  9. The 1979 panic was mostly hoarding psychology amplifying a modest shortfall, and the 1986 countershock was its mirror: high prices destroyed demand, summoned non-OPEC supply, and turned oil into an exchange-traded commodity.
  10. The book closes with Saddam in Kuwait in 1990 because its thesis is that the quest doesn’t end: whoever holds the master resource of the era holds a claim on money and power, and the world fights about it.

Concepts to keep

Hydrocarbon Man / Hydrocarbon Society

Yergin’s name for the civilization oil built: mobility, suburbs, plastics, mechanized agriculture, and the assumptions of cheap energy embedded in all of them. The point is that oil is not an industry within society but the substrate of it — which is why supply shocks read as civilizational events, not price changes.

The oil weapon

Supply denial as statecraft: Britain starving Germany of fuel in two world wars, America embargoing Japan in 1941, the Arab producers embargoing America in 1973, Eisenhower withholding oil from Britain at Suez. Its recurring lesson is that the weapon’s psychological blast radius far exceeds its physical one — and that it accelerates every victim’s search for substitutes.

Posted price vs. market price

The posted price was the administered reference on which producer taxes were computed; the market price was what barrels actually fetched. The gap between them is where twentieth-century oil politics lived: companies cutting postings triggered OPEC’s founding in 1960, and producers seizing the posting power in October 1973 ended the corporate era. When spot and futures markets took over in the 1980s, price-setting power dissolved into the trading screen.

The 50-50 principle

Venezuela’s 1948 formula — profits split equally between company and state via sovereign tax law — which spread to Saudi Arabia in 1950 and everywhere thereafter. A case study in how a simple, fair-sounding focal point can rewrite the rents of an entire global industry within a few years, and a way-station on the road from concession to nationalization.

Swing producer

The actor that absorbs supply and demand fluctuations to stabilize price: Texas under the Railroad Commission until 1972, Saudi Arabia thereafter. The role confers power and exacts a price — Saudi output fell from ~10 to 2.2 million barrels a day by mid-1985 defending OPEC’s price, until Riyadh quit the role and collapsed the market. Whoever plays swing producer subsidizes everyone else’s cheating.

Integration and the concession

The majors’ power rested on vertical integration — owning the barrel from wellhead (upstream) through refining and marketing (downstream) — wrapped around concessions that gave companies sovereign-scale rights over foreign territory. Nationalization broke the chain, turning owners into buyers and pushing oil onto open markets; the institutional form of resource control turned out to matter as much as the resource.

Threads to other books

Energy and Civilization — Vaclav Smil
Smil supplies the physics under Yergin’s politics: oil mattered because it is the densest, most transportable prime-mover fuel ever deployed at scale — and Smil’s slow-transition realism explains why Hydrocarbon Man, first named in 1990, is still behind the wheel.
Chip War — Chris Miller
Miller’s explicit frame is that chips are the oil of the twenty-first century; The Prize is the controlled experiment behind the analogy — embargoes, state-backed champions, chokepoint geography — and reading both shows where the analogy holds and where compute’s concentration makes it scarier.
Inflation: A Guide for Users and Losers — Mark Blyth
The 1970s “Great Inflation” that anchors every modern inflation debate was detonated by the oil shocks Yergin narrates from the supply side; Blyth shows what those same barrels did once they entered the price level, the wage bargain, and the political blame game.
Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital — Carlota Perez
Perez’s fourth great surge — oil, the automobile, and mass production — is the macro pattern Yergin documents from inside: cheap oil was the “key factor” of an entire techno-economic paradigm, and the 1970s shocks mark exactly where that paradigm’s golden age cracked.
Seeing Like a State — James C. Scott
Every ordering scheme in Yergin — concessions drawn with a red pencil on an Ottoman map, prorationing, Project Independence, OPEC quotas — is a legibility project imposed on an unruly geological and human reality, and Scott explains the recurring gap between the plan and the field.
For the AI researcher

Read The Prize as the long-run base rate for what happens when an input becomes strategic. The sequence is remarkably stable: a commercial industry scales in political innocence (Pennsylvania, early fabs); a military application makes the state a stakeholder (Churchill’s navy, the Pentagon’s chips); the state buys in, subsidizes, and eventually weaponizes supply (Anglo-Persian’s 51 percent, the golden gimmick — CHIPS Acts and export controls); producers learn their leverage and form coordinating institutions (OPEC — and whatever compute-governance regimes are coming); and consumers respond with efficiency, substitution, and panic-driven stockpiling. If you work on AI, you are working in the early-middle innings of this sequence, and Yergin lets you see the whole game board in advance — including the part where “equal misery” allocation falls to the companies caught between governments, a position today’s frontier labs and foundries should recognize.

The book is also the best available stress test of “compute is the new oil.” What the analogy gets right: a single input underneath every downstream activity, extreme rent concentration, state-industry entanglement, chokepoint geography (Hormuz and Suez then; Taiwan, EUV, and HBM now), and the way embargoes radicalize their targets — Japan in 1941 and the synthetic-fuel campaigns rhyme uncomfortably with chip bans and domestic-accelerator drives. What it gets wrong is just as instructive: oil is a fungible commodity that burns once and prices on a single world market, while compute is durable capital whose effective supply grows with algorithmic efficiency — there is no Moore’s Law for crude — and whose “reserves” are fabs and ideas, not geology. Cartelizing compute is therefore both easier (fewer producers than OPEC ever had) and harder (the resource improves and substitutes for itself).

Two specific lessons travel intact. First, the swing producer’s dilemma: whoever stabilizes a market for others — Texas, then Saudi Arabia; arguably the hyperscalers or TSMC in compute — pays for everyone else’s defection and eventually stops, violently. Second, demand is never as inelastic as the boom believes: the 1979 consensus that oil demand could only grow died within four years, killed by efficiency and substitution that high prices themselves induced. Anyone extrapolating compute demand curves through 2035 should keep the 1986 price collapse — and Yamani learning of his dismissal from a TV bulletin — somewhere in working memory.
