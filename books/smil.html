Energy & Material Substrate

Energy and Civilization: A History

Vaclav Smil · 2017
~14 hours to read Condensed read below: ~45 min read The material ledger of human history Pairs with: Chip War

Every other book in this library is, in a sense, downstream of this one. Before there are markets, institutions, chips, or models, there are joules — and Smil’s history of how humans learned to capture and convert ever more of them is the closest thing we have to a physics of civilization.

Vaclav Smil is an emeritus professor at the University of Manitoba who has spent five decades writing dense, number-saturated books about energy, food, and materials — more than forty of them — while cultivating a reputation as the man who deflates every fashionable techno-narrative with a spreadsheet. Bill Gates famously claimed to wait for new Smil books the way some people wait for new Star Wars films. Smil is neither an optimist nor a pessimist; he is an accountant of physical reality, and he writes with the mild impatience of someone who suspects his readers have never converted anything to watts per square meter.

Energy and Civilization is the mature version of a book he first published in 1994 as Energy in World History, rewritten and expanded after two more decades of scholarship. The question that provokes it is deceptively simple: if all human activity — eating, farming, building, fighting, computing — is at bottom energy conversion, then can history itself be retold as a sequence of energy transitions? Smil’s answer is a careful yes, with a crucial asterisk he saves for the final chapter: energy explains the envelope of what societies can do, but never fully determines what they choose to do within it.

The book’s influence runs quietly through the entire energy-transition debate. When someone points out that solar adoption will take decades rather than years, or that you cannot make steel, cement, ammonia, and plastics with good intentions, they are usually channeling Smil — whether they know it or not. For anyone trying to think clearly about the material requirements of the AI buildout, this is the foundational text.

The core argument

Energy is the only universal currency of human affairs: nothing happens without converting it, and every major epoch of history corresponds to a new way of capturing it — fire, farming, draft animals, water and wind, fossil fuels, electricity. Each transition raised the ceiling on what societies could do, but each took generations to unfold, and the current one will be no exception. Energy sets the boundary conditions of civilization; culture and politics decide what gets built inside them.

The mechanism behind this argument is bookkeeping, relentlessly applied. Smil measures everything in common units — the watts a laboring human body can sustain (roughly 60–100), the power of a good horse (about 700–800 watts), the energy density of dry wood versus coal versus gasoline, the net energy returned by a wheat harvest against the muscle invested in it. Once you see history through this lens, certain puzzles dissolve. Why did traditional societies stagnate for millennia? Because nearly all their usable energy came from photosynthesis — food, feed, and wood — and photosynthesis is a dilute, land-hungry flow that caps how many people, cities, and soldiers a territory can support.

Fossil fuels broke that cap. Coal, oil, and gas are not flows but stores — hundreds of millions of years of buried sunlight, withdrawable on demand at energy densities and power densities that no waterwheel or forest could ever match. The whole strangeness of modernity — billions of people, instant communication, cheap travel, abundant food — is, in Smil’s accounting, the consequence of a single shift from living on an annual solar income to spending an inherited solar fortune.

The asterisk matters as much as the thesis. Smil is no energy determinist: the same fossil-fueled capability built both penicillin factories and Auschwitz, and per-capita energy use correlates with quality of life only up to a surprisingly modest threshold, beyond which the gains flatten into waste. The book ends not with triumph but with a question — whether a civilization built on extraordinary energy throughput can deliberately moderate it — that Smil, characteristically, refuses to answer with comfortable optimism.

Smil’s project is to retell the whole of human history as a history of energy conversions: what prime movers a society commands, what fuels it burns, at what efficiencies, and at what power densities. The book moves chronologically through seven chapters — from foraging energetics through traditional farming, preindustrial machines, fossil fuels and electricity, the fossil-fueled modern world, and finally a synthetic chapter on energy in world history — and this condensation follows that order exactly. Smil’s method is relentless quantification (watts, joules, energy densities, hours of labor per hectare) joined to an equally relentless refusal of energy determinism: energy is, in his phrase, the only universal currency, but it buys very different civilizations. Omitted here are the book’s addenda — the units primer, the basic-measures tables, and the detailed chronologies — and many of the repeated regional yield calculations; the representative cases are kept with their numbers intact.

Chapter 1

Energy and Society

The opening chapter answers a deceptively simple question: why should energy organize a history of civilization at all? Smil’s answer is the book’s most quoted line:

Energy is the only universal currency: one of its many forms must be transformed to get anything done.

— Vaclav Smil, Energy and Civilization, ch. 1

Galaxies, photosynthesis, metabolism, empires, microprocessors — all are energy conversions, and the first law of thermodynamics guarantees that nothing happens without them. Human history, on this view, is a sequence of expansions in the kinds, quantities, and qualities of energy that people can harness, and in the efficiency and power of the conversions they control. Smil sets out the vocabulary the rest of the book uses without apology: energy in joules; power — energy per unit time — in watts; energy density (joules per kilogram of fuel) and power density (watts per square meter of land), the latter being the variable he considers most underrated in all of energy studies. He also fixes the human baseline early: a resting adult runs at roughly 70–80 watts, like a traditional incandescent bulb, and a laborer can sustain perhaps 60–100 watts of useful work over a long day, with brief exertions reaching several hundred watts. Every premodern society had to assemble its pyramids, harvests, and wars largely out of multiples of that small number.

Two framing commitments matter for everything that follows. First, what counts is not gross energy but useful energy — the work actually delivered after conversion losses — and the history of technical progress is largely a history of rising conversion efficiencies, from open fires that wasted more than nine-tenths of their wood to combined-cycle gas turbines converting around 60% of fuel to electricity. Second, Smil explicitly disavows the energy determinism of his predecessors. Wilhelm Ostwald had proposed an “energetic imperative” — waste no energy — as the law of cultural progress, and the anthropologist Leslie White made energy capture per capita the very measure of cultural evolution. Smil finds these framings useful but ultimately false as explanations: energy levels constrain what societies can do, but they do not dictate what societies will do, value, believe, or create. That tension — energy as universal substrate, energy as poor predictor of meaning — is announced here and resolved only in the final chapter.

Prime mover

Any converter that turns a primary energy source into mechanical work: human muscle, draft animals, water wheels, windmills, steam engines, internal combustion engines, turbines. Smil periodizes all of history by which prime movers dominate and how many watts each delivers.

The chapter closes with the book’s organizing arc in miniature. For most of human existence the only prime mover was somatic — human muscle fed by food. Domestication added animal muscle; antiquity and the Middle Ages added the first inanimate prime movers, water and wind; the nineteenth century added engines fed by fossil fuels; the twentieth added turbines, motors, and the wholly new currency of electricity. Each step raised the power under unit human command by roughly an order of magnitude, and each step rearranged settlement, work, diet, warfare, and the size of the social units that could cohere.

Chapter 2

Energy in Prehistory

What did the human energy economy look like before farming — and what conversions made us human in the first place? Smil starts with the body. Bipedal walking is an unusually economical gait, and humans are exceptional sweaters, able to dump metabolic heat fast enough to run down faster animals over long distances in persistence hunts. The decisive somatic fact, though, is the brain: about 2% of body mass consuming on the order of 20% of resting metabolism. Such an organ could only be paid for by foods of higher energy density and digestibility, which is why the trajectory toward larger brains is also a trajectory toward meat, marrow, tubers — and fire.

Foraging, analyzed as an energy system, was governed by net returns: kilojoules of food acquired per kilojoule of effort spent. The arithmetic explains foraging strategies remarkably well. Gathering plant staples could return on the order of 10–40 units of food energy per unit of labor; hunting small game often returned much less and was sometimes a net loss; but a successful kill of a large herbivore — a single bison or mammoth yielding hundreds of kilograms of fat-rich meat — offered the highest payoffs available to any preagricultural people. Hence the archaeological prominence of large-animal hunting, the prestige attached to it, and plausibly a contribution to the late-Pleistocene extinction of megafauna, though Smil treats the “overkill” hypothesis with his usual caution about monocausal stories. The same arithmetic explains the hard ceiling on foraging societies: typical population densities ran from roughly 0.01 to 1 person per square kilometer depending on environment, with rich exceptions — the salmon-harvesting peoples of the Pacific Northwest — proving the rule that density tracked the energy density of accessible food.

Tools, in this accounting, are energy concentrators. A wooden thrusting spear delivers only the power of an arm at the moment of contact; the atlatl, or spear-thrower, lengthens the lever and multiplies projectile speed; the bow — perhaps the first device to store muscular work as elastic energy and release it in a fraction of a second — turns a slow draw of perhaps a hundred watts into an arrow leaving at kilowatt-scale instantaneous power. Smil treats this as the template for all later weaponry: the military problem has never been the quantity of energy but the rate and precision of its release. The same logic governed nonlethal tools — digging sticks, grindstones, fish weirs — each raising the net energy return of some food pathway and thereby reshaping where and how bands could live through the seasons.

The mastery of fire is, for Smil, the first great extrasomatic energy conversion — the first time a species deliberately controlled an energy flow outside its own body. The earliest persuasive evidence (ash and burned bone at Wonderwerk Cave in South Africa) is roughly a million years old; regular, habitual use of hearths is clearly established by around 400,000–300,000 years ago. Fire delivered warmth, protection, light that extended the social day, and landscape management through burning — but its greatest dividend was cooking. Here Smil draws on Richard Wrangham’s argument: cooking gelatinizes starches and denatures proteins, sharply raising the share of food energy that digestion can actually capture, and it effectively outsources part of digestion to the hearth. Smaller guts, smaller teeth, and a larger brain become energetically affordable; the hearth becomes the first social institution. An open wood fire is a miserable converter — well under 10% of the wood’s energy reaches the food or the bodies around it — but the energy was gathered, not grown, and the gain in usable nutrition was transformative.

Why, then, did foragers ever farm? Early agriculture meant more work, worse nutrition, shorter stature, and new diseases — skeletal evidence makes this clear, and Smil does not romanticize the transition. His answer is energetic and demographic rather than aspirational: farming yields far less per hour but vastly more per hectare. Under post-glacial population growth and climatic stabilization, the societies that cultivated could feed ten to a hundred times more people from the same land, and once a population had grown on cultivated yields there was no path back. Agriculture was, in this telling, less an invention than a ratchet — the first of several one-way doors in the book.

Chapter 3

Traditional Farming

The longest chapter of the book asks how societies fed themselves for the ten millennia between the first cultivation and the fossil era — and why that mode of life was simultaneously a trap and a ratchet. Traditional farming, Smil insists, was everywhere the same kind of system underneath its regional variety: a managed conversion of solar radiation into edible carbohydrates, powered almost entirely by human and animal muscle, fertilized by recycled organic wastes, and limited above all by nitrogen. Its defining ratio was favorable in energy terms — staple grain harvests commonly returned on the order of ten to fifty units of food energy per unit of human labor energy invested — but the labor itself was crushing and the surpluses thin.

Smil works through the great regional solutions with his characteristic bookkeeping. Egyptian farming rode the Nile’s annual flood, which delivered both water and fertile silt at no muscular cost; this subsidy made Egypt antiquity’s great granary and let a state skim enough surplus to build monuments. (In one of the book’s set-piece calculations, Smil shows that the Great Pyramid’s roughly 2.5 million blocks could have been quarried, hauled, and set by a labor force on the order of 20,000–25,000 workers over two decades — no aliens, no lost technologies, just organized muscle, ramps, levers, and bread.) Chinese intensive farming went the other way: ever more labor per hectare, with irrigation, terracing, double-cropping of wet rice, meticulous recycling of every organic residue including human waste, and nitrogen-fixing green manures. Wet-rice cultivation could demand well over a thousand hours of labor per hectare per year, but by the late Qing it could support on the order of five people per hectare of arable land — among the highest sustained densities any solar-powered society ever achieved. Mesoamerica, lacking draft animals entirely, ran agriculture on human muscle alone, yet the chinampas — the raised, lake-mud “floating gardens” of the Basin of Mexico — were among the most productive fields in the preindustrial world, helping sustain Tenochtitlan as one of the largest cities of its era. European farming, long mediocre, was transformed late by rotations: the Norfolk four-course system (wheat, turnips, barley, clover) abolished fallow, fed livestock through winter, and — through clover’s rhizobial nitrogen fixation — roughly doubled the nitrogen flowing through English fields, raising yields and freeing labor in the century before steam.

Draft animals get sustained attention because they were the first great amplification of the human work rate. An ox delivers perhaps 300 watts of sustained draft; a good horse 500–800 watts — several laborers’ worth of pull that does not tire as a man does. But harvesting that power required innovations Smil enumerates like a connoisseur: the collar harness (developed in China and reaching Europe around the ninth and tenth centuries), which moved the load from the choking throat-and-girth strap to the shoulders and multiplied a horse’s effective pull severalfold; iron horseshoes; the whippletree that let teams pull evenly. And animal power carried its own land tax: a working horse needs feed grain, and feeding it claims cropland. This trade-off scaled all the way to the eve of the machine age — when American horses and mules peaked around 1918 at some 26–27 million animals, their feed claimed roughly a quarter of U.S. farmland.

The farming trap (and ratchet)

Traditional agriculture supported one to two orders of magnitude more people per hectare than foraging, but at the cost of harder labor, poorer diets, and vulnerability to harvest failure — and once population grew to the new ceiling, reversal became impossible. Each intensification (irrigation, rotation, draft animals, manuring) raised the ceiling and re-armed the trap at a higher level.

The chapter’s closing argument explains why this world could improve but never escape itself. Every gain — better rotations, more animals, more irrigation — was a gain within a solar budget fixed by photosynthesis (a conversion of well under 1% efficiency) and by the nitrogen that organic recycling could return to the soil. Traditional societies repeatedly approached their local ceilings, and population pressed against food in the manner Malthus described, not because people were improvident but because the energy system had a hard roof. Smil’s numbers make the stagnation vivid: the best traditional yields of the nineteenth century were perhaps only three to four times those of early dynastic antiquity, an improvement of a few tenths of a percent per year across four millennia. Breaking the roof required energy from outside the annual solar income — which is the story of the next two chapters.

Chapter 4

Preindustrial Prime Movers and Fuels

Before fossil fuels, what was the most power a society could aim at a single task — and what could it burn? Smil organizes the preindustrial world into its four prime movers (human muscle, animal muscle, water, wind) and its one thermal fuel family (biomass: wood, charcoal, crop residues, dung), and the chapter is essentially a watt-by-watt tour of their limits.

Muscle first. A laborer’s sustained 60–100 watts could be aggregated — galley crews, treadwheels, hauling gangs — but aggregation was expensive to feed and miserable to endure; the largest coordinated muscular efforts, like canal digging or pyramid building, represented at most a few hundred kilowatts of sustained power from thousands of bodies. Animals raised the unit: oxen at ~300 W, horses at 500–800 W (the modern unit of one horsepower, 745.7 W, memorializes James Watt’s marketing-minded estimate of a strong dray horse). Whim gins and capstans turned animal walking into rotary power for mills and mine hoists, but feed costs kept animal power scarce everywhere land was scarce.

Water was the first inanimate prime mover, and Smil treats it as antiquity’s one genuine energy revolution. Roman vertical wheels — described by Vitruvius — delivered a few hundred watts to perhaps two kilowatts each; the late-Roman flour-milling complex at Barbegal in southern Gaul ganged sixteen wheels into what may have been the largest power installation of the ancient world, on the order of 30 kW. Medieval Europe adopted the technology with extraordinary enthusiasm: the Domesday Book of 1086 recorded 5,624 water mills in England south of the Trent — roughly one for every 350 people — grinding grain, fulling cloth, sawing wood, crushing ore, working bellows and trip-hammers. By the eighteenth century, after John Smeaton’s systematic experiments showed overshot wheels to be about twice as efficient as undershot ones, the best iron wheels delivered tens of kilowatts; the giant Lady Isabella wheel at Laxey (1854) — over 21 meters in diameter — could exceed 200 kW. Water power, not steam, drove much of early industrialization, and Smil notes pointedly that many “Industrial Revolution” mills were water mills for decades.

Wind was less reliable but reached where water did not. European post mills appear in the late twelfth century; the Dutch made windmills a civilizational tool, draining polders and powering the sawmills, oil presses, and paper mills of the Golden Age — a typical mill delivering useful power in the low single kilowatts, the best later smock mills perhaps ten. The supreme wind machine, though, was the sailing ship: the one preindustrial technology that aggregated tens or hundreds of kilowatts of free, fuel-less power and applied it to transport. Square rigs, lateen sails, the sternpost rudder, and the magnetic compass cumulatively turned wind into the carrier of world trade, migration, and empire — Smil reminds the reader that Europe’s global expansion was a wind-and-gunpowder phenomenon long before it was a steam one.

Household energy in this world was overwhelmingly thermal, and overwhelmingly wasted. Cooking and heating ran on wood where forests allowed, and elsewhere on the leavings of agriculture — crop residues and dried dung, fuels of low energy density burned in fires and stoves that delivered perhaps a tenth of their heat to pot or room. Lighting was scarcer still: tallow candles and oil lamps converted on the order of 0.01% of their fuel’s chemical energy into visible light, which is why preindustrial nights were genuinely dark and why artificial light remained a luxury good for millennia. Smil likes this example because it bounds the whole era from below: the most universal daily energy services — a warm room, a cooked meal, a lit page — were exactly the ones served worst.

Industrial heat was scarcer and costlier. Air-dried wood holds roughly 15 MJ/kg; charcoal, at nearly 30 MJ/kg, was the premium fuel — smokeless, hot, and essential for smelting — but making it wasted most of the wood’s energy, and iron production devoured forests: a single eighteenth-century furnace could claim the annual growth of thousands of hectares of woodland. Deforestation around mines, cities, and smelters was the characteristic energy crisis of advanced organic economies, and it is no accident that the society that first ran short of accessible wood — England — was the first to dig seriously into the ground beneath it. The chapter ends on Smil’s favorite kind of irony: the preindustrial world’s prime movers had improved for two thousand years, yet on the eve of 1700 the totality of human, animal, water, and wind power in the richest countries amounted to per-capita useful energy only modestly above antiquity’s. The roof was solar, and only a subterranean store of fossilized sunlight could break it.

Chapter 5

Fossil Fuels, Primary Electricity, and Renewables

How did humanity move from annual solar income to the planet’s accumulated solar savings — and why did that shift take centuries rather than decades? Smil begins with coal, and with England, which was burning Newcastle “sea coal” in London hearths by the sixteenth century and mining millions of tonnes a year by 1700 — most of the world’s output. Coal’s virtues were energy density (good bituminous coal at 22–29 MJ/kg, half again better than wood) and, decisively, the fact that it was mined from points rather than grown over areas: extraction at high power density freed energy supply from the land constraint that strangled organic economies. Two bottlenecks had to break. Abraham Darby’s coke smelting at Coalbrookdale (1709) freed iron from charcoal and forests; and the steam engine freed mechanical power from muscle, water, and wind.

Smil narrates the steam engine as an efficiency staircase. Thomas Newcomen’s 1712 atmospheric engine delivered about 3.75 kW at roughly 0.5% efficiency — so wasteful it made sense only at coal mines, where fuel was nearly free; its job, fittingly, was pumping water out of coal mines, fossil energy bootstrapping its own extraction. James Watt’s separate condenser (patented 1769) and later improvements roughly quintupled efficiency and made steam economic away from the pithead; by 1800 Boulton & Watt engines averaged around 20 kW — a hundred laborers in a box. Through the nineteenth century, higher pressures, compounding, and triple-expansion designs pushed efficiencies past 10% and unit powers past a megawatt, while steam locomotives (Stephenson’s Rocket, 1829) and steamships annihilated preindustrial constraints of distance. By 1900 the largest stationary steam engines — like the ~3 MW giants ordered for New York’s subway power station — had reached the practical limits of reciprocating machinery, just as their successor arrived: Charles Parsons’s steam turbine (1884, first unit a mere 7.5 kW) scaled within decades to tens of megawatts and eventually beyond a gigawatt, becoming — as it remains — the workhorse converter of world electricity.

Crude oil enters as the second fossil epoch. Edwin Drake’s 21-meter well at Titusville, Pennsylvania (1859) founded an industry that first sold kerosene for lamps; the gusher at Spindletop, Texas (1901) and the discovery of the supergiant fields of the Persian Gulf (Ghawar, found 1948, the largest of all) made oil the cheapest portable energy in history. Oil’s advantages compound: energy density of 42–44 MJ/kg, liquid handling, ease of storage and transport by pipeline and tanker. Its perfect partner was the internal combustion engine — Otto’s four-stroke (1876), Benz and Daimler’s vehicles (1886), Diesel’s high-compression engine (1890s) — a prime mover with an unprecedented power-to-weight ratio. Smil lingers on that ratio because it created flight: the Wright Flyer of 1903 was possible only because a gasoline engine could deliver about 9 kW from under 100 kg, something no steam plant could approach. The gas turbine (Whittle and von Ohain, late 1930s) extended the same logic to jet aviation, the fastest prime mover ever applied to mass transport.

Natural gas completes the fossil trio, and Smil treats it as the most underrated of the three. Long flared as a nuisance at oil wells, methane became a major fuel only when seamless steel pipe and welded long-distance pipelines (an interwar and postwar achievement) made it transportable; liquefied natural gas tankers later made it global. Its merits are exceptional: the highest energy density per unit mass of any fossil fuel (about 53 MJ/kg), the cleanest combustion, the lowest carbon emissions per joule, and a uniquely efficient converter in the combined-cycle gas turbine, which pairs a jet-engine-derived gas turbine with a steam turbine running on its exhaust heat to reach efficiencies around 60% — the best large-scale heat-to-electricity conversion ever achieved. Gas is also feedstock: the hydrogen for ammonia synthesis comes overwhelmingly from methane, tying the world’s dinner tables to its gas fields. The slow, late rise of gas — still short of a quarter of world primary energy in the 2010s, eight decades after long-distance pipelines — is one of Smil’s favorite exhibits for the slowness of transitions even when the newcomer is superior on nearly every count.

The chapter’s emotional center, though, is electricity — in Smil’s judgment the most important systemic innovation in energy history, because it is not a fuel but a perfectly flexible, perfectly clean-at-the-point-of-use form of energy convertible to light, motion, heat, and information at efficiencies muscle and flame never approached. He repeatedly singles out the 1880s as perhaps the most inventive decade in history: Edison’s practical incandescent lamp (1879–80) and the Pearl Street generating station in Manhattan (September 1882, the first commercial system of generation, distribution, and metered sale); the practical transformer (1885); Tesla’s induction motor and the AC polyphase system (1888) that, after the brief “war of the currents,” enabled transmission over distance; Parsons’s turbine (1884) to generate it all. Light is his favorite measure of what followed: from the candle’s 0.01% conversion of fuel to light, through Edison’s carbon-filament bulbs at well under 1%, to fluorescent tubes and now LEDs above 10% — a gain of three orders of magnitude that turned the oldest luxury into the cheapest commodity, so that an hour of reading light costs the modern worker a fraction of a second of labor where it once cost hours. Hydroelectricity — the modern career of the water wheel — arrived in the same wave, with Niagara Falls (1895) as its showcase. Electric motors then quietly revolutionized manufacturing: replacing the central steam engine and its forest of belts and shafts with a fractional-horsepower motor on every machine reorganized the factory floor itself, a diffuse productivity gain Smil rates above most celebrated inventions.

Power density

Watts harvested or consumed per square meter of land. Biomass delivers well under 1 W/m²; solar farms on the order of 5–10; fossil fuel extraction 10²–10⁴. Cities and industry consume at 10–100+ W/m². Smil’s rule: civilization’s spatial structure reflects the gap between the density at which energy is produced and the density at which it is used — and shrinking that gap is the core difficulty of any return to renewable flows.

Renewables close the chapter in their modern guise — hydro dams, wind turbines, photovoltaics — and Smil is characteristically measured: these are real and growing conversions, but they harvest diffuse flows at low power densities, and (writing in the mid-2010s) modern wind and solar still supplied only a few percent of global primary energy. The fossil transition itself, he stresses, took generations: coal needed roughly sixty years to go from 5% to 50% of world primary energy; oil, half a century to go from 5% to a quarter. Anyone forecasting a faster exit from fossil carbon, he warns, is forecasting against every transition on record.

Chapter 6

Fossil-Fueled Civilization

What kind of society did this million-fold expansion of harnessed energy actually build? Smil’s answer in this chapter is: yours — in every load-bearing detail. He begins with the brute totals. Global primary energy use rose roughly sixteen-fold in the twentieth century while population quadrupled; an average inhabitant of a rich country now commands a continuous flow of several kilowatts — for an American, on the order of 10 kW of primary energy, the sustained muscular output of well over a hundred adult laborers working around the clock. No monarch of the solar era, with all his servants and horses, controlled household energy flows like an ordinary suburban family.

Food comes first, because modern agriculture is, in Smil’s blunt accounting, a system for turning fossil fuels into food. The single most consequential conversion is the Haber–Bosch synthesis of ammonia: Fritz Haber demonstrated catalytic high-pressure synthesis in 1909, Carl Bosch industrialized it for BASF (the Oppau plant opened in 1913), and synthetic nitrogen broke the constraint that had capped every traditional agriculture in history. Smil’s famous estimate: the dietary protein of roughly 40–45% of humanity — comfortably more than three billion people — now depends on nitrogen fixed in ammonia plants; without it, the present world population simply could not be fed. Around this core sit the other fossil subsidies: fuels for tractors and irrigation pumps, energy-intensive pesticides, grain drying, and worldwide transport. The result inverts the old energy ledger — a modern field returns less edible energy than the total energy invested in producing it, a ratio that would have starved any traditional society but is trivially affordable when energy is cheap — while labor productivity explodes: where a hectare of American wheat once demanded weeks of human and animal work, mechanized farming delivers it in an hour or two of machine-assisted labor, releasing the populations of entire countrysides into cities.

Cities are the second creation. Urbanization beyond a small parasitic minority was impossible in solar societies, which needed most hands on the land; fossil-subsidized farming and fossil-powered transport made it general, and in 2007 humanity passed the point where more than half of us live in cities. Megacities are extreme power-density phenomena — consuming tens of watts per square meter, fed by energy gathered from vast distances — and everything characteristic of them, from elevators (electric motors making the skyscraper habitable) to nighttime light to refrigerated food chains, is an energy artifact. Transport tells the same story as speed and cost: from the horse’s 10–15 km/h and the sailing ship’s passage measured in months to railways above 100 km/h, jetliners near 900 km/h, and — perhaps more important — ocean freight so cheap, especially after the container revolution of the 1960s, that distance nearly vanished from the cost of goods, creating the global economy as a thermodynamic fact before it was a policy.

Then come materials — a signature Smil theme. Modern civilization rests physically on a handful of energy-embodying substances produced at gigantic scale: steel (smelted with coke and electricity, roughly 20–30 MJ embodied in every kilogram, and still the skeleton of every machine, vehicle, bridge, and tower); cement (the binder of concrete, modest per kilogram at ~4 MJ but produced in billions of tonnes — China poured more cement in recent three-year spans than the United States used in the entire twentieth century); ammonia (the food molecule, above); and plastics (synthesized from fossil hydrocarbons that serve as both feedstock and fuel, at upwards of 80–100 MJ/kg). None of these can yet be made at scale without enormous fossil energy inputs, which is why Smil insists that decarbonization is a materials problem, not merely an electricity problem. Even the information economy sits on this substrate: microchips are among the most energy-intensive artifacts per gram ever manufactured, and the digital world’s apparent weightlessness is an accounting illusion.

The chapter does not omit the debit column. Fossil-fueled civilization brought the energetics of modern war — and a new order of destructiveness. Explosives matter less for their energy content than for their power: TNT holds only 4.6 MJ/kg, a tenth of gasoline, but releases it in microseconds; fossil-fueled industry could manufacture such compounds, and the engines to deliver them, without limit. The two world wars were, energetically, the first conflicts in which whole economies were converted into machines for projecting destructive power — culminating in nuclear weapons, where the Hiroshima bomb released about 63 terajoules from 64 kilograms of uranium in an instant. And the same combustion that feeds and moves the modern world loads the atmosphere with CO₂, the slow externality that Smil — who spent decades on biospheric flows before writing energy histories — treats as the central long-term consequence of the fossil era. High-energy civilization, he concludes, is an extraordinary achievement resting on a temporary subsidy, and its core habit — treating energy as too cheap to meter morally — is the habit it will find hardest to break.

Chapter 7

Energy in World History

The final chapter steps back and asks the synthetic questions: what is the grand pattern, what does energy explain about history — and, just as carefully, what does it not explain? The pattern itself Smil states crisply. Human history is a sequence of energy eras defined by dominant fuels and prime movers — muscle and biomass; the partial augmentations of water and wind; coal and steam; oil, gas, electricity, and turbines — and each era is marked by rising per-capita energy use, rising unit power of the largest prime movers (from the 100-watt laborer to the gigawatt turbine, ten million-fold in ten millennia, with most of the rise in the last two centuries), rising efficiency of conversions, and rising power density of supply. Annual per-capita energy use climbed from perhaps 5–10 GJ in foraging societies through several tens of gigajoules in advanced agrarian empires to around 150 GJ in Western Europe and 300 GJ in the United States today.

Energy transition

The shift in a society’s primary energy supply from one dominant source to another. Smil’s empirical law: transitions are inherently gradual, governed by infrastructure, capital stock, and scale — coal took ~60 years to go from 5% to 50% of world supply; oil ~50 years from 5% to 25%; natural gas slower still. The larger the system, the slower the substitution.

From this pattern Smil extracts his signature lessons. First, transitions are slow — the point he has made his career’s drumbeat. New prime movers take half a century or more to dominate even after they are clearly superior, because energy systems are embodied in long-lived capital: mines, wells, grids, pipelines, fleets, furnaces, and cities built to one fuel’s logic. Sailing ships carried meaningful trade into the twentieth century, sixty years after efficient steamers; steam locomotives ran decades into the diesel era; and the world of 2017 still drew roughly 85% of its primary energy from fossil carbon, a share not radically different from 1990’s. Second, there is no determinism of timing from invention: every fundamental technique of the fossil age — coke smelting, the steam engine, the dynamo — diffused over generations, and forecasting energy futures by extrapolating enthusiasm has failed so reliably that Smil devotes some of his driest scorn to the history of confident predictions, from imminent oil exhaustion to electricity too cheap to meter. He pointedly declines to forecast; the future of energy, he says, is open in detail even if constrained in physics.

A subtler regularity is the falling energy intensity of economies. Energy use and economic product grew together for two centuries, but not in lockstep: the energy needed to produce a unit of GDP has declined steadily in every maturing economy as conversions improve and output shifts from smelting and hauling toward services and information. Britain’s economy of 1900 needed several times more energy per unit of output than its economy a century later; the United States, China, and every other industrializer has traced the same curve from its own peak. Smil draws two lessons. The encouraging one: growth and energy can partially decouple, and efficiency is the largest, cheapest “source” of energy modern societies possess. The cautionary one: relative decoupling has never yet become absolute on the global scale — falling intensity has been a story of doing more with each joule while still using more joules in total, with efficiency gains partly eaten by rebound, larger vehicles, bigger houses, and longer supply chains.

Third, energy explains less than energeticists hoped. Here Smil returns to settle the account opened in chapter 1. War: higher energy use does not reliably produce victory — he notes that the world’s highest-energy society fought in Vietnam, against an opponent with a tiny fraction of its energy throughput, and lost; conversely the energy-poor Mongols built history’s largest land empire on grass-fed horsepower. Values: slavery flourished in low-energy Rome and in the high-energy American South; both democracies and totalitarian states have run on identical fuels. Culture: the timeless achievements of art and thought show no correlation with energy consumption — the Athens of Sophocles and the Florence of Michelangelo ran on muscle, wood, and sails. And welfare itself saturates: the indicators that matter most — life expectancy, infant mortality, nutrition, education — improve steeply with energy use up to roughly 100–120 GJ per capita and then flatten, so that the United States, consuming about twice as much energy per person as France or Japan, buys no additional longevity or measured happiness with the difference. The corollary is the closest Smil comes to advocacy: the rich world’s problem is no longer how to get more energy but how to want less — to pursue deliberate moderation of energy throughput rather than its maximization, both because the marginal returns have vanished and because the biospheric bill for fossil combustion is still being written.

The book closes where it opened, holding both truths at once. Energy is the universal currency; no history that ignores it can be right. But a currency is not a purpose. What civilizations have done with their watts — fed children or built bombs, raised cathedrals or strip malls — has always been decided by things no joule accounting can capture. The grand pattern of history is ever more energy, converted ever more efficiently, at ever higher power; the meaning of history is not in the pattern.

Reading it critically

Three lines of criticism recur in published responses. First, reviewers note that Smil’s anti-determinist disclaimers sit awkwardly atop a book whose every chapter is organized by energy — he denies that energy explains history while structuring history entirely around it, and critics on both sides (determinists and culturalists) find the balance unstable. Second, his transitions-are-slow law, drawn from coal and oil, has been challenged by the post-2017 trajectory of solar photovoltaics, wind, and batteries, whose cost declines and adoption curves have outrun his cautious mid-2010s figures; defenders reply that even record-fast renewable growth has so far displaced shares, not absolute fossil volumes, much as Smil predicted. Third, historians complain that the book is far stronger on quantities than on people — institutions, prices, and politics (why England, and not coal-rich China, industrialized first) get thinner treatment than joules do, a gap explored more fully by writers like E. A. Wrigley and Kenneth Pomeranz. None of these dent the book’s factual core, which remains the standard quantitative reference for energy in history.

If you remember ten things

  1. Energy is the only universal currency: nothing happens in nature or history without an energy conversion, and societies are usefully periodized by their dominant fuels and prime movers.
  2. The human baseline is small — a laborer sustains 60–100 watts — and most of history is the story of multiplying that number: ox (~300 W), horse (500–800 W), water wheel (kilowatts), steam engine (tens of kilowatts to megawatts), turbine (gigawatts).
  3. Fire and cooking were the first extrasomatic energy conversion, and by raising the digestible energy of food they helped pay for the human brain.
  4. Farming was an energy ratchet: far less food per hour than foraging but ten to a hundred times more per hectare, so once populations grew there was no way back.
  5. Every traditional agriculture was capped by photosynthesis and nitrogen; the best solar-powered farming (late Qing China) fed about five people per hectare, and four millennia of effort raised yields only a few-fold.
  6. Coal broke the land constraint because fossil fuels are extracted at high power density from points, not grown at low power density over areas — and power density remains Smil’s key variable for judging any energy system.
  7. Electricity, born in the astonishing 1880s (Edison, Tesla, Parsons, the transformer), is the most flexible and consequential form of energy ever deployed, even though it is a carrier rather than a fuel.
  8. Modern food is fossil fuel transformed: Haber–Bosch ammonia (1913) feeds roughly 40% of humanity, and modern fields consume more energy than they yield — affordable only because fossil energy is cheap.
  9. Civilization rests materially on steel, cement, ammonia, and plastics, none yet producible at scale without enormous fossil inputs — so decarbonization is a materials problem, not just an electricity problem.
  10. Energy transitions take generations (coal ~60 years from 5% to 50% of supply; oil ~50 years to 25%), forecasts reliably fail, and beyond ~100 GJ per person more energy buys no additional well-being — energy constrains history, but it does not give it meaning.

Concepts to keep

Energy as the universal currency

Every activity — biological, economic, computational — is an energy conversion, and money is just a noisy proxy for the underlying flows. This is the accounting identity that lets Smil put foragers, farmers, and factories on one ledger.

Prime movers

The devices that convert energy into useful motion: muscles, waterwheels, steam engines, turbines, motors. History’s great discontinuities track the arrival of new prime movers, because they determine the maximum power a society can apply to any task.

Energy density

Joules per kilogram of fuel. Wood is poor, coal better, oil superb — which is why oil conquered transport and why batteries, two orders of magnitude weaker, still struggle to. Energy density explains more of technological history than ingenuity does.

Power density

Watts per square meter of land. Fossil extraction delivers thousands; photosynthesis and most renewables deliver single digits. Modern civilization was built consuming energy at high power densities and producing it from compact sources — reversing that mismatch is the hidden difficulty of the renewable transition.

Energy transitions are slow

Coal needed generations to displace wood; oil needed generations to displace coal; each new source had to grow inside an enormous incumbent system. Smil’s most-cited warning: anyone promising a fast civilizational energy transition is arguing against the entire historical record.

Net energy returns

What matters is not gross energy captured but the surplus left after subtracting the energy spent capturing it. Farming persisted because it returned more food calories than the muscle it consumed; societies live on their energetic surplus, and the size of that surplus dictates how many people can do something other than produce energy.

Energy bounds, culture decides

Smil’s anti-determinist guardrail: energy availability defines the option space, never the choice. Similar energetic foundations have supported radically different societies — so energy history is necessary context for everything and a sufficient explanation for nothing.

“Energy is the only universal currency: one of its many forms must be transformed to get anything done.” — Vaclav Smil, opening of Chapter 1, “Energy and Society”
“To talk about energy and the economy is a tautology: every economic activity is fundamentally nothing but a conversion of one kind of energy to another, and monies are just a convenient (and often rather unrepresentative) proxy for valuing the energy flows.” — Vaclav Smil, Energy and Civilization
Smil’s recurring refrain, stated in many forms across the book: all great energy transitions have been gradual, protracted affairs spanning generations — and there is no evidence the one now beginning will be any faster. — close paraphrase of a theme running through Chapters 5 and 7

Threads to other books

Chip War — Chris Miller
Miller’s semiconductors are fossil-fueled civilization’s most refined artifacts — devices whose fabrication concentrates more embodied energy and precision per gram than anything else humans make, and whose geography Smil’s power-density lens helps explain.
Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital — Carlota Perez
Perez’s five great surges each ride on an energy substrate Smil documents — coal under railways, oil under mass production — so her financial cycles are, in a sense, the market’s delayed reaction to his energy transitions.
Machines of Loving Grace — Dario Amodei
Amodei’s “compressed century” of AI-driven progress runs head-on into Smil’s slowest variable: intelligence may scale exponentially, but the gigawatts, grids, and materials it needs are built at the stubborn pace of physical infrastructure.
Seeing Like a State — James C. Scott
Both books are correctives to schemes that ignore ground truth: Scott shows plans failing against local knowledge, Smil shows them failing against thermodynamics — two different ways reality refuses to be simplified.
For the AI researcher

Read this book as the physical-layer documentation for your field. The AI buildout of the mid-2020s is, in Smil’s terms, a power-density event without precedent in computing: individual training campuses now demand a gigawatt or more — the output of a large nuclear reactor concentrated on a few hundred acres — and U.S. datacenter demand has gone from a rounding error to one of the main forces bending national electricity forecasts upward for the first time in two decades. The binding constraint on frontier AI through the late 2020s is increasingly not algorithms or even chips but interconnection queues, transformers, turbines, and transmission lines — exactly the slow, lumpy infrastructure whose timescales Smil spent a career measuring.

His framework also sharpens the awkward questions. Energy transitions take decades, but AI demand is materializing in quarters — so the gap is being filled by whatever can be built fast, which in practice means natural gas turbines and life-extended coal plants alongside the solar, batteries, and (later) new nuclear that everyone would prefer. A Smilian analysis of your own work asks: what is the power density of this datacenter, where do its joules originate, and what is the net return — does the intelligence produced per megawatt-hour justify the draw, and who is doing that accounting?

Finally, take the asterisk seriously. Smil would not accept that abundant intelligence automatically yields abundant flourishing, any more than abundant energy did; his data show the marginal joule decoupling from welfare long ago. The honest research question his book leaves you with is whether the marginal FLOP will follow the same curve — and whether your field will notice if it does.
