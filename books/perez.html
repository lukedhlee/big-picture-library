Cycles of Technology & Finance

Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital

Carlota Perez · 2002
~7 hours to read Condensed read below: ~40 min read A grammar for bubbles and golden ages Pairs with: The Power Law

If you have ever wondered whether the AI boom is a bubble — and whether that would even be a bad thing — Perez wrote the answer two decades early. Her slim, schematic book argues that the bubble is not a malfunction of technological revolutions but a recurring phase of them, as structural as adolescence.

Carlota Perez is a Venezuelan-British scholar of technology and development, trained in the neo-Schumpeterian tradition and shaped by an unusual career arc: she spent years inside Venezuela’s ministry of industry watching the oil-and-mass-production paradigm exhaust itself, then turned that civil servant’s vantage into theory. Her 1983 paper on the mismatch between new technologies and old institutions made her name; this 2002 book — published, with impeccable timing, into the smoking crater of the dot-com crash — made her framework famous.

The question that provoked it was the crash itself. The conventional readings of 2001 were either moralistic (greed and madness) or dismissive (the internet was hype all along). Perez offered a third reading: this exact sequence — revolutionary technology, speculative mania, crash, then decades of widely shared prosperity built on the very infrastructure the mania overbuilt — had already happened four times since 1771. The dot-com bust was not the end of the information age; by historical analogy it was roughly the midpoint, the hinge between installing a technology and actually deploying it.

The book’s influence has been strongest in an unexpected quarter: venture capital. Marc Andreessen, Fred Wilson, and a generation of technology investors adopted Perez as a kind of house cosmology, finding in her phases a defense of the proposition that bubbles, for all their carnage, are how capitalism finances things too uncertain for sober money. Whether that adoption flatters or distorts her argument is a question worth holding onto as you read.

The core argument

Capitalism advances through great surges of development, each driven by a technological revolution, and each surge passes through the same two-act structure. In the installation period, financial capital takes command, funds the new technology indiscriminately, and inflates a bubble that crashes. After a turning point of recession and institutional reform, the deployment period follows: production capital takes over, the now-cheap infrastructure diffuses across the whole economy, and a “golden age” of broad growth ensues. This sequence has recurred roughly every half century since 1771.

The engine of the model is a division of labor between two kinds of money. Financial capital is mobile, impatient, and uncommitted — it seeks returns wherever they appear and abandons positions freely. Production capital is embodied in firms, factories, and accumulated know-how — patient, specific, and tied to particular technologies. Most of the time they work in tandem; at the start of a technological revolution they decouple. The new technology offers explosive returns that old industries cannot match, so financial capital floods toward it, bidding paper values far beyond any reasonable forecast of profits.

Perez’s heterodox move is to insist this frenzy is functional. Canal mania, railway mania, and the dot-com bubble all destroyed fortunes — and all left behind canals, railways, and fiber-optic networks priced at cents on the dollar, plus a trained workforce and a population converted to the new paradigm’s common sense. The bubble is how a society overpays, collectively and chaotically, for infrastructure no rational actor would fund alone.

But the gains of installation are narrow and the inequities glaring, and the crash exposes both. The turning point is therefore political as much as economic: institutions must be rebuilt to match the new paradigm — think of the 1930s reforms and postwar Bretton Woods order that turned mass-production technology into mass consumption and suburban prosperity. Where that institutional recomposition succeeds, deployment delivers its golden age; where it fails or stalls, the surge’s potential is squandered. The framework’s sobering implication: technology supplies the possibility of broad prosperity, but only governance converts it.

Perez wrote this book, by her own description, as a “think-piece”: a stylized model of why capitalism advances in half-century surges, each launched by a technological revolution, inflated by a financial bubble, broken by a crash, and redeemed — sometimes — by a golden age. Part I builds the technological half of the model; Part II re-runs the sequence from the standpoint of financial capital, phase by phase; Part III summarizes the driving forces and the implications. This condensation follows her fifteen chapters in order, merging the two closing chapters with the 2002 epilogue, and keeps her framework vocabulary intact. The bibliography and some repeated illustrations are omitted; the historical cases — canal mania, railway mania, 1929, the dot-com collapse — are kept, because in this book they do the proving.

Part I · Chapter 1

The Turbulent Ending of the Twentieth Century

The book opens with a date: on an ordinary day in November 1971, in Santa Clara, California, Bob Noyce and Gordon Moore launched Intel’s first microprocessor — “the big-bang of a new universe, that of all-pervasive computing and digital telecommunications.” At the time few people had heard of venture capital or “angels”; the word “derivative” still belonged mostly to mathematics. Within three decades, finance had become the central concern of people with old and new wealth, and even modest salary-earners had turned into hopeful “investors.”

Perez’s claim is that this had all happened before, four times. Henry Ford’s low-cost Model-T in 1908 played the same role for the age of the automobile and mass production; by the mid-1920s the New York stock market was perceived as the engine of the world economy, and by decade’s end even widows, small farmers and shoe-shine boys were putting their money into what she calls a glorified casino. A decade after Arkwright’s mill opened the world of mechanization in England there was canal mania, then canal panic. About fifteen years after the Liverpool–Manchester railway inaugurated the Age of Steam there was railway mania, ending in the panic of 1847. After Carnegie’s Bessemer plant of 1875, stock markets boomed through the 1880s and 1890s — now internationally — and the crashes came in the USA and Argentina, Italy and France.

Each time, a “new economy” takes root where the old was faltering, but in a violent, wasteful and painful manner. The installation decades are when capitalism shows what she calls its ugliest and most callous face — the time depicted by Dickens and Engels, Sinclair and Veblen, when the rich get richer with arrogance and the poor get poorer through no fault of their own. After the financial breakdown, if institutional adjustment succeeds, a golden age can follow: full employment, production at center stage, financial capital receding from public view into boardrooms. The sequence technological revolution–financial bubble–collapse–golden age–political unrest recurs about every half century, she argues, because of three features of the system: technical change happens by clusters of radical innovations forming distinct revolutions; financial and production capital are functionally separate agents pursuing profit by different means; and the socio-institutional framework has far greater inertia than the competitively driven techno-economic sphere.

Part I · Chapter 2

Technological Revolutions and Techno-Economic Paradigms

A technological revolution is a powerful, highly visible cluster of new technologies, products and industries capable of upheaving the whole fabric of the economy — a constellation that generally includes an all-pervasive low-cost input (often energy or a material), major new products, and a new infrastructure that changes the frontier in speed and cost of transport and communication. What earns the name “revolution” is not just the new industries: each cluster supplies generic technologies and organizational principles allowing a quantum jump in productivity for practically all economic activities, regenerating the whole productive system every fifty years or so.

Perez counts five such revolutions, each dated from a symbolic, imagination-firing “big-bang” event that announced a new universe of cheap possibility: the Industrial Revolution, from Arkwright’s mill opening in Cromford, Britain, 1771 (mechanized cotton, wrought iron, machinery; canals and turnpikes); the Age of Steam and Railways, from the test of Stephenson’s “Rocket” for the Liverpool–Manchester railway, 1829 (steam engines, iron and coal mining; railways, telegraph, great ports); the Age of Steel, Electricity and Heavy Engineering, from Carnegie’s Bessemer steel plant in Pittsburgh, 1875, with the USA and Germany overtaking Britain (cheap steel, heavy chemistry, electrical equipment; worldwide shipping via Suez, transcontinental railways, worldwide telegraph, electrical networks); the Age of Oil, the Automobile and Mass Production, from the first Model-T out of the Ford plant in Detroit, 1908 (mass-produced autos, cheap oil, petrochemicals, home appliances; highways, universal electricity, analog telecommunications); and the Age of Information and Telecommunications, from the Intel microprocessor announced in Santa Clara, 1971 (cheap microelectronics, computers, software; world digital telecommunications and the Internet).

Each revolution combines truly new industries with old ones redefined — coal and iron, long in use, became motive industries only when the steam engine arrived; oil and the internal-combustion engine matured inside the third surge as a luxury-car business before mass production made them revolutionary; electronics existed from the early 1900s, but only the 1971 microprocessor — “a computer on a chip” — made the potential of cheap microelectronics visible and pulled the related technologies into a coherent cluster. (Biotechnology and nanotechnology, she suggests, were in 2002 where oil and the automobile stood in the 1890s: developing inside the prevailing paradigm, awaiting their own cost breakthrough.)

Techno-economic paradigm

A best-practice model — a set of all-pervasive generic technological and organizational principles representing the most effective way of applying the revolution and modernizing the rest of the economy. Once generally adopted, these principles become the shared “common sense” for organizing any activity. The term extends Dosi’s Kuhnian “technological paradigm” to a meta-paradigm for the whole economy.

Each revolution induces a paradigm shift in this sense, opening what she calls a new design, product and profit space comparable to a gold rush. Her Table 2.3 sketches the successive common senses: factory production, mechanization and time-keeping for the first; economies of agglomeration, “scale as progress,” standard parts for the second; giant steel structures, vertical integration, science as a productive force and universal standardization for the third; mass production for mass markets, oil-based energy intensity, functional hierarchies and centralization for the fourth; and for the fifth, information-intensity, decentralized network structures, knowledge as capital, market segmentation, globalization and instant communication. The paradigm reaches well beyond production — the centralized hierarchical pyramid of the mass-production era was replicated in governments, hospitals, universities, parties and the Soviet system alike, until computers and the Internet made pyramids look rigid and clumsy next to flexible networks. Finally she defines the unit of the whole analysis: a great surge of development is the process by which a revolution and its paradigm propagate across economy and society, from small beginnings in the core country outward toward the peripheries — each surge raising the whole productive structure to a higher plateau of productivity and stretching capitalism further across the planet.

Part I · Chapter 3

The Social Shaping of Technological Revolutions

Why does technical change come by revolutions at all, rather than as a steady stream? Perez deliberately renames Schumpeter’s “long waves” as great surges — measured peak to peak over the life cycle of a single revolution, not trough to trough — and answers with a social mechanism. The full assimilation of a paradigm makes the environment overwhelmingly favorable to compatible innovations and hostile to incompatible ones. Once wired houses existed, dozens of electrical appliances followed naturally — even the electric can-opener was worth designing; once mass production was refined, assembly-line tourism (airplane to bus, bus to hotel) was obvious to conceive. This is the technological equivalent of Kuhn’s “normal science.” But the same conditions form a powerful inclusion–exclusion mechanism: innovations outside the paradigm are rejected, or adapted into minor roles. Railways were first developed to haul coal out of mines, in a world of canals and horses; semiconductors first stretched the radio market by making it portable. Edison thought his phonograph would record dying people’s wills; in the 1950s the head of IBM thought a few computers would cover world demand.

The gates break open when the prevailing paradigm approaches exhaustion. Core industries hit market saturation and diminishing returns to innovation; the mature giants — agents and victims of paradigm closure at once — start funding stretching experiments that often involve radical new technologies: high-pressure engines were tried on textile machinery in the early 1800s; Taylor developed “scientific management” to speed the moving of steel in steel yards; the auto industry trialed automation in the early 1960s. War expenditure is the conspicuous exception to the exclusion mechanism — political and military criteria fund extravagant excursions beyond the paradigm, and when war comes in the maturity phase (the 1960s Space and Arms Race being the notorious case) it can seed the next revolution.

Here financial capital makes its first appearance in the model. Because there is money looking for profit in the hands of non-producers, true outsiders — Carnegie, Bell, Edison, Ford, Noyce, Jobs, Gates, typically young and outside the established firms — can bring their ideas to commercial reality. As low-risk opportunities in the old paradigm dwindle, a growing mass of idle capital becomes willing to take high risks in new directions. Exhaustion thus supplies both the demand for radical entrepreneurship and the idle money to fund its trial and error; the breakthroughs come together into the next revolution; and the visible new paradigm then multiplies the entrepreneurs — the great clusters of talent come forth after the revolution is visible, and because it is visible.

Part I · Chapters 4–5

Installation, Deployment, and the Four Phases of Each Surge

Each great surge divides into two periods of roughly three decades. The installation period is when the new technologies irrupt into a maturing economy and advance “like a bulldozer,” disrupting the established fabric, setting up infrastructure, and battling the power of the old paradigm — a tense coexistence of two paradigms, one declining and one conquering ground. The deployment period is when the whole economy is rewoven by the triumphant paradigm, now normal best practice. Between them lies the turning point — usually a serious recession after the collapse of a financial bubble — when the regulatory and institutional recomposition is made that allows growth to resume on a sustainable path.

Installation is creative destruction with the accent on destruction. Arkwright’s water frame threatened hand spinners in England and India; the Liverpool–Manchester line announced the demise of long-distance coach travel (though, in one of her favorite ironies, horse numbers kept rising for fifty years, ferrying people from stations — a cousin of the never-arriving paperless office); the Suez Canal cut the India run from three months to one, wiping out sailing ships and England’s cargo-depot system; Bessemer steel menaced wrought iron. The economy splits along every seam — new industries versus mature ones, modern firms versus laggards, dynamic regions versus declining ones, new skills versus obsolete ones — and income distribution polarizes. The old bulk of the economy shows perverse price behavior: persistent deflation in the 1870s–80s, runaway inflation in the 1970s–80s, depending on the institutional setting. A Business Week chart she reproduces shows the US economy of 1989–96 visibly decoupling into a booming “high-tech” sector and a stagnant rest.

Chapter 5 splits each period into two phases, giving the four-phase anatomy of the surge. Irruption, the decade or so after the big-bang, is “a time for technology”: explosive growth of the new industries amid stagnation and unemployment in the old, a techno-economic split, and a cleavage in political parties between modernizers and the nostalgic. Frenzy, the late installation phase, is “a time for finance” — the Gilded Age, named for Twain and Warner’s 1873 novel about the money-mad alliance of financiers and politicians: new millionaires at one end, growing exclusion at the other, intense exploration of the paradigm’s possibilities through bold trial-and-error investment, and a gambling economy of asset inflation that ends in bubble. The turning point is not a phase but a process of contextual change — the pendulum swing from frenzied individualism toward collective well-being, when the unsustainable tensions built up during Frenzy are overcome (or not) by institutional recomposition. Synergy, the early deployment phase, is “a time for production”: convergent growth, economies of scale, rising employment, the reign of the middle class — the closest the system comes to a golden age. Maturity is the twilight that “shines with false splendor”: the last products have short life cycles, markets saturate, complacency at the top (“the Great Society syndrome”) confronts piled-up unfulfilled promises below — Luddism and Peterloo in the 1810s, the strikes and May 1968 romanticism at the end of the postwar boom.

Her dating (a working approximation, not a straitjacket): the second surge ran from 1829 through irruption in the 1830s, frenzy in the 1840s, a turning point in 1848–50, Victorian synergy 1850–57 and maturity 1857–73; the third from 1875 through frenzy 1884–93, turning point 1893–95, belle-époque synergy 1895–1907; the fourth from 1908 through the roaring-twenties frenzy 1920–29, a turning point stretching 1929–43 in the USA, postwar synergy 1943–59 and maturity 1960–74; the fifth from 1971, with frenzy from 1987 to 2001 and the turning point beginning in 2000–2001. Phases overlap — the maturity of one surge coincides with the irruption of the next (1908–18; 1971–74). The third surge is the great irregular case: Britain used its financial power to install transcontinental rails, ships and telegraph worldwide while neglecting the new steel, electrical and chemical industries at home; by 1900 the USA and Germany had overtaken her in both, and by 1907 Wall Street could challenge Lombard Street. The whole third surge in Britain had the flavor of a maturity phase — a caution against mechanical application of the model.

Part I · Chapter 6

Uneven Development and Time-Lags in Diffusion

Why don’t the surges show up as clean fifty-year waves in GDP statistics, as Kondratiev expected? Perez argues the long-wave tradition trapped itself with three misleading assumptions: that the phenomenon is endogenous to a narrowly defined economy, that it must appear as regular up- and downswings in aggregates, and that it must be simultaneous worldwide. Her model expects none of this. The action is in the inner workings of the economy: divergence between exploding new branches and declining old ones during installation, resynchronization during deployment; whether the sum reads as a “downswing” depends on shifting relative weights and rates.

Worse, money itself becomes unreliable as a measuring rod. The quantum jump in productivity means that during installation there are effectively “two moneys” operating under the guise of one: money buying electronics and money buying furniture have diverged since the early 1970s, just as steel prices fell through productivity while iron prices were forced down by dying competition. How do you compare a computer of the 1960s with one of the 1990s, or telegraph to India with mail by ship? Constant-money series spanning two or three paradigms are, she says flatly, senseless — which is why the statistical long-wave debate could never resolve.

Geographically, diffusion is sequential and lagged, spreading in ripples from core to periphery — though major crises transmit almost instantly. During synergy, investment concentrates at home; during maturity, the surge moves out to nearer and then farther peripheries. The first surge shows the pattern: in 1805 a third of British cotton textiles were exported; by 1840, with production tripled, 71 per cent went to the periphery. The mass-production paradigm repeated it — US expansion in the 1950s, the German, Italian and Japanese “miracles” of the 1960s, Brazil, Taiwan and Korea in the 1970s — even as the information revolution was already irrupting in the core. Strictly, then, a surge has six phases: gestation before the big-bang, the four core phases, and a final outstretching to the peripheries that overlaps the first phases of the next revolution. The fifth surge, born global, may be the first to involve the whole world in every phase.

Part II · Chapter 7

Financial Capital and Production Capital

Part II re-tells the whole sequence from the standpoint of finance, and begins by defining its two protagonists — not as kinds of capital but as agents and criteria.

Financial capital vs. production capital

Financial capital (FK) embodies the criteria of agents who hold wealth as money or paper assets and act to make it grow — mobile, footloose, indifferent to what a firm does so long as it promises gain; in essence the agent for reallocating and redistributing wealth. Production capital (PK) embodies the motives of those who create wealth by producing goods and services — builders, rooted in specific knowledge, equipment, networks and places, accumulating profit-making capacity. Schumpeter defined capitalism as “that form of private property economy in which innovations are carried out by means of borrowed money”: the two functions need each other, but they are not the same.

The relationship between them changes systematically across the surge — the itinerary the rest of Part II elaborates. In irruption, FK falls into a passionate love affair with the emerging revolution. In frenzy, it becomes convinced it can live and thrive on its own; production capital becomes one more object of manipulation; the decoupling is nearly complete, and the gap between paper and real wealth widens until it must collapse. At the turning point, the implosion brings paper values back into line with real values and opens the door to regulation that re-establishes the link. In synergy, a recoupled “happy marriage” puts production capital at the helm with finance as facilitator — the one time the textbook notion of finance as intermediary comes close to reality. In maturity, profitable outlets dwindle, money turns idle again, and FK drifts off toward distant loans and radically new technologies — sowing, respectively, the next debt crises and the next revolution.

Mapping two centuries of financial crises onto the phases, she finds the truly major collapses sitting two or three decades after each big-bang, and notes that these bubbles bear the name of the revolution’s infrastructure — canal mania, railway mania, Internet mania: the “object of speculation” in Kindleberger’s sense is, in these cases, technological. Collapses are rare in irruption and synergy, bunched in frenzy and maturity; she concedes a degree of circularity, since the phases were dated partly by the crises — that is the nature of a heuristic model.

Part II · Chapter 8

Maturity: Financial Capital Planting the Seeds of Turbulence

The phase-by-phase narrative starts, deliberately, a decade before the big-bang — in the maturity of the previous surge, where the next revolution gestates. The most powerful firms, facing exhaustion, seek market control: the railway “amalgamations” of 1860s Britain, the conglomerate wave of 1960s America — concentration to capture share of saturated markets, a band-aid that cannot cure paradigm constriction. Firms and finance also redeploy away from core countries and sectors: idle British capital poured into the post-Civil-War USA for railways; in the decade before the First World War the core countries staged a huge wave of worldwide investment. Her Table 8.1 shows the pulsation in UK figures: foreign investment was 20–29 per cent of total capital formation during synergy and irruption phases (1855–64, 1875–84, 1895–1904), but 40–53 per cent during maturity and frenzy (1865–74, 1885–94, 1905–14).

Idle money does not stop growing in maturity, and as worthy borrowers dwindle, lending standards fall. This is the phase that creates great volumes of sovereign and consumer debt and nurtures the future defaults. British funds lent to American states in the 1820s–30s for canals defaulted in the crash of 1837; those lent in the late 1860s–early 1870s for railways defaulted in 1873. The Latin American record, drawn from Marichal, shows loan fever arriving every fifty to sixty years in the maturity phase — independence loans of 1820–25, public-works loans of 1860–73, investment loans of 1904–14, development loans of the 1960s–70s swollen by petrodollars — with defaults in the installation period of the next surge: the 1980s debt crisis was the recurrence, not an anomaly. Kindleberger captures the supply side: years before the oil price rise, multinational banks swollen with dollars “tumbled over one another … and practically forced money on the less developed countries.” Questionable practices flourish too — currency games, the 1960s–70s flurry of tax havens. But the same dispersal of risk-hungry idle capital funds the embryonic technologies of the next revolution: after Suez, money was abundant for steamships and international telegraph; after Ford’s assembly line, for everything mass-production; from the mid-1960s, for electronics and computers.

Part II · Chapter 9

Irruption: The Love Affair of Financial Capital with the Revolution

After the big-bang, huge successes and incredible profit margins in certain pockets of the economy become potent magnets for idle capital, and what ensues is best described as infatuation. Each revolution calls forth its own risk-capital instruments. Cotton mechanization needed little fixed capital, so family money and trading fortunes (the “nabobs”) sufficed; railways required sums beyond any single firm, so joint-stock companies spread the risk, with individual promoters underwriting; the third surge made investment banking an indispensable part of the industrial system — Carnegie’s 1875 plant was still funded by fellow capitalists (and survived the 1873 crash only because Carnegie could buy out his partners), but by 1878 Edison was financed by young Morgan’s bank, and by century’s end finance in Germany and the USA was taking outright control of heavy industry. In the fifth surge the equivalent was the plentiful supply of venture capital in small quantities. Nor is it all private: state governments put up as much as 40 per cent of US railway funding, and from Gerschenkron’s latecomers to postwar Japan and Korea, the state has repeatedly promoted the financial innovations of catch-up.

FK also lubricates the trade in new goods (90-day revolving credit for early cotton; small short-term instruments once steamships and Suez quickened commerce; hire-purchase for interwar refrigerators and cars; plastic and virtual money since the 1970s) and funds the rejuvenation of the old giants — “wounded giants … ready to experiment even with ‘witchcraft’”: railways shifting to steel rails in the 1870s–80s; the US auto industry of the 1970s–80s adopting just-in-time, microelectronics and globalization in a decade; oil and chemicals turning to digital process control and specialty products. And the financial world is itself the most demanding test bed of each revolution: banks were early clients of the penny post, railways and telegraph, then of telephone, typewriter and calculator; branch networks, international stock markets, and from the 1970s instant global money movements each prefigured the organizational scale of the coming paradigm.

By the end of irruption the love affair leads finance astray. Inebriated with the profit rates of the new industries — still a small fraction of the economy — FK comes to expect those returns from every investment, and rather than fund boring old production it develops instruments to make money out of money. Whole industries are meanwhile wiped out: Wells’s 1889 account records how two or three coal-tar dye works in Germany and England extinguished the entire madder-growing economy of Holland, France, Italy and the Levant after 1872. The decoupling has begun; a mid-installation crash often marks the passage — England 1836, France 1882, the USA in 1920 and again in 1987. With or without one, Frenzy arrives as the triumph of financial capital, which from then on calls the tune.

Part II · Chapter 10

Frenzy: Self-Sufficient Financial Capital Governing the Casino

Frenzy — her chapter title borrows Susan Strange’s “casino” — is the tumultuous period when financial capital takes off on its own and its immediate interests overrule the whole system. Two contradictory things happen at once. The revolution genuinely flowers: the infrastructure is installed, the paradigm becomes the modernizing model, the new industries replace the old engines of growth — the prosperous side of the fence, singing the “new economy.” Simultaneously, everything not on the modernization path deteriorates, and FK accelerates the centrifugal forces: the rich get richer, the poor poorer, with idle money now in the hands of nouveaux riches who want to multiply wealth at the speed they made it.

The instruments multiply: inverted pyramids of holdings, leveraged buy-outs, derivatives and junk bonds making “everybody into an investor,” hedge funds — Long-Term Capital started 1998 with about $4.7 billion of investors’ capital and borrowed as much as $120 billion. Real estate is a perennial favorite: in 1980s Tokyo the grounds of the Imperial Palace reached the nominal value of all the land in California; of 1880s Chicago, the Tribune wrote:

People bought property at prices they knew perfectly well were fictitious … [being sure] that some still greater fool could be depended on to take the property off their hands and leave them with a profit.— Chicago Tribune, late 1880s, quoted in Kindleberger

Fragile debtors crack first: several American states defaulted in the frenzies of the second and third surges — having built canals when Britain was entering the railway boom, then iron railways when Bessemer steel was about to obsolete them — and the Third World debt crisis and the US Savings & Loan collapse played the part in the 1980s. Yet paradigm shifts also open real windows of opportunity in the periphery: Argentina in the 1880s, made a meat-and-wheat supplier by steam and refrigerated shipping, drew such an investment boom that the City of London thought it the next USA — until the Baring crisis of 1890; the Asian Tigers a century later rode foreign investment and Cold-War market access until the 1997 collapse, when over-provided funds “backed more by the booming atmosphere than by the credit-worthiness of the specific borrower” came to a head.

Late frenzy is mania proper: the intense repetition of one successful recipe — canals from any river to any river, railways where demand was doubtful and promoters unloaded shares at a premium, and in the 1990s ever more dot-coms and telecommunications. The Financial Times called the result a “trillion dollar scrap-heap”: in 2001 an estimated 1–2 per cent of the fiber optic cable buried under Europe and the United States had been lit, and receivers of bankrupt telecoms recovered under 10 per cent of construction costs. As 1929 approached, ordinary Americans took out loans to buy shares. Overinvestment breeds frantic competition and premature obsolescence (Wells described steamers of 1870–73 displaced by 1875–76, themselves discarded within a decade for triple-expansion engines), then mergers — the post-Sherman-Act wave of the 1890s, the mega-mergers of the late 1990s — sized and shaped by each paradigm: vertical integration in the third surge, horizontal in the fourth, transcontinental networks in the fifth. Ethics soften: railway-rights commissions in 1840s Britain, bootlegger networks in the 1920s and money laundering in the 1990s, Ponzi in mid-1920s Florida — and Enron as the emblem of the fifth surge’s opacity. “Financial markets are not immoral, they are amoral,” she quotes Soros. Beneath it all the tension builds: by 1995 the notional value of derivatives (~$64 trillion) had nearly reached the combined value of all bonds, equity and bank assets in the G17 (~$68 trillion); her chart of 1971–99 shows the Dow Jones breaking ever further from US GDP; Yahoo, a virtual company with hardly any physical assets, was briefly worth more than all of Eastman Kodak. The system cannot grow indefinitely along that path.

Part II · Chapter 11

The Turning Point: Rethinking, Regulation and Changeover

Why must the bubble burst? Because, whatever they are called — “mania” or “new economy” — late-frenzy booms are structurally unsustainable, resting on three tensions. First, between paper and real wealth: capital gains created by the collective faith of investors cannot be matched by the speed at which the economy produces real wealth (after the crash, Le Monde noted firms freed from the “impossible dogma” of 15 per cent return on equity). Second, between the profile of potential supply and that of demand: frenzy is at bottom a huge regressive redistribution of income toward the casino, and concentrated income causes premature market saturation — most people who cannot buy a computer at $1,000 cannot buy one at $500 either; only widened markets (exports, government spending, or income redistribution) relieve it. Third, between the excluded and the beneficiaries: the ratio of the richest fifth of the world’s population to the poorest fifth in per-capita GNP went from 30:1 in 1960 to 60:1 in 1990 and 74:1 by 1999 — “the best of times” for many, “the worst of times” for many more.

The collapse — the NASDAQ implosion of spring 2000 was the fifth surge’s deathblow, as 1929 was the fourth’s and 1847 the second’s — serves as catharsis and as the door to regulation. Financial capital, arrogant from years of vindicated genius, accepts rules only after much of the rapidly made gains have evaporated; hence her pointed warning about bail-outs: without some significant bankruptcies not bailed out, finance will never abide by the necessary regulation. Big crashes teach big lessons (short-lived ones, Galbraith and Kindleberger observe, but many rules survive in law and common sense — the post-1929 framework lasted until the early 1970s, though it took a decade and a world war to be accepted).

The historical record bends the model rather than breaking it. Britain in the third surge had no domestic frenzy: its financial capital toured the globe, the bubbles and crashes happened abroad (Baring was fully bailed out by the Bank of England in 1890), and the home industrial base withered. The USA, forging ahead, had its true stock-market madness during synergy, between the “rich man’s panic” of 1903 and the crash of 1907 — and J.P. Morgan’s banking fraternity performed central-bank functions and took direct possession of production rather than handing over power. As for why the US depression after 1929 lasted so long when most post-mania depressions ran two years or less: the New Deal was systematically opposed for fear of Socialism, and it took wartime collaboration in the military–industrial complex for American capital to accept a coordinating state — in a sense, war production was the beginning of deployment. The deeper point is about power. Golden ages happen when production capital is at the helm, because only PK has an interest in pursuing trajectories, scale and ever-wider markets. Finance, given the choice, prefers the short-lived capital gain:

Securities analysts believe that companies make money. Companies make shoes!— Peter Drucker, Fortune interview, 1998

Turning the tables requires both the weakening of finance by the collapse of its paper mountain and the intervention of political forces. The outcome — golden age or merely a modified gilded age — is genuinely undetermined; the turning point is the moment when the mode of growth for the next two or three decades is chosen.

Part II · Chapter 12

Synergy: Supporting the Expansion of the Paradigm

Once the framework is recomposed, deployment opens with synergistic growth: real production as the basic source of wealth, “the confident optimism of company expansion” replacing “the arrogant self-complacency of cunning speculation.” It is the only period when aggregate statistics are consistent enough for safe extrapolation. Both American synergies returned primacy to the real economy, but differently — Chandler’s “financial capitalism” in the Progressive Era, with financiers on the boards, versus “managerial capitalism” in the postwar golden age, when professional managers embodied production capital. The postwar case is her clearest instance of PK in control: in 1947 Wall Street “completely failed to reflect the nation’s economic exuberance,” and a 1962 congressional report found that postwar corporations financed capital requirements chiefly from internal sources, with only a quarter from borrowing and securities.

Each synergy is enabled by an institutional build-out matched to its paradigm. For the Victorian boom: the Bank Act of 1844 separating money issue from credit, factory and mine legislation of 1842–44, the repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846, joint-stock banking, checks as means of payment. For the belle époque: the universal Gold Standard centered on London, the 1883 Paris Convention on patents, generalized limited liability — and Bismarck’s social-security experiment of 1883 spreading across Europe in the 1890s. For the postwar golden age: Bretton Woods with the dollar as gold-equivalent, the IMF, World Bank, BIS, GATT and the Marshall Plan abroad; Keynesian policies, the FDIC, the SEC, the Banking Act of 1935, compartmentalized banking (Strange’s “Chinese walls”), welfare systems, and consumer and mortgage credit made safe by unemployment insurance at home.

Technology’s role changes too: from the gigantic experiment of installation to the build-out of the chosen constellation — Brian Arthur’s “arrangements-for-use,” the thousand and one sub-technologies that adapt us to the new technologies and them to us; innovations creamed at the luxury end become cash cows at the mass end; cost reductions in core inputs and infrastructure (cheaper steel and freight in the third surge, oil and electricity in the fourth, microelectronics and telecommunications in the fifth) drive a virtuous circle for the whole economy. But synergy carries its own ending. A mid-life jolt may come (the 1857 crash in Britain, the 1960 US recession); then maturity returns with mergers for market share, idle money, labor unrest demanding the promised benefits — and emigration time again for both production and financial capital, while at the core the next revolution prepares to irrupt.

Part II · Chapter 13

The Changing Nature of Financial and Institutional Innovations

Chapter 13 systematizes Part II in two tables. Table 13.1 is a typology of financial innovations ranked from most to least useful to the real economy: Type A, capital for new products and services (venture capital, joint stocks, infrastructure finance); Type B, instruments for growth and expansion (bonds, government funding, finance for production abroad); Type C, modernization of financial services themselves (telegraph transfers and checking accounts through automatic tellers and e-banking); Type D, profit-taking and risk-spreading instruments that pull investors in (mutual funds, IPOs, junk bonds, derivatives, hedge funds); Type E, instruments to refinance obligations or mobilize existing assets (swaps, Brady bonds, acquisitions, takeovers); and Type F, questionable innovations — legal loopholes, fiscal havens, arbitrage, and “making money without money,” from pyramid schemes to insider trading and outright swindles.

Table 13.2 maps the types onto the phases. Irruption shows the maximum intensity and variety of real financial innovation — all six types at once. Frenzy tilts toward D, E and F: escape control, attract funds, speculate, inflate assets. Synergy shifts to adaptive A, B and C innovations accompanying growth — the “back to basics” trend toward safe price–earnings ratios and sharing in real profits. Maturity revives B and E (outspreading, concentration of ownership) plus F (propping up profits through opacity): in the early 1910s US banks set up investment affiliates to buy stock the law forbade them — “a masterpiece of legal humor” — and the uncovering of the “Money Trust” led to the Glass–Owen Act and the timid forerunner of the Federal Reserve.

Across paradigms, finance is remolded by the revolution it serves. The local banks of the first surge could never have assembled railway capital; the joint-stock and limited-liability machinery of the second and third built financial empires that could fund US Steel, General Electric, Siemens and AEG — but would have struggled to envisage the myriad small consumer-credit arrangements the fourth surge required. Mass production converted daily living into an activity supported by “home capital goods” bought by installments — Seligman saw it in 1927 as “another revolution in economic science and economic life” — and made government a fundamental economic actor. The fifth surge poses harder puzzles: knowledge, experience and information have become capital goods. How is knowledge capital measured? Can it serve as collateral? What is the value of a product infinitely reproducible at almost zero cost? Each new economy, she insists, is real — but none abolishes the cyclical character of the system, and each requires its own economics, as Keynes supplied one for mass production whose tools went impotent when stagflation arrived.

Part III · Chapters 14–15 & Epilogue

The Recurring Sequence, Its Causes and Implications

Part III compresses the model. Development proceeds by great surges because three spheres — technology, the economy, and institutions — co-evolve in constant reciprocal action, each changing at its own speed. Technology is the fuel of the capitalist engine, but it clusters into revolutions for social, not scientific, reasons: paradigms over-adapt society until exhaustion forces the gates open. In the economy, the decouplings and recouplings of financial and production capital set the rhythm and direction of growth: financial capital enables the succession of surges; production capital ensures the full deployment of each. The institutional sphere, seat of politics, ideology and the mental maps of society, has the greatest inertia — salutary when it protects the full spread of a revolution’s benefits, obstructive when the next one irrupts. Capitalism may advance precisely by pendular extremes: installation periods of unhindered private profit-seeking, deployment periods when those forces are moderated for widespread social benefit — a vision she finds anticipated in the historian Edwin F. Gay.

The implications chapter is a user’s manual. The model is a heuristic, “a very blunt and fuzzy instrument,” built — she admits — on four and a half cases; dogmatic dating defeats its purpose, wars have their own dynamics, and for individual crises Minsky or Kindleberger serve better. But it explains the ebb and flow of economic ideas: Pigou observed money treated as a passive “veil” before 1914, an “evil genius” in the 1920s–30s, and insignificant again after the war — tracking deployment, installation, deployment; the Methodenstreit that expelled history from economics came in the third surge’s installation period, exactly parallel to the monetarists’ defeat of the Keynesians in the fifth’s; Greenspan echoed Irving Fisher’s 1929 “permanently high plateau” on the eve of the NASDAQ collapse. For policy: the near future is often not an extrapolation of the near past; paradigm shifts redraw the political spectrum itself, splitting every grouping between those looking backward and those looking forward; and proposals for regulation must be designed during frenzy so they are ready “after the necessary catastrophe.”

The epilogue, dated Caracas, June 2002, declares the world at the turning point of the fifth surge. She invokes Keynes’s 1930 diagnosis — no real recovery “until the idea of lenders and the idea of productive borrowers are brought together again” — and warns against blaming the recession on September 11th as stagflation was once blamed on OPEC. The Enron and WorldCom scandals were not an epidemic of dishonesty but the consequence of frenzy’s impossible profit pressure: during frenzy, crime paid well. Against saturation pessimism she offers the automobile precedent: the 23 million cars registered in the USA in 1929 — 32 per cent coverage of 72 million adults, which then looked unsurpassable — passed 70 per cent by 1970. The tasks she sets are enforceable regulation, demand-shaping policies to extend the Information Revolution, and action on global polarization, with the developing world as the great unsaturated market. Her closing line: “A golden age of worldwide expansion is possible. Making it happen will require thinking big, deciding wisely and acting boldly.”

Reading it critically

The standard criticisms begin where Perez herself does: a model built on “four and a half cases” invites overfitting, and dating the phases partly from the crises they are meant to predict is, as she concedes, circular. Long-wave skeptics (following Rosenberg and Frischtak’s challenge) question whether the regularities survive once aggregate evidence is ruled inadmissible — her “two moneys” argument immunizes the theory against statistical refutation as much as it explains measurement failure. The third surge requires so many exceptions (no British frenzy, an American frenzy during synergy, finance never handing over power) that critics ask what would count as disconfirmation. Events since 2002 cut both ways: admirers read the 2008 crisis as the aftershock completing the fifth surge’s turning point; skeptics note that the golden-age deployment she anticipated has, two decades on, kept receding. The book’s afterlife is undeniable: it became canon in venture capital (Marc Andreessen and Fred Wilson assign it; “we’re in the deployment phase” is Silicon Valley vernacular) — an irony, given that its policy heart, strict financial regulation and income redistribution as preconditions for golden ages, is the part its loudest fans cite least.

If you remember ten things

  1. Five technological revolutions, each dated from a big-bang: Arkwright’s mill (1771), the Liverpool–Manchester railway (1829), Carnegie’s Bessemer plant (1875), Ford’s Model-T (1908), the Intel microprocessor (1971).
  2. Each revolution propagates through a techno-economic paradigm — a shared “common sense” of best practice that can rejuvenate the whole economy, not just the new industries.
  3. A great surge of development lasts roughly half a century and divides into installation (irruption, then frenzy) and deployment (synergy, then maturity), hinged at a turning point.
  4. Financial capital and production capital are functionally separate agents: footloose money seeking gain versus rooted builders of productive capacity — and their decoupling and recoupling drives the cycle.
  5. In irruption, finance falls in love with the revolution; in frenzy it decouples and runs the casino; at the turning point it is re-regulated; in synergy it serves production; in maturity its idle money funds bad loans abroad and the seeds of the next revolution.
  6. The frenzy bubble — canal mania, railway mania, the roaring twenties, the dot-com boom — bears the name of the revolution’s infrastructure, and for all its waste it installs that infrastructure and teaches the paradigm.
  7. The crash is structurally necessary: paper wealth grows faster than real wealth can, concentrated income saturates markets prematurely, and polarization becomes politically untenable.
  8. Golden ages are not automatic — they require institutional recomposition (an 1844 Bank Act, a Bretton Woods) that puts production capital at the helm and widens demand; the alternative is a continued gilded age.
  9. Diffusion spreads in ripples from core to periphery with decade-long lags, which is why maturity-phase loan fevers to the periphery recur — and default in the next installation period.
  10. The model is a heuristic for asking when you are, not a clock: each surge is unique in content, recurrent only in sequence, and policy at the turning point decides whether deployment becomes a golden age for the many or the few.

Concepts to keep

Great surge of development

The full fifty-odd-year arc by which a technological revolution diffuses from big bang to maturity. Perez’s unit of analysis — longer than a business cycle, shorter than an age — and the frame that makes a crash look like a midpoint rather than an ending.

Techno-economic paradigm

The shared common sense of an era about what is cheap, efficient, and possible — mass production’s standardization, ICT’s networks and flexibility. Paradigms outlive their technologies as habits and institutions, which is why each revolution must fight the ghost of the previous one.

Installation vs. deployment

The two halves of every surge: a finance-led period that builds the infrastructure amid speculation and inequality, then a production-led period that spreads the benefits. Diagnosing which half you are living in is the framework’s main practical use.

Frenzy

The late-installation phase when financial capital fully decouples from production: asset inflation, “new economy” rhetoric, fraud, and overinvestment in the revolution’s infrastructure. Destructive for investors, weirdly generative for society — the mania pays for the rails.

The turning point

The post-crash interval in which institutions must be recomposed to match the new paradigm. Not automatic and not guaranteed to succeed: golden ages are made by regulation, redistribution, and rebuilt demand, or they are missed.

Financial vs. production capital

Mobile, uncommitted money versus money embodied in firms and capabilities. Their decoupling causes frenzies; their recoupling enables golden ages. The distinction gives you a precise question for any boom: whose logic is in command right now?

Institutional recomposition

Perez’s term for the socio-political creativity each surge demands — new rules, safety nets, and demand structures suited to the new paradigm. Technology determines what is possible; this determines who benefits.

In the author's words

“The sequence technological revolution–financial bubble–collapse–golden age–political unrest recurs about every half century and is based on causal mechanisms that are in the nature of capitalism.” — Carlota Perez, Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital, introduction
Perez’s recurring claim, made throughout Part Two: the bubble is functionally necessary — only the irrational exuberance of uncommitted financial capital will overbuild a revolution’s infrastructure, and the crash is the price of handing that infrastructure to society at a discount. — close paraphrase of the frenzy argument
Her equally recurring warning, easy to miss amid the VC enthusiasm for her work: golden ages are not delivered by technology but by politics — every deployment period in history required deliberate institutional recomposition, and surges whose turning points fail get stagnation instead of synergy. — close paraphrase of the argument of Part Three

Threads to other books

The Power Law — Sebastian Mallaby
Mallaby’s venture capitalists are Perez’s financial capital with names and offices on Sand Hill Road — the professionalized, permanent institution her irruption phase always had to improvise before.
The Innovator’s Dilemma — Clayton Christensen
Christensen supplies the firm-level mechanics inside Perez’s macro cycle: disruption is how the new paradigm’s entrants dismantle incumbents who are still optimizing for the old one’s common sense.
Energy and Civilization — Vaclav Smil
Each of Perez’s surges rests on an energy foundation Smil documents — coal under steam, oil under mass production — and his slow-transition evidence explains why her surges take half a century rather than a decade.
Inflation: A Guide for Users and Losers — Mark Blyth & Nicolò Fraccaroli
Blyth’s insistence that macroeconomic regimes are political settlements is Perez’s turning point examined under a magnifying glass: both books argue that who wins from an economic era is decided by institutions, not technology.
Machines of Loving Grace — Dario Amodei
Amodei’s essay is best read as a deployment-period manifesto written during an installation period — a description of the golden age AI could fund, before the turning-point work that Perez says such ages always require.
For the AI researcher

Perez hands you a diagnostic instrument for your own moment, so use it honestly. The AI boom of the mid-2020s rhymes loudly with her frenzy phase: hundreds of billions of dollars in datacenter capex, circular vendor-financing deals, valuations priced off imagined future demand, “this time is different” rhetoric, and a widening gap between AI asset prices and AI revenues. On her reading, some substantial financial reckoning would be unsurprising — and also not the verdict on the technology. The railways were real; so was the railway mania’s collapse. Both things can be true of AI, and the researcher who internalizes that will neither panic at a crash nor mistake the absence of one for proof of sober pricing.

The genuinely hard question is where to place AI in her scheme, and intellectual honesty requires admitting the framework underdetermines the answer. Is AI the synergy phase of the ICT surge that began with Intel in 1971 — the moment information technology finally penetrates every industry — or the irruption of a sixth surge with its own big bang (the transformer, or ChatGPT)? The two readings imply different futures: one suggests we are nearer the harvest, the other that the turbulence has barely started. Perez herself has favored versions of the first reading; much of Silicon Valley prefers the second, perhaps because it flatters the present. Treat the ambiguity as a research question, not an inconvenience.

Either way, her deepest lesson lands on you directly: the golden age is not a deliverable of the technology. If AI’s deployment period is to spread its gains beyond the capital owners of the installation phase, someone must do the unglamorous turning-point work — on labor, on competition, on safety institutions, on who captures the surplus. Researchers tend to assume that part is someone else’s job. Every failed surge in Perez’s history made the same assumption.
