Geopolitics of Compute

Chip War: The Fight for the World’s Most Critical Technology

Chris Miller · 2022
~12 hours to read Condensed read below: ~35 min read How computing power became the new oil Pairs with: Energy and Civilization

Every gradient you compute runs through a supply chain so concentrated that a handful of buildings in Taiwan, the Netherlands, and Japan are now the most strategically contested real estate on Earth. This book explains how that happened — and it is the indispensable map of the physical and political substrate beneath all of modern AI.

Chris Miller is not a tech journalist but an economic historian at Tufts University’s Fletcher School, and he came to semiconductors sideways. His earlier books were about the Soviet Union — why Gorbachev’s reforms failed, how Putin’s economy works — and while studying why the USSR lost the Cold War he kept bumping into the same answer: Moscow could match American tanks and warheads but never American microelectronics. The question that provoked Chip War was essentially Cold War history asked forward: if computing power decides great-power competition, who controls its production?

The answer turned out to be terrifyingly few people, and Miller’s timing was uncanny. The book appeared in October 2022, the same month the Biden administration imposed sweeping export controls on advanced chips and chipmaking tools bound for China — a policy whose logic the book explains better than any government document. It won the Financial Times Business Book of the Year and became standard reading in Washington, Brussels, Taipei, and, one suspects, Beijing.

What makes the book durable rather than topical is its method: sixty years of industrial history told through people — engineers, salesmen, spies, and one extraordinary Taiwanese-American executive — showing that today’s chokepoints were not designed by strategists but accreted through thousands of commercial decisions that nobody understood as geopolitical at the time.

The core argument

Computing power is the strategic resource of the twenty-first century, playing the role oil played in the twentieth — except that its production is far more concentrated than oil ever was. Decades of globalization produced not a distributed supply chain but a series of chokepoints: one Taiwanese foundry, one Dutch lithography firm, a few American design-software and tool companies. Whoever controls these chokepoints controls the future of military and economic power, which is why the US–China rivalry has crystallized around silicon.

The mechanism behind the concentration is Moore’s Law understood correctly — not as a law of physics but as an economic treadmill. Each generation of chips requires roughly doubling capital intensity: a leading-edge fabrication plant now costs more than twenty billion dollars, and an EUV lithography machine is among the most complex devices humans have ever manufactured. At that cost curve, the market can support only one or two players at each layer of the stack. Competition didn’t fail; it succeeded so brutally that it exhausted the field.

The political consequence follows directly. Because the chokepoints sit in allied territory — Taiwan, the Netherlands, Japan, South Korea, the United States — the US possesses a non-military weapon of extraordinary power: it can deny any country access to advanced computing by coordinating a handful of firms. But the same concentration creates fragility. Roughly ninety percent of the most advanced logic chips are fabricated by TSMC on an island that China claims, within range of thousands of missiles. The system’s efficiency and its danger are the same fact viewed from different sides.

Miller resists both triumphalism and doom. His history shows that leadership has changed hands before — America nearly lost the industry to Japan in the 1980s — and that every attempt to build self-sufficiency by state fiat, from the Soviet copy-it strategy to China’s subsidy torrents, has so far foundered on the same problem: the knowledge is tacit, networked, and accumulating faster than it can be stolen.

Miller, an economic historian at Tufts, set out to write a history of Soviet missile guidance and discovered that every thread led back to one artifact: the silicon chip. Chip War is the seventy-five-year history of that artifact told as great-power politics — eight parts and fifty-four short chapters running from Bell Labs in 1947 to the Taiwan Strait in 2022. His thesis is blunt: semiconductors are the oil of the twenty-first century, except more concentrated. Taiwan fabricates roughly 37 percent of the world’s new computing power each year, a single Dutch company builds the only machines that can pattern the most advanced chips, and a handful of American firms sit astride the software and tools every fab on earth depends on. The post–Cold War assumption that this interdependence was safely apolitical has collapsed; the fight to control chokepoints in the chip supply chain is now the central arena of US–China competition. This condensation follows the book’s own eight-part structure, merging its short chapters into one section per part. It preserves Miller’s journalistic specificity — names, dates, fab nodes, dollar figures — while omitting only repeated examples and some minor character sketches.

Part I

Cold War Chips

The question Part I answers: where did the transistor come from, and why did the integrated circuit become an industry in California rather than a laboratory curiosity? Miller starts with the problem the chip solved. Wartime computers like ENIAC ran on eighteen thousand vacuum tubes — bulky, hot, power-hungry, and so unreliable that moths flying into the tubes could crash a calculation (the original “bug”). In December 1947, at Bell Labs in New Jersey, John Bardeen and Walter Brattain — working under the brilliant, abrasive William Shockley — demonstrated the transistor: a sliver of semiconducting material that could switch and amplify current with no vacuum, no filament, and almost no power. The three shared the 1956 Nobel Prize. That same year Shockley left Bell Labs to found Shockley Semiconductor in Mountain View, California, near his aging mother in Palo Alto — a decision of filial convenience that accidentally sited the future of the world economy.

Shockley was a world-class physicist and a catastrophic manager; he subjected employees to lie-detector tests and publicly posted salaries. In 1957 eight of his young researchers — the “traitorous eight,” including Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore — walked out. A young financier named Arthur Rock found them backing from Sherman Fairchild’s camera company, and Fairchild Semiconductor was born, the institutional ancestor of essentially all of Silicon Valley. Meanwhile in Dallas, Texas Instruments — a former oil-surveying outfit pushed into electronics by its hard-driving executive Pat Haggerty — hired a tall, quiet engineer named Jack Kilby. In the summer of 1958, alone in the lab because new employees had no vacation time, Kilby built multiple electronic components on a single piece of germanium: the first integrated circuit, demonstrated on September 12, 1958. Months later Noyce at Fairchild independently devised a better version — a silicon chip using colleague Jean Hoerni’s planar process, with metal interconnects printed directly onto the surface, far more manufacturable than Kilby’s flying wires. Kilby got the Nobel Prize in 2000; Noyce, who died in 1990, got the industry. A third unsung figure made mass production possible: Jay Lathrop, who with chemist James Nall had turned a microscope upside down at a US Army lab to project a shrunken pattern of light onto photoresist-coated semiconductor material — photolithography, the printing technology that still defines the industry. Lathrop took the technique to TI; Nall took it to Fairchild.

Chips were initially absurdly expensive, and Miller is emphatic that the Cold War state was the first customer of last resort. After Sputnik in 1957, NASA’s Apollo program needed a guidance computer light enough to fly to the moon; MIT’s Instrumentation Lab bet on Noyce’s Fairchild chips, and by the mid-1960s the Apollo computer was the most chip-intensive object ever built. The Air Force’s Minuteman II missile did the same for Texas Instruments — by 1965 the Minuteman program alone was buying around a fifth of all integrated circuits sold in America. Military demand bought down the learning curve: the average price of an integrated circuit fell from about $32 in 1961 to under $2 by the end of the decade. But Noyce — by then running Fairchild — deliberately refused to let the company become a defense contractor. He slashed prices, sometimes selling chips below manufacturing cost, to crack open the civilian computer market, betting that volume would drive costs down faster than any military program. It worked: within a few years the civilian computer industry, not the Pentagon, was the chipmakers’ biggest customer.

Moore’s Law

In a 1965 article in Electronics, Fairchild’s Gordon Moore observed that the number of components that could economically be crammed onto a chip was doubling every year (he later revised it to every two years) and predicted the trend would continue. In Miller’s telling, Moore’s Law is not a law of physics but the industry’s collective business plan — a treadmill of exponential improvement that, sustained for half a century, made computing power roughly a million times cheaper and turned whoever controlled the leading edge into a strategic power.

Part I closes in the Soviet Union, with one of the book’s best stories. Two American engineers, Joel Barr and Alfred Sarant — members of the Rosenberg spy ring who fled the FBI and resurfaced in the USSR as “Philip Staros” and “Joseph Berg” — convinced Nikita Khrushchev to build a Soviet microelectronics city. Khrushchev, charmed during a 1962 visit to their Leningrad lab, ordered the construction of Zelenograd outside Moscow: a purpose-built Soviet Silicon Valley with its own institutes, fabs, and apartment blocks. But the system around it was poisoned from the start. When an engineer brought back a Texas Instruments chip from the United States, the minister in charge of Soviet electronics, Alexander Shokin, issued the order that became doctrine: “Copy it, one-to-one, without any deviations.” Miller’s analysis of why “copy it” failed is one of the book’s central arguments. Copying meant the Soviets were always reverse-engineering chips that were already obsolete, in an industry where the frontier moved every two years. COCOM export controls denied them the precision manufacturing equipment — and, crucially, the equipment to make the equipment. Soviet metrology used different standards, so even faithful copies often didn’t work. And because the customer was the military rather than a mass consumer market, there was no volume to drive down costs and no profit signal to drive innovation. The USSR ended up with a semiconductor sector that was structurally five to ten years behind and falling further back every cycle — a gap that, Miller argues, eventually decided the Cold War.

Part II

The Circuitry of the American World

Part II asks how chips became the connective tissue of the American-led order in Asia — and how they transformed warfare. The first thread is Japan. In 1953, Sony co-founder Akio Morita traveled to New York and licensed the transistor patent from AT&T for $25,000; Bell’s engineers reportedly suggested hearing aids as an application. Morita and his partner Masaru Ibuka instead built radios — the TR-55 in 1955 and the pocket-sized TR-63 in 1957, which sold by the millions in America. Washington not only tolerated this but encouraged it: a prosperous, export-oriented, capitalist Japan anchored to American technology was Cold War strategy. When Charles de Gaulle dismissed Japanese prime minister Hayato Ikeda as a “transistor salesman,” he meant it as an insult; Miller treats it as a description of the entire postwar settlement in the Pacific. Japan got transistor licenses and access to the American market; America got an ally whose prosperity refuted Communism. Sony’s genius, Morita insisted, was not invention but product innovation — identifying what consumers would buy and manufacturing it superbly, a formula that would later terrify the Americans who had taught it.

The second thread is offshoring, which began astonishingly early. Fairchild’s hard-charging operations chief Charlie Sporck — a man who fought unions in New York and California — discovered in 1963 that semiconductor assembly (attaching the hair-thin wires, packaging the die) could be done by hand in Hong Kong for about a tenth of American wages: roughly twenty-five cents an hour versus $2.50. Fairchild’s Hong Kong plant was the industry’s first Asian outpost, and the model spread down the wage gradient: Singapore, where Lee Kuan Yew’s government kept labor docile; Penang in Malaysia; South Korea; Taiwan. Miller stresses that this was geopolitics as much as economics. In 1968, Texas Instruments’ Mark Shepherd flew to Taipei to negotiate a plant with Taiwan’s economic architect, minister K.T. Li — a prickly meeting in which Li initially scoffed at intellectual property as a tool of imperialists, then proceeded to make Taiwan indispensable to American industry anyway. Li understood that semiconductor employment stitched Taiwan’s security to America’s: the more chips America’s economy needed from the island, the harder it would be to abandon. By 1980, TI Taiwan had shipped its billionth unit. Across non-Communist Asia, chip assembly jobs became the first rung of an electronics ladder — and a standing advertisement that integration with American capitalism paid better than revolution.

The third thread is precision warfare. In Vietnam, American bombing was lavish and ineffective: the Thanh Hoa bridge — the “Dragon’s Jaw” — survived hundreds of sorties and years of raids. A TI engineer named Weldon Word, working to a deliberately austere budget, built the Paveway laser-guided bomb: a simple silicon sensor divided into quadrants, a few cheap chips, and steerable fins that kept a falling bomb centered on a laser spot, for a few thousand dollars per guidance kit. In 1972, Paveway-equipped jets finally dropped the Dragon’s Jaw. Hardly anyone noticed amid a lost war, but two groups paid close attention. In the Pentagon, William Perry — a Silicon Valley engineer turned undersecretary of defense for research — built the “offset strategy” in the late 1970s: since NATO could not match Soviet numbers, it would offset them with microelectronics, precision sensors, and guided munitions. And in Moscow, Marshal Nikolai Ogarkov, chief of the Soviet general staff, began warning that a “military-technical revolution” built on computing was underway — one the Soviet Union, with its copy-it chip industry, could not join. Miller’s point: by the late 1970s the silicon supply chain running from California through Japan, Taiwan, and Southeast Asia was simultaneously America’s economic order and its military edge.

Part III

Leadership Lost?

Part III answers: how did the country that invented the chip nearly lose the industry in a decade? The shock arrived on March 25, 1980, when Hewlett-Packard executive Richard Anderson presented quality data comparing American and Japanese DRAM memory chips. The worst Japanese supplier had a lower failure rate than the best American one; Japanese chips failed far less often in the field. American chipmakers cried foul, then checked their own data and went quiet. Through the early 1980s, Japan’s big vertically integrated electronics firms — NEC, Toshiba, Hitachi, Fujitsu — seized the DRAM market generation by generation. Their advantages compounded: obsessive manufacturing quality; patient, nearly free capital from allied keiretsu banks (American firms paid double-digit interest rates under Volcker; Hitachi borrowed almost for nothing); government coordination through MITI; and a willingness to keep investing through brutal price crashes. In the 64K DRAM generation Japanese firms took the majority of the market. By the late 1980s Japan was outproducing the United States in chips overall, supplying around half the world market, and Japanese firms dominated the equipment and materials beneath it — Nikon in lithography, Japanese suppliers in silicon wafers and photoresists.

DRAM vs. logic

DRAM — dynamic random access memory — is the commodity workhorse chip that stores data temporarily. DRAMs are sold on price and density, making memory a capital-intensive treadmill in which the producer with the cheapest capital and best yields wins. Logic chips (like microprocessors) execute instructions; their value lies in design and architecture, not just manufacturing yield. The pivotal American move of the 1980s was abandoning the memory treadmill to the Japanese (and later the Koreans) and retreating up the stack into logic — which turned out to be where the profits and the strategic leverage lived.

The carnage in Silicon Valley was real. Intel — founded by Noyce and Moore in 1968 precisely to make memory chips — was bleeding money in its core business. Miller retells the famous 1985 scene: Andy Grove, Intel’s Hungarian-refugee president, asked Gordon Moore what a new CEO would do if the board threw them out. “He would get us out of memories,” Moore said. Grove’s reply: then why shouldn’t we walk out the door, come back in, and do it ourselves? Intel exited DRAM and bet everything on microprocessors — the seed of Part IV. Others chose combat. Charlie Sporck, now running National Semiconductor, led the industry’s political war, accusing Japan of dumping and espionage (a sting had caught Hitachi employees buying stolen IBM secrets in 1981). The result was the 1986 US–Japan Semiconductor Agreement, which set price floors on Japanese DRAMs and an informal target of 20 percent foreign market share in Japan — a deal whose main effects, Miller notes wryly, were to fatten Japanese producers’ margins and open a price umbrella under which South Korea’s Samsung crept into the memory business. In 1987 the industry and the Pentagon co-funded Sematech, a $500-million-a-year consortium in Austin led by Bob Noyce himself, to repair America’s manufacturing base.

Sematech’s record was mixed, and Miller’s case study of failure is GCA Corporation, the Massachusetts firm that had invented the stepper — the machine that exposes chip patterns one die at a time — in 1978 and briefly led the world in lithography. GCA was an innovation-rich, execution-poor company: arrogant with customers, chaotic in manufacturing, whipsawed by the DRAM crash. Nikon, which had entered the business supplying lenses, beat it on reliability and service. Despite Sematech subsidies and repeated rescue attempts, GCA died in 1993, and with it American leadership in the most critical chipmaking tool. The anxiety of the era was captured by Sony’s Morita and nationalist politician Shintaro Ishihara in their 1989 tract The Japan That Can Say No, which argued that Japan’s chip supremacy meant it could tip the military balance between the superpowers by selling — or withholding — semiconductors. CIA analysts took the scenario seriously. In 1986 Fujitsu moved to buy Fairchild itself; the political uproar killed the deal, but the symbolism was unmissable: the company that had founded Silicon Valley was on the block, and Washington was learning that chip supply chains were power.

Part IV

America Resurgent

How did the United States come back? Miller’s answer braids four stories: a potato farmer, a paranoid CEO, a Korean counterweight, and a war that proved the offset. The potato farmer is J.R. Simplot, the Idaho billionaire who supplied McDonald’s french fries and drove a Lincoln with the license plate “MR SPUD.” In the early 1980s he bankrolled Micron, a tiny Boise DRAM startup founded by twin brothers Ward and Joe Parkinson. Everyone knew an American memory company could not survive the Japanese onslaught; Micron survived precisely by being un-Japanese — fanatically cheap, design-frugal, shrinking dies to squeeze more chips from each wafer, run by people with no aerospace-contractor habits. It became, and remains, the last American DRAM maker, proof that commodity manufacturing could survive in America on ruthless cost discipline rather than subsidy.

The paranoid CEO is Andy Grove. Having abandoned memory, Intel staked its future on the microprocessor — the chip that, since its invention at Intel in 1971, put a computer’s brain on one die. The bet rode a wave Intel did not control: IBM had chosen Intel’s x86 processor (and Microsoft’s operating system) for its 1981 PC, and as PC clones exploded, Intel’s 386 and successors became the de facto standard of personal computing. Grove — author of Only the Paranoid Survive — drove the company with production discipline learned from the memory wars and a near-monopoly architecture rooted in software lock-in. By the early 1990s Intel was the world’s largest chipmaker, and America’s comparative advantage had visibly shifted: not commodity manufacturing but architecture, design, and the software ecosystems wrapped around chips. Japan, meanwhile, had overinvested in DRAM capacity just as the PC era made microprocessors the value center; when Tokyo’s bubble burst after 1990, the patient capital vanished, and Japan’s share of the world chip market began a long slide from which it never recovered.

The counterweight is South Korea. Samsung’s founder Lee Byung-chul declared in February 1983 — the famous Tokyo declaration — that the chaebol would enter semiconductors, a capital-devouring business in which Korea had no evident advantage except will. Crucially, Silicon Valley helped: Micron licensed its 64K DRAM designs to Samsung for needed cash, and American firms saw Korean memory as a stick with which to beat their Japanese rivals — my enemy’s enemy, in Miller’s framing. With government-backed credit, Stanford- and Silicon Valley–trained engineers, and the price floors of the 1986 agreement working in its favor, Samsung climbed the DRAM learning curve and by the 1990s was overtaking Japan in memory. The pattern matters for the whole book: American technological leadership has repeatedly worked by cultivating allied Asian manufacturing — first Japan, then Korea and Taiwan — accepting dependence on friends as the price of beating rivals.

The war is the Persian Gulf, 1991. The Soviets had tried to close the chip gap by theft — the KGB’s Directorate T and its “Line X” officers systematically stole Western technology, and Soviet fabs ran on smuggled and copied Western equipment — but stolen blueprints could not conjure the surrounding ecosystem, and the “copy it” trap held: perpetual obsolescence. When American Paveway-guided bombs dropped down ventilation shafts in Baghdad and Patriot batteries engaged Scuds on live television, Marshal Ogarkov’s prophecy was vindicated in the most public way possible. Soviet generals understood Desert Storm as a demonstration that their military, built on mass, had been technologically outclassed by a military built on microelectronics. Within a year the Soviet Union — whose semiconductor industry, Miller notes, had by then mostly collapsed into making digital-watch chips and counterfeit Western parts — was gone. Part IV’s conclusion: the Cold War’s outcome was decided in part at the fab. Computing power had become a decisive input to military power, and America and its friends controlled it.

Part V

Integrated Circuits, Integrated World?

Part V asks where the industry’s globalized structure came from — and its answer is mostly one man’s biography. Morris Chang was born in Ningbo, China, in 1931, and spent his childhood fleeing wars: Guangzhou, Chongqing, Shanghai, Hong Kong. He landed at Harvard in 1949 — the only Chinese freshman in his class — transferred to MIT for mechanical engineering, and twice failed the PhD qualifying exam, which he later called the luckiest break of his life. He joined the young transistor industry at Sylvania in 1955, moved to Texas Instruments in 1958 (arriving the same summer Kilby built the integrated circuit), and became a legend of production: a manager who tuned manufacturing processes like an engineer-gambler, lifting yields on lines others had written off, rising to run TI’s entire worldwide semiconductor business. In the early 1980s, passed over for the CEO job as TI drifted toward consumer products, Chang left. In 1985, Taiwan’s K.T. Li — the same minister who had courted TI in 1968 — recruited the 54-year-old Chang to run Taiwan’s Industrial Technology Research Institute with a mandate to build a Taiwanese chip industry.

Chang’s answer was a business model, not a product. Taiwan could not out-design Intel; but Chang had noticed at TI that chip designers increasingly didn’t want or need their own fabs, while fabs were becoming so expensive that only the largest firms could afford them. His idea: a foundry that would manufacture chips designed by others and would never design chips of its own — a manufacturer constitutionally incapable of competing with its customers. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company was founded in 1987 with the Taiwanese government providing about 48 percent of the capital, Philips contributing $58 million plus production technology and IP licenses for a 27.5 percent stake, and Taiwan’s reluctant business families strong-armed by the government into supplying the rest. The intellectual groundwork had been laid in academia: Caltech’s Carver Mead (who coined the phrase “Moore’s Law”) and Xerox PARC’s Lynn Conway had standardized chip design methods — their 1980 textbook Introduction to VLSI Systems taught a generation that design could be cleanly separated from fabrication, like authors separated from printing presses. Miller calls this the industry’s Gutenberg moment, and TSMC was its printing press.

The foundry model and the fabless revolution

A foundry manufactures chips for anyone; a fabless firm designs chips and owns no factory. Before TSMC, a chip startup needed hundreds of millions of dollars for a fab; after 1987, it needed only designers and a TSMC contract. The model unleashed the fabless wave — Qualcomm (founded 1985, Irwin Jacobs’s bet on CDMA wireless), Nvidia (founded 1993 by Jensen Huang, whose graphics processors and later CUDA software became the engine of AI), Broadcom, and eventually Apple’s silicon team — while concentrating the world’s actual manufacturing, year by year, on one island in the Taiwan Strait. Efficiency and fragility were the same fact.

Two counterpoint stories fill out the part. The first is Maoist China, where the chip industry was strangled at birth: the Cultural Revolution sent semiconductor experts to feed pigs, championed slogans about the masses making transistors in backyard-furnace style, and left China — which in the 1950s had a credible electronics research base — decades behind even the Soviet Union. The opening under Deng changed the direction but not quickly the level. The second is the man who tried to close the gap: Richard Chang (no relation to Morris), a devout Christian and Texas Instruments veteran born in Nanjing and raised in Taiwan, who founded SMIC — Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation — in Shanghai in 2000, with foreign capital, secondhand equipment, hundreds of engineers recruited from Taiwan, and churches built near his fabs. SMIC grew fast but ran straight into the IP problem: TSMC sued it for trade-secret theft, extracting a $175 million settlement in 2005 and then, after a second suit in 2009, another payment plus a roughly 8 percent equity stake in SMIC; Richard Chang resigned. The lesson Miller draws forward into Part VII: catching up by hiring away Taiwan’s engineers and borrowing its know-how was possible, but the frontier kept moving, and the legal-political immune system of the existing ecosystem fought back.

Part V also opens the book’s lithography arc. In the early 1990s, with optical lithography approaching the limits of visible and near-ultraviolet light, the industry had to choose what would come next. Intel’s Andy Grove placed the decisive bet on extreme ultraviolet (EUV) light — wavelength 13.5 nanometers — and in the late 1990s Intel pushed the formation of the EUV LLC consortium with the US national laboratories (Lawrence Livermore and Sandia did much of the foundational science). The politically fateful detail: no American company was left that could build the machines. GCA was dead; Silicon Valley Group, the last US lithography firm, was subscale (ASML bought it in 2001 with Washington’s acquiescence). Japan’s Nikon was excluded as a strategic rival. So the consortium licensed America’s EUV research to a midsize Dutch company: ASML, spun out of Philips in 1984, which had only recently clawed its way to relevance. The United States, Miller observes, outsourced the future of lithography to a friend — a decision that looked like efficient globalization and turned out to be the single most consequential chokepoint placement of the era.

Part VI

Offshoring Innovation?

Part VI carries two intertwined stories through the 2000s and 2010s: the most complex machine ever sold, and the slow-motion fumble of the American champion. Start with the machine. Making EUV lithography work took roughly two decades and tens of billions of dollars across ASML and its suppliers — a bet sustained long after most rational firms would have quit (Nikon and Canon both declined to even try). The physics reads like satire. EUV light is absorbed by air and by lenses, so everything happens in vacuum, with mirrors. The light source, built by Cymer of San Diego (founded by two UCSD laser PhDs in 1986; ASML bought it in 2013), fires tiny droplets of tin — fifty thousand per second — across a vacuum chamber, where each droplet is struck twice by a high-power carbon-dioxide laser: a first pulse to flatten the droplet into a pancake, a second to blast it into plasma at temperatures of several hundred thousand degrees, many times hotter than the surface of the sun, emitting a puff of 13.5-nanometer light. The laser itself, developed by Germany’s Trumpf, has exactly 457,329 component parts. The light is gathered and steered by mirrors from Zeiss made of layered silicon and molybdenum — the smoothest objects humans have ever manufactured; scaled to the size of Germany, their largest imperfection would be a fraction of a millimeter. Each production EUV machine has more than 100,000 parts, costs well over $100 million, and ships to customers in 40 freight containers. ASML shipped the first revenue-generating EUV tools in the late 2010s; TSMC and Samsung ran them at scale on the 7- and 5-nanometer nodes from 2018–2020. Miller’s punchline is about political geography: the machine is “Dutch” the way a Boeing is “American” — modules from Germany, California, Connecticut, Japan — but every unit is assembled in Veldhoven, and only ASML can make it. The most advanced manufacturing step on earth passes through one company in one small country, and behind it sits American-origin research and American-made components: a chokepoint waiting for a hand to grip it.

Now the fumble. In the mid-2000s, Steve Jobs came to Intel CEO Paul Otellini with the contract to build the processor for a new phone. Otellini turned it down: the volumes Apple promised wouldn’t cover the costs Intel projected. He later admitted the volume turned out to be a hundred times anyone’s forecast, and that the world would have been very different had Intel said yes. The iPhone launched in 2007; Apple started designing its own A-series processors (the A4 in 2010), manufactured first by Samsung and then, from 2014’s A8 onward, increasingly and finally exclusively by TSMC. Apple’s billions made TSMC’s leading edge: the iPhone became the volume driver that funded each new node, and Cupertino’s chip designs — fabricated in Taiwan, assembled into phones in China — became the emblem of an electronics world where America designed, Taiwan fabricated, and China did the final, low-value assembly. Intel, meanwhile, missed mobile entirely, was slow to embrace EUV (the technology it had once funded into existence), and ran aground on its own manufacturing: the 10-nanometer node, due around 2016, slipped year after year amid yield failures, and by 2020 Intel — for half a century the steward of Moore’s Law — had fallen behind both TSMC and Samsung at the leading edge.

The structural consequence was extreme concentration. AMD’s flamboyant founder Jerry Sanders had once roared that “real men have fabs,” but in 2009 AMD itself spun off its factories into GlobalFoundries, owned by Abu Dhabi’s Mubadala fund; in August 2018 GlobalFoundries abandoned its 7-nanometer program, quitting the leading edge for good. That left three firms on the planet capable of frontier logic fabrication — TSMC, Samsung, Intel — and, given Intel’s stumbles, effectively two, with TSMC alone holding around 90 percent of the world’s most advanced (sub–10-nanometer) capacity. America’s share of global chip fabrication fell from 37 percent in 1990 to about 12 percent by 2020. Miller is careful with the ledger: offshoring wasn’t theft or folly — it was Moore’s Law’s economics, the staggering cost of each new node (a leading-edge fab now runs ~$20 billion, the most expensive factories in human history) rewarding scale and specialization. The efficiency was real. So was the new map: the entire digital world now rested on a few buildings in Hsinchu and Tainan, a hundred miles from the Chinese coast.

Part VII

China’s Challenge

Part VII asks whether China can do what Japan, Korea, and Taiwan did — and why Beijing is trying so much harder. The motivating fact is dependence: China spends more money importing semiconductors each year than it spends importing oil — chip imports have run above $250 billion a year, exceeding Saudi Arabia’s oil exports or the global aircraft trade. Every Chinese tech giant is built on foreign silicon, and Xi Jinping has said so plainly, warning that core technologies controlled by others are a hidden danger lodged in the foundations of China’s rise. The policy response escalated through the 2010s: the National IC Industry Investment Fund — the “Big Fund” — launched in 2014 with roughly $20 billion, followed by a second tranche of similar scale and a swarm of provincial imitators, perhaps $100 billion in state capital all told; then Made in China 2025, announced in 2015, which set explicit targets to slash chip import dependence and produce most chips domestically within a decade. Where Japan and Korea had climbed inside the American-led system, Miller argues, China’s ambition — stated in its own documents — is to escape it.

The campaign’s methods get a chapter each. Buying: Tsinghua Unigroup’s Zhao Weiguo — who spent part of his childhood herding pigs and goats in Xinjiang, where his parents had been exiled, before becoming a billionaire dealmaker — went on a global shopping spree, bidding $23 billion for Micron in 2015 (rebuffed as politically impossible), seeking a stake in Western Digital, courting TSMC and MediaTek; almost everything was blocked by CFIUS or pre-emptively abandoned, and Unigroup itself later collapsed into default. Forced partnership: foreign firms desperate for market access signed technology transfer deals — IBM opened its Power architecture to Chinese partners; AMD licensed x86 server chip technology into a Chinese joint venture; Arm sold majority control of its China subsidiary in 2018 — each deal individually defensible, collectively a slow leak of crown jewels. Stealing: the cleanest case is DRAM-maker Fujian Jinhua, which used engineers hired from Micron’s Taiwanese subsidiary, via Taiwan’s UMC, to acquire Micron’s trade secrets; the US indicted the companies in 2018 and put Jinhua on the export blacklist, cutting it off from American equipment — the firm’s fabs went quiet almost immediately, a preview of the chokepoint weapon. And building: Yangtze Memory (YMTC) in Wuhan became a genuinely competent NAND flash maker, and a generation of engineers poached from TSMC and Samsung — thousands of them, at multiples of their salaries — seeded fabs across China.

The most formidable piece is Huawei. Ren Zhengfei, a former People’s Liberation Army engineer, founded the company in Shenzhen in 1987 with a few thousand dollars, importing telephone switches; three decades later Huawei was the world’s largest telecom equipment maker, a top smartphone vendor, and — through its chip-design arm HiSilicon — China’s only world-class chip designer, with Kirin smartphone processors designed in-house and fabricated by TSMC at the leading edge, competitive with Apple’s and Qualcomm’s best. Huawei’s R&D budget rivaled the biggest Western tech firms; its 5G base stations were better and cheaper than Nokia’s or Ericsson’s. Miller is even-handed about the espionage allegations — documented cases of copying (Cisco source code, a stolen robot arm called Tappy) sit alongside genuine engineering excellence — but the strategic picture alarmed Washington regardless: a Chinese national champion at the heart of the world’s communications infrastructure, dependent on the US-led chip ecosystem and steadily working to outgrow it. SMIC, meanwhile, kept advancing — reaching 14 nanometers and, with clever multi-patterning of older DUV tools, edging toward 7 — but remained years behind TSMC and utterly dependent on American, Dutch, and Japanese equipment it could not yet replace. That dependence is the hinge on which the book’s final part turns.

Part VIII

The Chip Choke

The final part answers: what happens when the United States stops treating the chip supply chain as commerce and starts treating it as a weapon? Miller borrows the political-science frame of weaponized interdependence — the insight that globalized networks have hubs, and whoever controls a hub can coerce everyone who depends on it. The chip industry, mapped honestly, is nothing but hubs. Three American firms (Cadence, Synopsys, and Mentor, now owned by Siemens) control essentially all the electronic design automation software without which no modern chip can be designed. American equipment makers — Applied Materials, Lam Research, KLA — plus ASML and Tokyo Electron control the tools without which no fab can run. ASML alone makes EUV machines. TSMC makes around 90 percent of the most advanced processors. Nearly every chip on earth touches American software, American tools, or American IP somewhere in its gestation — which means American law can reach it.

Weaponized interdependence & the chokepoint

A chokepoint is a stage of the supply chain with so few alternative suppliers that denial of access functions as a blockade. The October 2022 innovation was the expansive use of the foreign direct product rule: US controls attach not just to American exports but to anything made anywhere using American tools or software. Because no fab on earth runs without American equipment, this converted private dependence into extraterritorial state power — Miller’s “chip choke.”

The weapon was tested by escalation. In April 2018, ZTE — caught violating Iran and North Korea sanctions — was briefly denied access to American components; the company, with seventy-five thousand employees, began dying within weeks, and was revived only when Trump, after a personal appeal from Xi, commuted the sentence to a $1.4 billion fine. Washington noticed how fast the patient had crashed. In December 2018 Huawei CFO Meng Wanzhou — Ren’s daughter — was arrested in Vancouver on US fraud charges. In May 2019 Huawei went on the Entity List, cutting it off from American chips; Huawei worked around the ban through TSMC, so in May 2020 the Commerce Department invoked the foreign direct product rule: no chip made anywhere with US equipment or software could be sold to Huawei without a license. TSMC complied and cut Huawei off that September. The effect was devastating and surgical — HiSilicon’s Kirin line ended, Huawei’s smartphone business collapsed (it sold off its Honor brand), and its 5G ambitions were hobbled — all without a shot, a sanction on China generally, or a dollar of tariffs. In parallel, sustained US lobbying persuaded the Dutch government from 2019 onward to withhold the export license for ASML’s EUV machines to China: SMIC, added to the Entity List itself in December 2020, would be frozen out of the one tool the leading edge requires.

Then came the strategic codification. The pandemic-era chip shortage of 2020–21 — car plants idled for want of $1 chips — taught even casual observers what Miller’s subjects already knew, and Congress passed the CHIPS and Science Act in August 2022: $52 billion, including $39 billion in manufacturing incentives, to pull fabrication back onshore (TSMC had already announced an Arizona fab in 2020, initially $12 billion, later vastly expanded; Samsung and Intel announced their own). And on October 7, 2022 — three days after Chip War was published, in a coda Miller addresses in the book’s afterword and his subsequent commentary — the Biden administration imposed the most sweeping technology controls since the Cold War: bans on exporting advanced AI chips (Nvidia’s A100/H100 class) to China; bans on the equipment needed to make logic chips below roughly 14–16 nanometers, DRAM below 18 nanometers, and NAND above 128 layers; and a prohibition on US persons supporting advanced Chinese fabs, forcing American engineers to choose between their jobs and their passports. The explicit goal was no longer to stay a couple of nodes ahead — the old “sliding scale” — but to freeze China’s advanced computing capabilities outright, on the theory that AI and supercomputing are the military technologies of the coming decades.

The book closes where it opened: the Taiwan Strait. Miller began with the destroyer USS Mustin steaming through the strait in August 2020 as China’s military drilled around it, and he ends by tallying what is actually at stake along that hundred-mile stretch of water. Taiwan fabricates roughly 37 percent of the world’s annual new computing power; TSMC’s most advanced fabs — like Fab 18 in Tainan, a structure costing on the order of $20 billion — produce virtually all of the world’s cutting-edge processors. Taiwanese strategists call this the “silicon shield”: the world cannot afford to let the island burn. Miller is less comforted — dependence can deter, but it also tempts, and it guarantees that any war would be an economic catastrophe dwarfing any previous supply shock, with damage measured in trillions of dollars and a decade of lost computing growth. TSMC chairman Mark Liu has said publicly that an invasion would render TSMC’s fabs inoperable — they live on real-time connections to suppliers and engineers in Europe, Japan, and America. Nobody can rebuild the system quickly; ASML’s machines, TSMC’s process knowledge, and the thousand-firm supply chain behind them took half a century of cumulative, compounding learning. Moore’s Law is slowing — transistors are approaching atomic scales, and the easy doublings are gone — but demand for computing, above all for AI, is accelerating, which makes the chokepoints more valuable, not less. The fight for the world’s most critical technology, Miller concludes, has only begun.

Reading it critically

Reviewers have been near-unanimous on the book’s craft (it won the Financial Times Business Book of the Year, 2022) but raise recurring objections. First, perspective: the story is told largely from Washington’s side, and some critics find the framing of export controls insufficiently skeptical — Miller documents the chokepoints brilliantly but spends less time on whether weaponizing them accelerates the very decoupling and indigenization they aim to prevent. Second, prediction: events since publication cut both ways. The October 2022 controls vindicated the book’s thesis within days, but Huawei’s August 2023 Mate 60 Pro — powered by a 7-nanometer Kirin 9000S fabricated by SMIC using multi-patterned DUV — showed the choke was leakier than advertised, and China’s subsequent surge of state investment, equipment stockpiling, and toolmaker substitution suggests controls bought time rather than a freeze. Third, economists note the book lightly treats counterarguments: that “copy it” failed the Soviets less because copying is inherently doomed than because they lacked market discipline; and that subsidy-driven reshoring (CHIPS Act fabs have seen delays and cost overruns) may recreate the inefficiencies Miller elsewhere shows the market punishing. None of this undermines the history — it sharpens the open question the book deliberately leaves: whether chokepoint power depreciates with use.

If you remember ten things

  1. Chips are the new oil, but more concentrated: a handful of firms in a handful of countries control inputs the entire world economy needs, and Taiwan alone fabricates roughly 37 percent of the world’s new computing power each year.
  2. The US military was the chip industry’s first big customer — Apollo and Minuteman bought down the cost curve — but Noyce’s decision to chase civilian volume with low prices is what made Moore’s Law self-funding.
  3. The Soviet “copy it” strategy failed structurally: in an industry that doubles every two years, copying guarantees perpetual obsolescence, and export controls plus the absence of a consumer market did the rest.
  4. Precision-guided munitions — TI’s Paveway dropping the Thanh Hoa bridge in 1972, then the 1991 Gulf War — turned microelectronics into military power and vindicated both Perry’s offset strategy and Ogarkov’s warnings.
  5. Japan’s 1980s DRAM conquest nearly killed Silicon Valley; America recovered not by winning memory back but by retreating up the stack — Grove’s Intel pivoting to microprocessors while Micron survived on ruthless cost discipline.
  6. Morris Chang’s TSMC (1987) invented the foundry model — manufacture for everyone, compete with no one — which unleashed fabless design (Qualcomm, Nvidia, Apple) while concentrating the world’s advanced fabrication on one island.
  7. EUV lithography took about two decades, tens of billions of dollars, and a planetary supply chain — Cymer’s 50,000 tin droplets per second, Trumpf’s 457,329-part laser, Zeiss’s smoothest-ever mirrors — and only ASML can build the machine.
  8. Intel’s decline was self-inflicted in increments: turning down the iPhone chip, adopting EUV late, and fumbling 10nm — leaving TSMC with about 90 percent of leading-edge capacity.
  9. China spends more importing chips than oil; its catch-up drive (Big Fund, Made in China 2025, SMIC, YMTC, Huawei/HiSilicon) is formidable but still hangs on American, Dutch, and Japanese tools it cannot yet replace.
  10. Washington has weaponized that dependence — ZTE, the Huawei Entity List, the foreign direct product rule, the October 7, 2022 controls — betting it can freeze China’s advanced computing; Taiwan is simultaneously the system’s linchpin and its most likely flashpoint.

Concepts to keep

Chokepoints

Layers of the supply chain where one or two firms control near-total capacity: ASML in EUV lithography, TSMC at the leading edge, US firms in design software. Chokepoints are simultaneously instruments of coercion for whoever holds them and single points of failure for everyone.

The fab/fabless split

Morris Chang’s foundry model separated chip design from chip manufacturing, creating both the explosion of fabless innovators like Nvidia and the extreme concentration of fabrication itself. It is the institutional invention underneath the entire modern computing economy — including every AI accelerator.

Moore’s Law as economics

Transistor doubling was sustained not by physics but by exponentially growing capital expenditure and learning-by-doing. Each node bankrupts more competitors, so miniaturization and monopolization are the same process — concentration is the cost of progress, not a deviation from it.

The EUV monopoly

The clearest case of a globally produced, singly controlled technology: science from everywhere, integration by exactly one Dutch firm. It proves Miller’s deepest point — that in advanced manufacturing, tacit integration knowledge, not patents or capital, is the true barrier to entry.

Export controls as economic weapons

The foreign direct product rule extends US jurisdiction to anything made with American tools anywhere on Earth, turning private supply chains into instruments of state power. Once used against Huawei, the precedent generalized — and it now defines the boundary conditions of global AI development.

Taiwan as silicon shield

The theory that TSMC’s irreplaceability deters Chinese invasion and guarantees American protection. Miller shows the shield is double-edged: the same concentration that protects Taiwan makes a Taiwan crisis the single largest tail risk to the world economy — and to the compute supply of AI.

Government as first customer, not designer

The US won by buying early chips at any price (Apollo, Minuteman) and then letting commercial markets drive scale, while the Soviet command model failed by copying. A recurring template for thinking about industrial policy — including today’s CHIPS Act and national AI compute programs.

Threads to other books

Energy and Civilization — Vaclav Smil
Smil shows that every civilization is built on its dominant energy conversion; Miller documents the moment a second physical substrate — computation — joined energy as a resource states will reorganize the world to control.
The Power Law — Sebastian Mallaby
The same Fairchild founding scene launches both books: Mallaby traces the financial machine that funded the chip industry, Miller traces the industrial machine it built, and neither story is complete alone.
Nexus — Yuval Noah Harari
Harari treats information networks as the engine of history but mostly abstracts away their hardware; Miller is the corrective, showing that whoever controls the physical chokepoints of computation constrains every network built on top.
Machines of Loving Grace — Dario Amodei
Amodei’s timeline to powerful AI silently assumes uninterrupted access to leading-edge compute; Miller’s book is the risk register for that assumption, from export controls to the Taiwan Strait.
Seeing Like a State — James C. Scott
Scott catalogs the failures of top-down schemes that ignore tacit local knowledge; Miller’s Soviet Zelenograd and China’s subsidy campaigns are semiconductor-scale case studies of exactly that pathology.
For the AI researcher

Your research sits at the absolute end of Miller’s supply chain, and the dependencies run one way. The GPU hours behind every experiment you run pass through TSMC’s Taiwanese fabs, ASML’s EUV machines, and a tool-and-materials chain with no redundancy. The export-control regime that began with Huawei in 2020 and generalized in October 2022 has since become the central instrument of US AI policy — compute thresholds, country tiers, licensing of frontier-scale clusters. Reading Chip War is how you understand that these are not arbitrary regulations but the deliberate exercise of chokepoint power whose sixty-year construction Miller documents. It also explains the counter-reaction you can see in the literature: the surge of Chinese work on training efficiency, model distillation, and domestic accelerators is what adaptation to a chokepoint looks like from the inside.

Two implications are worth internalizing rather than just noting. First, compute access is now a political variable in your career planning: where you work determines what scale of experiment you can run, and the gap between compute-rich and compute-poor research is itself partly a policy artifact. Research directions that reduce compute dependence — algorithmic efficiency, better scaling science, evaluation that doesn’t require frontier-scale replication — carry a geopolitical option value that pure scaling work does not. Second, take the Taiwan scenario seriously as a tail risk to the entire field. A blockade or war would not merely raise GPU prices; it would freeze the leading edge for years, since no fab outside Taiwan can currently replace TSMC’s most advanced capacity. Anyone whose five-year research plan assumes smoothly compounding compute should at least know that the assumption rests on the calmest possible reading of the Taiwan Strait.
