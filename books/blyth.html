Inflation: A Guide for Users and Losers

Mark Blyth & Nicolò Fraccaroli · 2025
~6 hours to read Condensed read below: ~25 min read Inflation as a fight over who pays Pairs with: Seeing Like a State

Between 2021 and 2023 the rich world relearned, painfully, that prices can move — and then reached for a forty-year-old playbook to stop them. This short, combative book asks the question that playbook buries: inflation is never just a number, so who actually pays when we fight it, and who quietly collects?

Mark Blyth is a Scottish political economist at Brown University’s Watson School, best known for Austerity: The History of a Dangerous Idea (2013), which did for budget-cutting what this book tries to do for inflation-fighting: expose a technical-sounding policy as a distributional choice wearing a lab coat. His co-author, Nicolò Fraccaroli, is a younger economist who has worked at the World Bank and researches central banking — a useful pairing, since the book’s polemic energy comes with someone who can read the Fed’s models from the inside. Published by Norton in May 2025, it was picked as one of the Financial Times’s best summer books that year and promptly annoyed reviewers on both flanks — monetarists for its dismissal of money-supply stories, and some sympathizers for how far it pushes the alternative.

The provocation behind the book is the post-pandemic episode itself. Central banks failed to predict the inflation, misdiagnosed it as transitory, then fought a supply-driven price surge with demand-side tools — interest rate hikes — whose burden falls on borrowers, workers, and the young, while savers and asset owners did fine. Blyth and Fraccaroli want readers to notice that this outcome was not an accident of bad forecasting but a predictable consequence of which story about inflation our institutions are built to believe.

It is also, deliberately, a guide: shorter and funnier than its subject deserves, written for the citizen rather than the seminar, with the stated aim of arming “users” of the economy who would otherwise be its losers.

The core argument

Inflation is not one disease with one cause; it is a family of different events — demand surges, supply shocks, profit expansions, currency collapses — that get narrated through competing stories. The story that wins politically determines the cure, and the cure determines who pays. Because the dominant story was written in the 1970s, we keep treating supply-driven inflations with demand-killing interest rates, sending the bill to the people least able to pay it — and the coming age of climate shocks, tariffs, and fractured supply chains will make that mistake increasingly expensive.

Start with the deceptively simple measurement point. Economists define inflation as the rate of change of a price index; ordinary people experience the level of prices at the till. So a central banker can truthfully announce that “inflation is coming down” while groceries remain a third more expensive than in 2020, and the resulting fury is not innumeracy — it is two different definitions colliding. Layer on the politics of the index itself — what goes in the basket, how housing is counted, whose consumption pattern the average represents — and “the inflation rate” turns out to be a constructed object that fits some lives far better than others.

The book’s engine is the idea of inflation stories. Every inflation gets a narrative that names a culprit: greedy unions and profligate governments in the 1970s telling, money-printing central banks in the monetarist telling, supply chains and corporate margins in the post-COVID telling. These stories are not idle commentary — they are blame-assignment machines. If workers caused it, wages must be crushed; if governments caused it, budgets must be cut; if firms caused it, profits and prices become legitimate targets. Identifying the storyteller, Blyth and Fraccaroli insist, tells you who is being set up to pay.

Their reading of 2021–23 is that the evidence pointed mostly to supply — pandemic disruptions, energy prices after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, and firms with pricing power padding margins under cover of general confusion (the European Central Bank itself attributed a large share of 2022 euro-area inflation to profits). Yet the policy response was the 1970s cure: raise rates, cool labor markets, restore “credibility.” It worked, in the sense that inflation fell — but the authors argue it fell mainly because supply chains healed and energy prices normalized, while the rate hikes redistributed income toward creditors and made housing scarcer by choking construction. The lesson they draw is not that central banks are villains but that a one-tool regime is structurally unsuited to the supply-shock era we are entering.

After thirty years in which rich-world inflation was something that happened to other people, prices everywhere jumped by the most in four decades in 2021–23 — and the explanations on offer were, Blyth and Fraccaroli argue, mostly reheated leftovers from the 1970s. This short, wry book (W.W. Norton, 2025; an introduction, six chapters, and a conclusion, condensed here in order) is a political economy of inflation rather than a textbook: what inflation actually is, how the measurement sausage gets made, why the only tool we reach for is interest rates, why every inflation comes wrapped in a story about whose fault it is — and why those stories matter, because the story that wins decides who pays. The title is deliberate: not “winners and losers” but users and losers, because some actors don’t just survive inflation, they use it. This condensation folds the introduction into Chapter 1 and trims repeated worked examples of index arithmetic; everything else follows the book’s own running order.

Introduction & Chapter 1

Five Things They Don’t Tell You About Inflation

The book opens with a complaint disguised as a question: why, when prices surged in 2021, did public debate instantly snap back to scripts written half a century ago — print less money, crush the wage demands, channel Paul Volcker? Blyth and Fraccaroli’s answer is that the conventional wisdom about inflation is stuck in the past, and that the simple playbook — inflation goes up, rates go up — hides a multitude of beliefs about why prices rise that are often wrong, frequently damaging, and surprisingly thin on empirical support. Before you can argue about any of that, though, you need to know what inflation is. Their definition is strict: inflation is all prices rising together, and continuing to rise. Eggs getting dearer because avian flu killed the hens is a relative price change. Rent going up in Austin is a housing story. Inflation is the general level of prices moving — which immediately creates the first great public misunderstanding, because economists talk about the rate of change of prices while normal humans experience the level. When inflation falls from 9 percent to 3 percent, an economist says the problem is nearly solved; a shopper notices that nothing got cheaper, it’s merely getting more expensive more slowly. Both are correct. Much of the political rage about inflation lives in that gap.

The first chapter then delivers five things “they” don’t tell you. First, inflation comes in types. Borrowing a framing from the ECB’s Fabio Panetta, the authors sort it into the good, the bad, and the ugly. Good inflation is the gentle 2-percent-ish kind that accompanies a growing economy where wages rise alongside prices — a sign of health, and the reason central banks don’t target zero. Bad inflation is what a supply shock does: energy or food or shipping suddenly costs more, firms pass it through, real incomes fall. Ugly inflation is when the bad kind persists long enough that everyone starts pricing in more of it — firms raise prices in anticipation, workers demand wages in anticipation — and expectations come unanchored. The crucial point: these are different diseases, and a treatment designed for one can be useless or harmful for another.

Second, inflation is measured, not observed — and measurement is full of choices. A consumer price index is a weighted average over a basket of goods meant to represent what a typical household buys: statistical agencies collect tens of thousands of prices every month, weight them by expenditure shares, and publish a single number. Strip out food and energy — the volatile bits — and you get “core” inflation, which central bankers prefer because it reveals the underlying trend. Notice what that means in human terms: the items removed to make the statistic better behaved are precisely the items poor households spend most of their money on. Third, what matters is what is measured: there is no single true measure of inflation, and the “average” basket is nobody’s actual basket. A household in the bottom income decile, spending most of its budget on food, fuel, and rent, can live through substantially higher inflation than the headline number while a high-saving professional household barely notices.

Fourth, housing — the largest expense most people have — is the thing indices handle worst. You can’t just put house prices in a consumer price index, because a house is partly an asset; so the U.S. uses “owners’ equivalent rent,” which asks roughly what homeowners think their home would rent for — a survey-based fiction that moves slowly and badly. The UK, in switching its target measure in the early 2000s, effectively dropped owner-occupied housing costs out of the targeted index; the euro area’s harmonized index has long excluded them too. The result is official inflation numbers that can stay polite while the lived cost of shelter explodes. Fifth, you should care about all this arcane statistical stuff because the index is wired into everything: pension uprating, wage bargains, benefit levels, inflation-linked bonds, and — above all — the political narrative. Change what’s in the basket and you change who appears to be suffering, which changes what governments feel obliged to do.

Headline vs. core inflation

Headline inflation is the rate of change of the full consumer basket; core inflation strips out volatile food and energy prices to expose the underlying trend. The authors’ point: “core” is analytically useful and distributionally loaded, since food and energy dominate poorer households’ budgets — the statistic gets cleaner by ignoring the people inflation hurts most.

Chapter 2

Why Do We Do What We Do When We Have Inflation?

If inflation has many types and many causes, why does policy have exactly one reflex — raise interest rates? Because, the authors argue, our institutions are the fossilized trauma of a single decade. They call the dominant narrative “That ’70s Show.” The 1970s really were awful: OPEC’s 1973 embargo took oil from roughly $3 to $12 a barrel within months, and the Iranian revolution in 1979 doubled it again; U.S. inflation peaked near 15 percent in early 1980, and Britain’s went above 20 percent in 1975. And the 1970s had a transmission mechanism today’s economy mostly lacks: around a third of the workforce was unionized, and many contracts had automatic cost-of-living adjustment clauses, so an oil shock fed mechanically into wages, and wages back into prices — a genuine wage-price spiral.

Out of that decade came an intellectual victory and an institutional settlement. Milton Friedman’s 1968 address had argued that exploiting the Phillips curve trade-off between inflation and unemployment was a mirage: once people expect inflation, the trade-off vanishes and you get stagflation — which the 1970s then appeared to confirm. Monetarism rode in: control the money supply and you control prices. Volcker’s Federal Reserve put the doctrine to work in 1979–82, pushing interest rates toward 20 percent. Inflation broke — and so did a lot of other things. Unemployment hit 10.8 percent by late 1982, the highest since the Depression; worker bargaining power was crushed and never recovered; the savings-and-loan industry was gutted; and developing countries that had borrowed in dollars were devastated, giving Latin America a lost decade of growth. Volcker is remembered as the man who slew inflation. The bill, the authors insist, should be remembered too — and remembered as a bill paid mostly by people who hadn’t caused the problem.

The lessons of the decade were then poured into institutional concrete. Politicians can’t be trusted with the printing press, so make central banks independent. Expectations are everything, so give those banks a public target to anchor them — New Zealand invented formal inflation targeting in 1990, and its roughly-2-percent number spread around the world less because of deep theory than because it seemed to work and everyone copied it. There is some level of unemployment (the NAIRU) below which inflation accelerates, so don’t let labor markets get too tight. And disinflation has a price, formalized as the sacrifice ratio: the output and jobs you must destroy to shave a point off inflation. The toolkit, once crowded — price controls, credit controls, fiscal policy, incomes policies — shrank to a single hammer held by an unelected institution.

Two ironies get buried in this telling, and the book digs them up. First, monetarism itself was quietly abandoned almost immediately: when central banks in the early 1980s tried to actually steer money-supply aggregates, the relationship between money and prices fell apart in their hands, and they went back to interest rates while keeping the monetarist sermon. Second, the era’s great heresy — price controls — has a more interesting record than the canon admits. Richard Nixon froze wages and prices for ninety days in August 1971, and the freeze itself worked; inflation fell and the policy was wildly popular. What failed was the aftermath: the later phases relaxed into voluntary compliance, the controls were run with one eye on the 1972 election, and OPEC then blew the whole framework apart. The failure, the authors argue, was more political than economic — but the story we kept was simply “price controls never work,” which conveniently left the central bank’s hammer as the only legitimate tool in the box.

The sacrifice ratio

The cumulative loss of output (and the unemployment that goes with it) required to reduce inflation by one percentage point via demand suppression. It is the price tag on the conventional cure — and because the unemployed and the indebted pay it rather than the comfortable, the authors read it as a distributional choice dressed up as a technical parameter.

Central bank independence & inflation targeting

The post-1970s settlement: remove monetary policy from elected politicians (who face temptation to inflate before elections) and commit the central bank to a public inflation target — pioneered by New Zealand in 1990, with ~2 percent becoming the global folk number. The design assumes inflation is a demand-and-expectations phenomenon that interest rates can manage; it has no answer for supply shocks.

Chapter 3

Inflation Stories and the Politics of Blame

The book’s pivotal chapter lines up the four suspects blamed for the 2021–23 inflation and treats each as what it really is: not just a causal hypothesis but a story that allocates blame, and therefore a story about who should pay to fix it. This is the authors’ core move. Inflation stories are political weapons. Pick the villain and you’ve picked the policy; pick the policy and you’ve picked who eats the cost.

Suspect one: too much money — specifically, too much government spending. Larry Summers warned in early 2021 that the $1.9 trillion American Rescue Plan, layered on top of earlier pandemic packages, was the least responsible macroeconomic policy in four decades, and when inflation arrived he looked prophetic. The story’s political payload is austerity: if spending caused it, cut spending and benefits. The authors’ problem with it is the data. Inflation arrived nearly everywhere at once — in big-stimulus America and in small-stimulus Europe — and the amount of stimulus a country spent correlates poorly with how much inflation it got. The U.S. ran a couple of points hotter than peers, which fiscal policy plausibly explains; the global surge itself, it cannot. As for the strict monetarist version — Friedman’s most famous sentence — the authors grant it as accounting and deny it as explanation.

Inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon.— Milton Friedman, whose dictum the book treats the way Blyth does in interviews: true in the sense that shootings are always and everywhere ballistic phenomena — and about as useful for finding out who pulled the trigger and why.

Suspect two: workers — the wage-price spiral, the ghost of the 1970s. The Phillips curve (A.W. Phillips, 1958, drawn from a century of British wage data) says tight labor markets push up wages and therefore prices; pandemic-era labor markets were historically tight, so central bankers braced for the spiral. It never came. Union density in the U.S. private sector is now below 10 percent; the COLA clauses are gone; and through the worst of the inflation, real wages fell — workers were chasing prices, not driving them. The IMF’s own historical work struggled to find many episodes where wage-price spirals actually spiraled. Yet the story persisted, the authors note, because it justifies the conventional cure: if wages are the problem, engineered unemployment is the solution, and the central bank already owns that tool.

Suspect three: the stuff itself — supply. A pandemic shut the world’s factories and ports, then reopened them into a wall of goods demand; the cost of shipping a container from Shanghai to the U.S. West Coast went from around $2,000 to over $12,000 at the peak; semiconductors vanished and used-car prices went vertical; then Russia invaded Ukraine and Europe’s natural gas, and the world’s grain and fertilizer, repriced violently. On this story — the one the authors find does most of the explanatory work for 2021–23 — inflation was the bill for the pandemic meeting an energy war. Its political implication is uncomfortable for everyone: nobody domestic is to blame, rate hikes can’t print container ships or gas, and the honest answers are targeted relief and patience.

Suspect four: corporations — “greedflation,” or in Isabella Weber’s more careful term, sellers’ inflation. The mechanism (worked out by Weber and Evan Wasner) is not that firms suddenly discovered greed; it’s that a big, publicly known cost shock acts as a coordination device in concentrated markets. When everyone knows everyone’s costs went up, every firm can raise prices more than costs without fear of being undercut — cover for margin expansion. The numbers were not small: corporate profits accounted for an unusually large share of euro-area price rises in 2022 by the ECB’s own decomposition — on the order of 40 percent, with an IMF study putting it near half — and U.S. oil and gas companies booked roughly $220 billion in excess profits in 2022. The story’s political payload runs the other way from Summers’s: windfall taxes and price regulation, not austerity. Which, the authors dryly observe, is exactly why this story was policed so aggressively — and why your preferred suspect tends to correlate with your politics rather than with the evidence. All four stories contain truth; the authors’ own reading is supply shock first, amplified by profits, with fiscal heat explaining the American increment and wages explaining almost nothing. But the chapter’s real lesson is the meta-point: whoever wins the blame game wins the distribution game.

Sellers’ inflation

Weber and Wasner’s mechanism: an economy-wide, publicly visible cost shock lets firms in concentrated sectors raise prices in step without explicit collusion — the shock is the coordination signal — so margins widen under cover of “passing on costs.” Distinct from cartoon “greed”: the preferences didn’t change, the opportunity did.

Chapter 4

When Inflation Goes Hyper

No inflation argument gets far before someone says “Weimar” — wheelbarrows of marks, Zimbabwean trillion-dollar notes, Venezuela. Hyperinflation is the rhetorical nuclear weapon of fiscal politics: any deficit, any stimulus, is the first step on the road to it. So the authors devote a chapter to asking what actually happens in hyperinflations, and their answer is that the slippery-slope story has it backwards. Hyperinflation is vanishingly rare — economists have documented fewer than sixty episodes in all of recorded history — and it is not the end stage of ordinary inflation. It is a symptom of state collapse. The printing press shows up at the scene of the crime, but it is rarely the original perpetrator.

Take the canonical case. Weimar Germany in 1923 was not a normal economy that overdid the stimulus. It was a defeated state owing war reparations payable in gold and foreign currency — debts that printing marks could not touch — whose industrial heartland, the Ruhr, was occupied by French and Belgian troops, and whose government paid striking workers to resist the occupation by printing money while production collapsed. Output cratering plus foreign-denominated obligations plus a political legitimacy crisis: that is the recipe, and the printing is the last ingredient, not the first. Tellingly, the hyperinflation ended within months once the politics changed — the Rentenmark and a renegotiation of reparations stabilized prices in late 1923 — which is not how things work if money-printing alone is the disease.

The modern cases rhyme. Zimbabwe’s hyperinflation followed the collapse of its agricultural production — the supply base of the economy — after the land seizures of the early 2000s. Venezuela was a petrostate that lost its only real export earner when oil prices collapsed in 2014, then layered sanctions and political meltdown on top. Argentina’s chronic high inflation is bound up with a century of borrowing in a currency it cannot print and recurring exchange-rate crises. In each case the money supply exploded — the authors don’t deny the arithmetic — but the explosion was downstream of a state losing its productive base, its foreign-currency lifeline, or its legitimacy. Which is why the United States, the issuer of the world’s reserve currency, with the deepest capital markets on earth, is not “twelve months of deficits away from Weimar,” and why the hyperinflation card, whenever it is played against ordinary fiscal policy, should be read as a move in the blame game rather than a forecast.

Chapter 5

Why Didn’t We See This One Coming?

Central banks employ thousands of PhD economists and produce forecasts with impressive error bands. They failed to see the 2008 financial crisis, and then they failed to see the largest inflation in forty years — insisting through 2021 that it would be “transitory” — and then, having missed it, arguably misdiagnosed it. Why? The authors’ answer is that the failure was baked into the models. The workhorse forecasting frameworks treat inflation as the product of domestic demand and expectations: output gaps, labor-market slack, anchored or unanchored expectations. Supply — energy, shipping, war, disease — enters, if at all, as an “exogenous shock,” a residual the model is built to assume away. A model that cannot represent a container shortage or a gas war cannot predict the inflation they cause. The forecast failure wasn’t bad luck; it was the institutionalized 1970s worldview meeting a non-1970s inflation.

A second pillar of the framework fared no better: expectations. The whole apparatus of modern central banking rests on the idea that households and firms watch the central bank, believe its target, and set prices and wages accordingly — which is why so much of the job is speeches. The empirical record reviewed in the book is brutal and funny: surveys show most people have no idea what the central bank’s forecast is, form their inflation expectations from the prices they actually see — gasoline, groceries, rent — and increasingly report inflation expectations that track their partisanship more than any macro variable. Central bank communications circulate less widely than cat videos. You cannot anchor expectations that nobody is paying attention to.

Then there is the strange case of the cure. The famous “transitory versus permanent” fight — Team Krugman versus Team Summers — ended, the authors note, in a draw that flattered nobody: inflation was not permanent (U.S. CPI fell from its 9.1 percent peak in June 2022 to around 3 percent within a year), but “transitory” turned out to mean two-plus years. More awkwardly for the standard model, the disinflation arrived without the recession the sacrifice-ratio playbook demanded: unemployment barely moved — the “immaculate disinflation.” The timing suggests that supply chains healing and energy prices falling did much of the work that interest rates took credit for. The Fed raised rates from zero to over 5 percent, and something disinflationary happened; the rooster crowed, and the sun came up.

The chapter closes with the episode that best illustrates the politics of acceptable stories. In December 2021, the economist Isabella Weber suggested in a Guardian op-ed that strategic, targeted price controls — of the kind the U.S. used successfully during World War II — deserved a place in the toolkit for a supply-shock inflation. Paul Krugman called the idea “truly stupid” (he later apologized), and a remarkable pile-on followed. Within eighteen months, the substance of her position — that profits and specific bottleneck prices were central, and that targeted interventions could help — had migrated toward the mainstream, complete with ECB and IMF profit decompositions. The lesson the authors draw is not that Weber was right about everything; it’s that the boundaries of respectable inflation talk are themselves part of the political economy of inflation. Some stories get peer review; others get ridicule first and rehabilitation later.

Chapter 6

Are Inflation Wars Class Wars?

Inflation looks egalitarian — the price of eggs goes up for everyone — but it isn’t, and this chapter is the book’s ledger of who actually pays. Start with the losers. Households in the bottom half of the income distribution spend essentially everything they earn, and spend it disproportionately on exactly the categories that led this inflation: food, energy, rent. For them, inflation operates as a steep, regressive consumption tax, and the official statistics — core measures that exclude food and energy, housing measured by owners’-equivalent-rent fictions — systematically understate their experience. Through 2021–23, nominal wages rose but real wages for most workers fell: the average worker ended the episode able to buy less than before, whatever the “strong labor market” headlines said.

Now the users. Households at the top spend a small fraction of income on consumption, so inflation barely dents them — while their balance sheets quietly improve. Anyone holding a fixed-rate mortgage watched inflation eat the real value of their debt (the Fisher effect: unexpected inflation transfers wealth from creditors to debtors). And anyone holding equity in firms with pricing power got the sellers’-inflation dividend directly: of the roughly $220 billion in excess profits American oil and gas companies earned in 2022, about half flowed to shareholders through dividends and buybacks — and since the top 1 percent of households own about half of all equities, that is on the order of $100 billion landing on the wealthiest. Petrostates cashed in too; Saudi Arabia’s windfall was large enough, Blyth likes to note, to buy Cristiano Ronaldo for a league nobody watches. Some people don’t merely endure inflation. They use it.

Here is the chapter’s sting: the cure is class-coded too. Raising interest rates fights inflation by making the indebted poorer and some of the employed unemployed — that is the mechanism, not a side effect. Higher rates raise the cost of the credit poor households already pay the most for, while delivering higher risk-free returns to savers and bondholders. The standard framework asks workers to accept unemployment so that everyone can have price stability, and calls this neutral technocracy. The authors frame the underlying reality the way a growing academic literature (they cite work in the spirit of Blanchard, and Lorenzoni and Werning) now does: inflation is distributional conflict — a fight over who absorbs the real-income loss from a shock, conducted through prices and wages. Firms try to pass the loss to consumers, workers try to pass it back to firms, and the state’s choice of anti-inflation tool decides who finally eats it. Volcker’s cure crushed inflation, and also crushed labor for a generation; whether the disease or the cure did more damage, and to whom, is precisely the question the “inflation is bad for everyone” framing is designed not to ask. Inflation wars are class wars, fought under an assumed name.

Conclusion

So Have We Seen the Last of Inflation?

No — and the reasons are structural. The great moderation of 1990–2020 wasn’t primarily a triumph of central bank credibility, the authors argue; it was a thirty-year disinflationary tailwind. China added half a billion workers to the global labor pool and exported deflation in everything manufactured; Russian gas was cheap; supply chains were ruthlessly optimized for cost over resilience; the climate behaved. Every one of those tailwinds is now reversing. Deglobalization and tariff wars re-price the goods that globalization made cheap. Working-age populations are shrinking across the rich world and China, tightening labor supply for decades. And climate change is the great inflation machine of the century: crop failures and food-price spikes (“climateflation”), the volatile fossil-energy prices of a disorderly transition (“fossilflation”), and — the sleeper — property insurance, already repricing catastrophically in places like Florida and California, feeding straight into shelter costs. The future, on this view, is recurring supply-shock inflations in a world whose only institutionalized weapon is a demand-side hammer.

So widen the toolkit. The book’s closing argument is not “fire the central bank” — interest rates remain the right tool for genuine demand overheating and for ugly, expectations-driven spirals. It is: match the tool to the shock. For supply shocks, that means tools that work on supply. Buffer stocks: the U.S. release of 180 million barrels from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve in 2022 measurably calmed gasoline prices, and China runs a strategic pork reserve for the same reason; grain and critical-mineral stocks could do likewise. Targeted price interventions in crises: Spain’s “Iberian exception” — capping the price of gas used for electricity generation — helped drive Spanish inflation to roughly half the euro-area rate, and Germany’s gas price brake cushioned households; whereas Hungary’s blunt across-the-board food price caps produced shortages, black markets, and the highest inflation in the EU (peaking around 25 percent). The lesson is not “price controls work” or “price controls fail” — it’s that design and context decide, which is exactly the kind of unglamorous truth the slogan war on both sides refuses. Add windfall taxes on shock-driven excess profits, market redesign (Europe’s marginal-cost electricity pricing turned a gas shock into an everything-electric shock), and fiscal support targeted at the households actually being hurt. And keep one eye, always, on the story being told. When the next inflation comes — and it will — listen for who is being blamed, because that is the sound of someone deciding who pays.

Buffer stocks

Public reserves of essential commodities — oil, grain, pork, minerals — accumulated in calm times and released into shortages to cap price spikes at the source. The authors’ favorite example of a supply-side anti-inflation tool: it acts on the actual scarce thing, rather than suppressing the whole economy’s demand to bring one price down.

Reading it critically

The sharpest published pushback, from review essays in outlets like The Independent Review, argues that the book dismisses the monetary side too breezily: it gives little space to the Fed’s near-zero rates and massive asset purchases running alongside the 2021 stimulus, and its hyperinflation chapter concedes that money creation is a necessary precondition even while demoting it — critics read that as having it both ways. Mainstream estimates of the profit contribution to 2021–23 inflation also vary widely, and several economists attribute rising profit shares to strong demand meeting inelastic supply rather than to sellers’-inflation coordination; the 40-percent-ish figures the authors lean on are decompositions, not proofs of mechanism. Spain-versus-Hungary is likewise contested — Spain’s energy mix and LNG capacity, not just its price cap, explain part of its good outcome. Finally, the book is a 2025 snapshot wagering on a structurally inflationary future; that prediction is unfalsified but also untested. None of this dents the book’s central, durable claim — that inflation explanations carry distributional politics inside them — but readers should treat the policy chapters as an opening argument, not a settled verdict.

If you remember ten things

  1. Inflation is all prices rising together and continuing to rise; economists track the rate of change while people experience the level, and much political anger lives in that gap.
  2. There is no single true measure of inflation: baskets, weights, “core” exclusions, and housing fictions like owners’ equivalent rent are choices — and the average basket is nobody’s basket, least of all a poor household’s.
  3. Inflation comes in types — good (growth with rising wages), bad (supply-shock pass-through), and ugly (unanchored expectations) — and a cure designed for one can be useless for another.
  4. Our entire anti-inflation architecture — independent central banks, 2 percent targets, the rate-hike reflex — is the institutionalized trauma of the 1970s, applied since to a world that no longer resembles them.
  5. Volcker’s disinflation broke inflation and also broke labor, the S&Ls, and Latin America: the sacrifice ratio is a distributional choice dressed as a technical parameter.
  6. The 2021–23 inflation was, on the evidence, mainly a supply shock (pandemic plus energy war) amplified by profit-led “sellers’ inflation,” with U.S. fiscal stimulus explaining only the American increment — and the feared wage-price spiral never showed up.
  7. Hyperinflation is not late-stage CPI; it is state collapse — lost production, foreign-currency debts, broken legitimacy — and invoking Weimar against ordinary deficits is a blame-game move, not a forecast.
  8. Inflation’s burden is regressive (a consumption tax on the bottom half) while its benefits flow to debtors and to firms and shareholders with pricing power — and the rate-hike cure deliberately makes the indebted and the employed pay again.
  9. The deflationary tailwinds of 1990–2020 — China’s labor, cheap gas, optimized supply chains, a stable climate — are reversing, so supply-shock inflations will recur and demand a wider toolkit: buffer stocks, targeted and well-designed price interventions, windfall taxes, market redesign.
  10. Every inflation comes wrapped in a story about whose fault it is, and the story that wins decides who pays: when prices surge, ask first who is being blamed, and who benefits from that blame.

Concepts to keep

Inflation as distributional conflict

Price changes redistribute real income between debtors and creditors, wages and profits, young and old — and so does the policy response. Treating inflation as a neutral aggregate hides the fact that both the disease and the cure have winners and losers.

Inflation stories

Every inflation gets a narrative naming a culprit — unions, governments, central banks, corporations — and the winning narrative licenses the cure. Stories are blame-assignment machines: identify the storyteller and you have usually identified who is being set up to pay.

Cost-push vs. demand-pull

Demand-pull inflation comes from spending outrunning capacity; cost-push comes from supply shocks raising input prices. The distinction matters because interest rates address the first and mostly punish bystanders in the second — yet the policy regime barely distinguishes them.

Sellers’ (profit-led) inflation

Isabella Weber’s argument, adopted here: in moments of general cost shock, firms with pricing power raise prices beyond costs because everyone expects increases and coordination against them collapses. ECB and IMF decompositions of 2022 euro-area inflation gave the idea empirical teeth.

The sacrifice ratio

The output and unemployment cost of reducing inflation by one point — the regime’s own accounting of disinflation’s price. The book’s contribution is to keep asking the suppressed follow-up: who, concretely, does the sacrificing, and who is exempted?

Rate of change vs. price level

Economists track the derivative; people experience the level. “Inflation is back to 2 percent” and “everything still costs 25 percent more” are simultaneously true, and the gap between them is a standing source of political rage that experts persistently misread as ignorance.

The supply-shock age

Climate disruption, deglobalization, tariffs, and demographics are turning supply shocks from rare events into a regime. If so, inflation governance built solely on demand management will extract recurring sacrifice — and the toolkit must widen to reserves, buffer stocks, and supply-side investment.

In the author's words

“We were interested in who was making each one up and who was saying it, because what you’re identifying is: who caused it, and therefore who has to pay for it?” — Mark Blyth, on the book’s inflation stories, in a 2025 podcast interview about the book
Inflation does not hit everyone equally: some people are badly hurt by rising prices, while others — debtors, asset owners, firms with pricing power — can come out ahead. Knowing the mechanics is how you find out which one you are. — close paraphrase of the book’s framing of “users and losers”
When the only tool you have is the interest rate, every inflation has to be treated as if it were a demand problem — whatever actually caused it. — close paraphrase of the argument of chapter 2

Threads to other books

Seeing Like a State — James C. Scott
The CPI is a textbook Scottian instrument of legibility — a simplification that lets the state “see” prices — and Blyth’s measurement chapter shows exactly what such simplifications erase and who lives in the erased remainder.
Chip War — Chris Miller
Miller’s chokepointed supply chains are the microfoundation of Blyth’s supply-shock age: when production concentrates in a few fragile nodes, price stability becomes hostage to geopolitics, and demand-side tools can’t reach the cause.
Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital — Carlota Perez
Perez shows how each technological surge reshapes finance and macro policy; Blyth supplies the distributional accounting for the installation phase we are living through, where massive capital deployment meets constrained supply.
Influence — Robert Cialdini
Blyth’s inflation stories are persuasion operating at civilizational scale — authority, social proof, and framing deciding which economic narrative wins — and Cialdini gives you the micro-level mechanics of why the winning story sticks.
For the AI researcher

The AI buildout is now a macroeconomic event, and this book hands you the right questions about it. Hundreds of billions of dollars a year are flowing into data centers, chips, and the electricity to run them — a classic demand shock landing on supply chains (power transformers, grid interconnects, HBM memory, construction labor) that cannot expand quickly. Blyth and Fraccaroli’s framework predicts what we are already seeing: localized inflation in electricity prices near data-center clusters, and a distributional fight over who pays — ratepayers or hyperscalers — that is being narrated through competing stories in exactly the way chapter 3 describes. When utilities, politicians, and AI firms each explain rising power bills, ask the book’s question: whose story is this, and who does it bill?

The deeper application is to AI’s own future narrative. If AI meaningfully raises productivity, it is disinflationary in the long run — more supply per unit of labor — and that story will be told loudly by the industry. If it instead displaces wages while concentrating pricing power in a few compute-owning firms, the “users and losers” ledger looks very different, and sellers’-inflation dynamics could migrate into AI-mediated markets where a handful of models set prices across the economy. Both stories will be in circulation simultaneously, told by parties with stakes; the book’s discipline — trace the narrative to its beneficiary — is a genuinely transferable research skill, not unlike asking who funded a benchmark.

Finally, a practical note: your field’s funding is rate-sensitive. The 2021–23 hikes ended the era of free money that had floated speculative compute spending, and the macro regime Blyth describes — chronic supply shocks, structurally higher rates — would mean AI capex must increasingly justify itself in cash flows rather than narratives. Understanding inflation politics is, indirectly, understanding the budget constraint on your own research agenda.
