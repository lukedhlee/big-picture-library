Material Substrate

Material World: The Six Raw Materials That Shape Modern Civilization

Ed Conway · 2023
~13 hours to read Condensed read below: ~25 min read The invisible economy under the visible one Pairs with: Chip War & Energy and Civilization

If Smil is the accountant of civilization’s joules and Miller the historian of its transistors, Conway is the travel correspondent of its tonnage — the reporter who actually went down the mines, into the furnaces, and across the salt flats to see where the physical world we stopped noticing actually comes from. This is the supply-chain companion to both books: the one that shows you the rock that becomes the chip, the brine that becomes the battery, the hole in Chile that becomes the wire in your wall.

Ed Conway is the economics and data editor of Sky News and a columnist for The Times — a macroeconomics journalist who spent two decades covering GDP figures, central banks, and financial crises before noticing that almost nothing in his professional world ever touched the substances economies are actually made of. The book was born from that embarrassment. We live, he argues, increasingly in the ethereal world — services, software, brands, finance, the place where nearly all measured economic value now seems to reside — while the material world that makes it physically possible has been priced near zero, offshored out of sight, and dropped from the mental maps of the people who run things. The numbers that anchor the book are staggering precisely because nobody quotes them: in a single recent year humanity mined, dug, and blasted more material from the earth’s surface than it extracted in all of history up to 1950.

Conway’s method is shoe-leather empiricism. He descends a kilometre under the North Sea into a salt-and-potash mine, stands at the lip of Chuquicamata, walks the floor of a blast furnace at Port Talbot, tours the evaporation ponds of the Atacama, and traces a single grain of high-purity quartz from a mountainside in North Carolina through half a dozen countries until it returns as the silicon brain of a phone. Published in 2023 — into the aftermath of COVID shortages, the European gas crisis, and the chip wars — it was an Economist Book of the Year and a finalist for the FT Business Book of the Year, and it has become the standard one-volume answer to a question that suddenly matters again: what is the modern world actually made of, and who controls the places it comes from?

For this library, it is the missing middle layer: Smil explains the energy that animates civilization, Miller explains the most refined artifact civilization makes, and Conway maps everything in between — the six substances without which neither the joules nor the chips exist.

The core argument

Six materials — sand, salt, iron, copper, oil, and lithium — underpin essentially everything in modern life, from buildings to medicines to microchips to batteries. The economy that produces them is vast beyond intuition, almost perfectly invisible to the ethereal economy that sits on top of it, and concentrated in a surprisingly small number of mines, plants, and chokepoints whose failure would stop the world. And the energy transition, far from dematerializing civilization, is the biggest rematerialization in history: to stop burning fossil fuels we will have to dig more, not less.

The argument unfolds as a series of inversions of conventional wisdom. We are told the world is dematerializing — that value has migrated to ideas, services, and software. Conway’s ledger says otherwise: extraction has accelerated, not declined; the material world has simply gotten so efficient, so cheap, and so geographically remote that it slipped below the threshold of attention. The six materials are not a metaphor. Concrete and glass are processed sand; the chemical industry is processed salt; every machine and building stands on a steel skeleton; every electron travels on copper; oil and gas are not just fuels but the feedstock of plastics and fertilizer; and lithium is the substance on which the post-fossil world is being bet.

The second inversion is about fragility. Precisely because these industries are brutally capital-intensive and low-margin, they have consolidated into chokepoints: one Appalachian town with the world’s purest quartz, one Dutch company that makes the machines that make advanced chips, a handful of Chilean and Australian deposits supplying the world’s lithium, Chinese refineries processing most of it. The more sophisticated our technology becomes, the fewer the places that can produce its ingredients — modern capitalism is a teetering edifice balanced on sites most people have never heard of.

The third inversion is the one Conway most wants policymakers to hear. Net zero is usually narrated as a story of leaving the ground alone. In material terms it is the opposite: electric cars need several times the minerals of combustion cars, grids need millions of tonnes of new copper, and batteries need lithium at volumes that did not exist as an industry two decades ago. The fossil-fuel age was an energy bonanza; the transition out of it will be a materials bonanza — with all the geology, geography, and geopolitics that implies.

The book is organized into six parts, one per material, ordered roughly by the age of our relationship with each: sand, salt, iron, copper, oil, lithium. Each part runs the same arc — deep history, a journey to the places where the material is won and transformed, and a reckoning with what the material means for the energy transition. The voice throughout is Conway’s reported wonder: he goes to the mine, the furnace, the refinery, the pond, and writes down what he sees and what the people there tell him, with the macroeconomist’s habit of attaching a number to everything. This condensation follows the six parts in order, giving sand two sections (its glass-and-concrete half and its silicon half deserve separate treatment) and one section to each of the rest. Omitted are many of the historical digressions and several secondary site visits; the central journeys, places, companies, and figures are kept.

Part One · Sand

Glass, concrete, and the world built on sand

Conway opens with the oldest of our six relationships: Homo faber, man the maker, and the substance he has made the most of. Silicon and oxygen are the two most abundant elements in the earth’s crust, and their compound — silica, quartz, sand — is the raw ingredient of more of the built world than anything else. Sand and gravel are the most extracted solid materials on the planet, on the order of 50 billion tonnes a year, dwarfing every metal combined; and yet sand appears in no commodity index, trades on no exchange, and registers in the public mind only when a beach erodes. That gap — gargantuan tonnage, total invisibility — is the book’s pattern, established here in part one.

The first transformation is glass. Heat silica with soda ash and lime to around 1,500°C and you get a material civilization quietly depends on for far more than windows: lenses, laboratory vessels, fibre-optic cables, the screens between you and almost all information. Conway dwells on the strange historical contingency of glass — how Renaissance Venice guarded its Murano glassmakers like nuclear scientists, and how the lens and the flask arguably gated the scientific revolution itself: no glass, no microscope, no telescope, no test tube. The modern industry runs through a single elegant trick — the float-glass process developed by Alastair Pilkington in the 1950s, in which molten glass is poured onto a bath of molten tin and spreads into a perfectly flat ribbon. Virtually every pane of glass in the world is now made this way, another instance of one process, invented in one place, becoming a planetary monoculture.

Conway lingers on glass’s strangest property: it is the material through which we see everything else, and so the one we literally look past. The same is true economically. Float glass, container glass, fibreglass insulation, the hair-thin optical fibres that carry essentially all intercontinental data — drawn so pure that, in a famous industry comparison, a window of the same material miles thick would still be transparent — none of it registers as “tech,” yet the ethereal world is piped through melted sand. He notes too the curious chemistry that links his materials: glass will not melt at practical temperatures without a flux, and the flux is soda ash — a salt product — so every windowpane is already a marriage of part one and part two.

The second transformation is concrete: crushed rock and sand bound by cement, the most used substance on earth after water. The numbers here are the book’s first jaw-dropper. Humanity pours tens of billions of tonnes of concrete a year — several tonnes for every person alive — and China alone used more cement in some recent three-year spans than the United States poured in the entire twentieth century. Concrete is miraculous (cheap, strong in compression, mouldable, the reason billions live in cities that would otherwise be impossible) and cursed twice over: cement-making alone accounts for roughly 7–8% of global CO₂ emissions, and concrete’s appetite for sand has spawned a genuinely violent shadow economy. Desert sand, rounded smooth by wind, is useless for construction — which is why Dubai imports sand while sitting on a desert, and why “sand mafias” in India dredge rivers illegally and have murdered the officials who tried to stop them; and why Singapore, which has grown its own territory by roughly a quarter with reclaimed land, has been accused of importing whole Indonesian islands’ worth of sand. Even here, in the humblest material, Conway finds scarcity, specificity, and bodies.

Concrete also introduces the book’s time-bomb theme. Unlike stone, reinforced concrete has a lifespan: the steel rebar inside it rusts, expands, and cracks the structure from within — engineers call it “concrete cancer” — which means the twentieth century’s building binge is simultaneously a maintenance liability the twenty-first has barely begun to price. We have poured more concrete since 1950 than in all prior history, much of it now entering middle age. The material world, Conway keeps insisting, is not a stock of solved problems; it is a flow that must be perpetually renewed, by industries nobody watches.

The part’s underlying lesson: a material can be common as dirt in general and chokepoint-rare in the particular grade you need. That lesson sets up the most extreme case of all — the sand that becomes silicon.

Part One · Sand, continued

The longest journey: from a Carolina mountainside to a microchip

The chapter Conway calls “The Longest Journey” is the book’s set piece, and the one readers of Chip War should put beside Miller’s account. It begins in Spruce Pine, North Carolina — a small Appalachian town that happens to sit on the purest deposit of quartz ever found. The quartz matters not because chips are made from it directly, but because of the crucibles: to grow the flawless single crystals of silicon that become wafers, you must melt hyper-refined silicon in a vessel that will not contaminate it, and only quartz of Spruce Pine’s purity will do. Two companies — the Belgian group Sibelco and The Quartz Corp — mine and process it, and the operation is wrapped in industrial secrecy Conway clearly relishes reporting: at one facility, he is told, visiting technicians were blindfolded and led to the machines they had come to fix, so that no outsider would see the process whole. Essentially every semiconductor fab on earth depends on crucibles traceable to this one town. (The point stopped being a curiosity in September 2024, after this book was published, when Hurricane Helene flooded Spruce Pine and the world’s chipmakers briefly held their breath.)

Chokepoint

A node in a supply chain where production is so concentrated — one town’s quartz, one company’s lithography machines, one country’s refining capacity — that its failure would propagate through the entire material world. Conway’s rule of thumb: the more advanced the product, the more specialized its inputs, and the fewer the places that can supply them.

From Spruce Pine the journey accelerates into what Conway argues is the most extraordinary manufacturing chain humans have ever built. Quartzite is smelted in electric furnaces into silicon metal at around 98–99% purity — fine for aluminium alloys, hopeless for electronics. Chemistry takes over: reacted with hydrochloric acid (whose chlorine, Conway notes, comes from salt — the parts of this book interlock) into trichlorosilane gas, distilled, and re-deposited as polysilicon at purities the industry counts in “nines” — 99.9999999% and beyond, the purest material humans make in bulk. Then the crystal: in the Czochralski process — named for Jan Czochralski, the Polish chemist who discovered it in 1916, by legend after absent-mindedly dipping his pen into molten tin instead of his inkwell — a seed crystal is dipped into molten silicon inside a Spruce Pine quartz crucible and drawn slowly upward, rotating, so that a single perfect crystal grows beneath it: a gleaming ingot weighing a couple of hundred kilograms, sliced by diamond wire into wafers polished flatter than any mirror.

Each stage is its own oligopoly. The polysilicon plants cluster where electricity is cheap — historically Germany (Wacker) and the United States, increasingly Xinjiang, with all the political complications that entails. The crystal-pulling and wafer-slicing business is dominated by two Japanese firms, Shin-Etsu and SUMCO, whose 300-millimetre wafers — each eventually diced into hundreds of chips — are polished to a flatness measured in atoms. At every step the tolerances tighten and the number of qualified suppliers shrinks, until entire stages of the journey have only one or two doors the world’s electronics must pass through.

Then the fab. Conway compresses what Miller spends a book on: the wafers travel — typically to Taiwan, where TSMC makes roughly 90% of the world’s most advanced chips — to be patterned by lithography machines that only one company on earth can build. ASML of Veldhoven, in the Netherlands, makes the extreme-ultraviolet machines, each costing on the order of $150–200 million, in which droplets of molten tin are hit by a laser tens of thousands of times a second to create a plasma emitting 13.5-nanometre light, focused by Zeiss mirrors so smooth that scaled to the size of a country their largest imperfection would be measured in millimetres. By the time a finished chip reaches a consumer it has crossed dozens of borders and travelled tens of thousands of miles, gaining value at every step — from sand worth pennies to wafers worth more than their weight in gold many times over.

Conway’s point in lavishing this detail is not gee-whizzery. It is that nobody is in charge of this. No government designed the silicon journey; no single firm comprehends every step; it assembled itself over decades through specialization and trade, and it is simultaneously the strongest argument for globalization ever built and the most fragile artifact of it. The journey from Spruce Pine to your pocket is, he suggests, the closest thing the material world has to a miracle — and the clearest demonstration that the ethereal world’s crown jewel, the chip, is at bottom a very, very refined grain of sand.

Part Two · Salt

Salt: the mother substance of chemistry — and of gunpowder

Salt looks like the quaint entry on the list — the stuff of Roman salary legends and medieval trade routes — and Conway plays with that expectation before demolishing it. Yes, the deep history is here: salt as preservative was the difference between surviving winter and not, salt taxes funded empires and helped trigger revolutions (Gandhi’s 1930 Salt March being the canonical modern case), and the world is honeycombed with the legacy of brine — Conway’s native Britain included, where Cheshire’s salt country has been mined since Roman times and the land still subsides over old workings. But of the roughly 300 million tonnes of salt produced annually, only a small fraction is eaten. The real story of salt is that it is the chemical industry’s mother substance.

The pre-chemical history matters because it establishes the pattern the rest of the book repeats. Salt was the first substance governments learned to weaponize fiscally and strategically: France’s hated gabelle helped light the fuse of the Revolution; British salt taxes in India gave Gandhi his most legible grievance; and wherever salt could be made — coastal pans, brine springs, rock-salt seams — towns, roads, and fortunes followed. Cheshire’s brine fed the alkali works of Merseyside, which is a large part of why the chemical industry — and arguably industrial chemistry itself, through firms that eventually merged into ICI — grew up in England’s northwest. The lesson Conway draws: every age has its “boring” strategic substance, and the people of that age are always astonished, afterward, that wars and revolutions turned on it.

The hinge is the chlor-alkali process: run electricity through brine and it splits into chlorine, caustic soda, and hydrogen — three of the foundational reagents of industrial civilization. Follow the chlorine and you reach PVC pipes and window frames, disinfected drinking water, pharmaceuticals, pesticides, and — closing the loop with part one — the hydrochloric acid used to refine silicon for chips and solar panels. Follow the caustic soda and you reach soap, paper, aluminium refining, and more. Add salt’s sibling soda ash, the flux that makes glass meltable, and Conway’s claim comes into focus: strip out the products that depend somewhere on salt chemistry and you lose something like half the shelves of the modern economy. Salt, he writes, is the magic ingredient with which we transform our world. The reporting set piece is one of the book’s best: Conway descends roughly 1.1 kilometres at Boulby on England’s northeast coast — Britain’s deepest mine — into hot, dark tunnels of salt and polyhalite that run for miles out beneath the North Sea, where the fertilizer of the future is cut from the evaporated bed of an ancient ocean.

Then the part takes its dark turn, into the salts that explode. Gunpowder — the “fire drug,” in the literal translation of its Chinese name — is charcoal, sulphur, and saltpeter, and saltpeter (potassium nitrate) was for centuries the strategic substance: scraped from dung heaps and cellar walls by royal “petermen,” imported from India by the East India Company, the constraint on every army’s ability to fight. In the nineteenth century the world’s nitrogen supply concentrated in one place: the caliche deposits of the Atacama desert, the only large natural nitrate beds on earth. Chile, Peru, and Bolivia fought the War of the Pacific (1879–1884) substantially over them — Bolivia losing its entire coastline in the process — and for decades Chilean nitrates fertilized the world’s fields and filled its shells, until the deposits’ ghost towns, like Humberstone, were left to the desert.

What broke the Atacama monopoly was chemistry’s most consequential invention: Fritz Haber’s 1909 synthesis of ammonia from air, industrialized by Carl Bosch at BASF from 1913. Haber–Bosch is the book’s recurring moral knot. It feeds roughly half of humanity — without synthetic nitrogen the present world population could not exist — and it also freed Germany from the British nitrate blockade and lengthened the First World War; Haber himself went on to pioneer chemical weapons. The same molecule, ammonium nitrate, is fertilizer in one context and a bomb in another — a duality written in disasters from BASF’s own Oppau plant in 1921 to the 2,750 tonnes that levelled the port of Beirut in 2020. Salt’s lesson generalizes the book’s thesis backward in time: control of an “unremarkable” substance has always been strategic; we have simply forgotten because, for now, the substances are cheap.

Part Three · Iron

Iron: the skeleton of civilization, and the hardest habit to break

Iron is the most-used metal on earth by an enormous margin — roughly 95% of all metal smelted — and its alloy, steel, is so ubiquitous that Conway frames it as civilization’s skeleton: the rebar in the concrete, the frame of every car, ship, machine tool, pipeline, wind turbine, and transmission tower, the hidden tonnage in every building you have ever entered. The world now produces close to two billion tonnes of steel a year, more than half of it in China — which in some recent two-year periods poured more steel than Britain, the industry’s birthplace, has produced since the Industrial Revolution. Steel is also the material where Conway’s reporting is most elegiac, because he watches the West’s capacity to make it from scratch flickering out in real time.

The deep history is compressed but pointed. Iron was worked for three millennia as a craft metal — smelted in bloomeries, hammered by smiths — but steel, iron’s harder, springier alloy with a whisker of carbon, remained a luxury until Henry Bessemer’s converter of 1856 made it possible to blow a batch of molten iron into steel in minutes rather than days. Cheap steel is, more than almost any other single input, what the late nineteenth century was made of: rails, ships, skyscraper frames, artillery — and Andrew Carnegie’s fortune, built on the insight that whoever made steel cheapest would own the age. The twentieth century’s wars were steel wars (Conway notes that access to ore — Sweden’s Kiruna magnetite among it — shaped strategy in both world wars), and the twenty-first century’s industrial league table is still, at bottom, a steel table, with China at the top by an order of magnitude.

The set piece is his visit to the blast furnaces of Port Talbot in South Wales — the chapter he calls “Inside the Volcano.” A blast furnace is one of the few industrial processes that would be recognizable to an eighteenth-century ironmaster, scaled to the sublime: a tower continuously fed with iron ore, coke, and limestone, blasted with superheated air, running uninterrupted for a decade or two at a time because letting it cool would crack its lining, tapping rivers of molten iron at around 1,500°C. Conway stands close enough to feel it and writes about the place with the awe of a war correspondent — and with the knowledge (vindicated after publication, when Port Talbot’s last blast furnaces closed in 2024) that Britain was about to become unable to make virgin steel at all, dependent on remelting scrap and on furnaces elsewhere. The ore itself comes from places like the LKAB mine at Kiruna in Swedish Lapland — a deposit so rich and so vast that the entire town above it is being moved, building by building, three kilometres sideways to keep the mine growing — and from the iron ranges of Australia and Brazil that feed China’s mills.

Why does this rate a sixth of the book? Because steel is where decarbonization gets brutally hard, and Conway wants the reader to understand the chemistry of why. Coke in a blast furnace is not merely fuel; it is the reductant — the carbon that strips oxygen atoms from iron oxide, leaving metallic iron and, unavoidably, CO₂. Roughly two tonnes of carbon dioxide accompany each tonne of primary steel, and steelmaking alone is responsible for about 7–8% of global emissions, comparable to all of cement. You cannot fix it by changing the fuel, because the carbon is in the recipe. The escape routes exist but are narrow: recycling scrap in electric-arc furnaces (already about a quarter to a third of production, but limited by how much scrap exists — a fast-building country like China generates far less than it needs); or replacing coke with hydrogen as the reductant, as Sweden’s HYBRIT venture (SSAB, LKAB, and Vattenfall) demonstrated in 2021 with the world’s first fossil-free steel, delivered ceremonially to Volvo — a genuine breakthrough that nonetheless requires staggering quantities of cheap clean electricity to scale. The chapter’s title phrase — “you don’t have a country” — echoes the blunt slogan of steel protectionists (Donald Trump tweeted the line in 2018: if you don’t have steel, you don’t have a country), and Conway treats it with more sympathy than a free-trade economics journalist might be expected to: the pandemic and the Ukraine war made the strategic case for keeping some volcanoes lit harder to dismiss.

Part Four · Copper

Copper: electricity’s metal and the coming squeeze

If steel is the skeleton, copper is the nervous system: the best practical conductor of electricity (only silver beats it, and not by enough to matter at scale), the metal in every wire, motor winding, transformer, undersea cable, and circuit board on earth. Conway frames the part with the story of electrification itself — the chapter title “The Next Greatest Thing” borrows the words of a Tennessee farmer at the arrival of rural electrification, who said the greatest thing was to have the love of God in your heart, and the next greatest was to have electricity in your house. Everything the modern world calls progress travels down a copper wire; and everything the energy transition proposes — electrify cars, electrify heat, electrify industry — is, materially, a proposal to use vastly more copper.

Copper’s modern career began with communication, not power: the telegraph wires of the 1840s, then the first transatlantic cables, then the great copper rush that Edison’s Pearl Street station and the electrification of cities turned into one of history’s defining commodity booms — the boom that made Montana’s Butte “the richest hill on earth” and the Guggenheim and Anaconda fortunes with it. The metal’s grip has never loosened because its job cannot be substituted at scale: aluminium can carry high-voltage lines, but in motors, transformers, and dense wiring, copper’s conductivity-per-volume keeps it irreplaceable. A typical home contains perhaps 100–200 kilograms of it, almost all invisible behind walls; the grid contains millions of tonnes; and the sea floor is veined with copper-cored cables carrying both the internet and, increasingly, offshore wind power ashore.

The journey goes to Chile, which produces more copper than any other country, and to the Atacama again — the same desert that supplied the world’s nitrates now supplying its conductor. The emotional center is Chuquicamata, “The Hole”: the largest open pit ever excavated, mined for over a century, nearly a kilometre deep, the mine whose copper — under American owners Anaconda and Guggenheim capital, then nationalized under Allende into state-owned Codelco — electrified the twentieth century. (A young Che Guevara’s visit to Chuquicamata in 1952, recorded in The Motorcycle Diaries, radicalized him; the mine has a way of appearing at history’s hinges.) Chuquicamata’s pit has grown so deep that hauling rock out costs more than the rock is worth, and the mine is now converting itself, at a cost of billions, into one of the world’s largest underground operations. Down the road, BHP’s Escondida — the world’s most productive copper mine — supplies on the order of 5% of global output by itself.

Ore-grade decline

A century ago miners worked copper ores of 2–3% metal or richer; today the average grade at major mines is around 0.6% and falling. Every percentage point lost means exponentially more rock blasted, hauled, crushed, and leached — more energy, more water, more waste — per tonne of metal. The cheapest, richest deposits of every major metal have already been found and eaten; what remains is more rock for less metal, forever.

The arithmetic of grade decline is the part’s analytical core, and Conway makes the reader feel it physically: at 0.6% grade, winning a single tonne of copper means moving and pulverizing hundreds of tonnes of rock — whole cathedral-volumes of the Andes ground to dust, leached with acid, and electro-refined into cathodes of 99.99% purity. Copper mining disturbs more of the planet’s surface than the extraction of any other metal. And demand is about to explode: an electric car contains roughly three to four times the copper of a combustion car; offshore wind farms, grid reinforcement, and datacenters all bid for the same cathodes; by some widely cited industry estimates, the world will need to mine as much copper in the next quarter-century as it has mined in all of history. Conway visits the frontier where that pressure is pushing — deeper underground, into lower grades, and toward the genuinely novel: the polymetallic nodules of the deep Pacific seabed, copper and nickel and cobalt lying loose on the abyssal plain, whose harvesting might spare the Andes at the price of an ecosystem nobody yet understands. The part ends on the book’s recurring discomfort: there is no decarbonization without copper, and there is no copper without holes.

Part Five · Oil

Oil: the fuel everyone sees, the feedstock nobody does

Oil is the one material on Conway’s list nobody forgets — it has its own literature, its own wars, its own price ticker on the evening news — so his contribution is deliberately oblique: to show the reader the parts of oil and gas that aren’t fuel, and to insist that this is the part the energy transition has no plan for. The world burns on the order of 100 million barrels a day, and the geography of that flow still runs through the “elephants” — the supergiant fields, above all Ghawar in Saudi Arabia, the largest conventional oilfield ever found, whose crude streams to the export terminal at Ras Tanura on the Gulf, the single most consequential piece of infrastructure in the hydrocarbon world. Gas has its twin in Qatar’s Ras Laffan, perched on the mammoth North Field. Conway tells the discovery stories — Drake’s well, Spindletop, the Anglo-Persian gamble — briskly, because Yergin told them definitively; his interest is in where the molecules go now.

Why did oil conquer everything? Conway gives the Smilian answer in passing — energy density (roughly 45 megajoules per kilogram, far beyond coal, vastly beyond any battery), liquidity (it pumps, pours, and ships), and sheer abundance once the supergiants were found — before adding his own: chemical promiscuity. Crude is not one substance but thousands of hydrocarbons of every length, which means a refinery is less a fuel factory than a molecular sorting house, and the twentieth century learned to find a use for every fraction. That versatility is why oil’s tendrils reach places no pipeline map shows: the average person in a rich country is dressed partly in oil (polyester), walks on oil (asphalt, shoe soles), eats food grown with gas-derived nitrogen, and weathered a pandemic behind oil-derived masks, gowns, and syringes.

Where they go, in surprising measure, is into things. Conway tours the refinery — he reports from Shell’s giant Rhineland complex at Wesseling, near Cologne — and renders it as the material world’s grand central station: a city of pipes where crude is boiled and fractionated by molecular weight into gases, naphtha, kerosene, diesel, and bitumen, each stream feeding a different limb of civilization. Follow the naphtha and you reach the steam crackers, where long hydrocarbons are split into ethylene and propylene — the Lego bricks of every plastic: polyethylene bags and pipes, polypropylene car parts, PVC (chlorine from salt meeting ethylene from oil — the materials marry again), polyester clothing, the resins in wind-turbine blades and the insulation on copper wires. Follow the natural gas and you reach Haber–Bosch: the hydrogen in synthetic ammonia is stripped from methane, which means the world’s bread is, at one remove, baked from natural gas. Petrochemicals, fertilizers, lubricants, asphalt, pharmaceuticals — a barrel of oil is dismembered into thousands of products, and the no-burn future still needs most of them.

The “Pipes” half of the part, written in the shadow of Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, is Conway’s demonstration that the material world can reassert itself in months. Europe had built its prosperity on cheap pipeline gas — from Groningen in the Netherlands (discovered 1959, now being shut down because extraction was causing earthquakes) and, fatefully, from Siberia. When the Russian flows stopped and the Nord Stream pipelines were sabotaged in September 2022, the ethereal world rediscovered, at extraordinary speed and cost, that fertilizer plants shut when gas is expensive, that glass and steel and chemicals follow, and that LNG terminals cannot be conjured by fiscal policy. Energy crises, Conway notes, are really materials crises wearing a price tag. The part closes with his characteristic both-hands honesty: oil is the incumbent that must be dethroned, and it is also — in sheer energy density, portability, and chemical versatility — the most extraordinary substance humans have ever pulled from the ground, which is precisely why replacing it is the hardest thing we have ever attempted.

Part Six · Lithium

Lithium: white gold and the rematerialized future

The final part is the youngest relationship on the list — a metal that was an industrial footnote (grease, glass additives, mood stabilizers) until the lithium-ion battery made it the pivotal substance of the twenty-first century. Conway tells the battery’s invention as a three-act, three-continent story honored by the 2019 Nobel Prize in Chemistry: Stanley Whittingham’s first lithium battery at Exxon in the 1970s (an oil company midwifing its own disruptor, then dropping it), John Goodenough’s cobalt-oxide cathode at Oxford in 1980, Akira Yoshino’s safe carbon anode at Asahi Kasei in 1985 — and commercialization by Sony in 1991, because Japan’s camcorder industry wanted lighter batteries. Lithium is the lightest metal and ferociously electropositive: per kilogram, nothing rechargeable stores energy better, which is why the entire electric-vehicle bet rests on it and why demand is projected to grow by an order of magnitude or more over the coming decades.

Then Conway goes to where it comes from, and the Atacama completes its trilogy: the desert that gave the world its nitrates and much of its copper also holds, beneath the blinding white crust of the Salar de Atacama, a vast underground reservoir of lithium-rich brine. SQM — the privatized heir of Chile’s nitrate industry, with all the political baggage that implies — and the American firm Albemarle pump the brine into chains of evaporation ponds, turquoise rectangles visible from space, where the desert sun concentrates it over roughly eighteen months into lithium carbonate. It is among the cheapest lithium on earth, and among the most contested: this is one of the driest places on the planet, and the Atacameño communities at the salar’s edge dispute every cubic metre of water and brine the industry takes. The other pole of supply is Greenbushes in Western Australia — a nineteenth-century tin mine reborn as the world’s largest hard-rock lithium operation, blasting spodumene ore from an open pit. Australia mines roughly half the world’s lithium; and nearly all of its spodumene sails to one destination.

A battery, Conway is at pains to show, is not lithium alone but a small material world of its own: graphite anodes (another Chinese-dominated supply), cathodes blending nickel, manganese, and cobalt — the last mined overwhelmingly in the Democratic Republic of Congo, under conditions that haunt the industry — or, increasingly, cheaper iron-phosphate chemistries that trade energy density for cost and abundance; copper and aluminium foils; electrolytes and separators. Each chemistry choice reroutes a global supply chain, which is why cathode formulations are followed in Beijing and Washington with the attention once reserved for missile telemetry. And the quantities are new in kind: a single EV battery pack contains on the order of eight to ten kilograms of lithium and tens of kilograms of other battery metals, multiplied by tens of millions of vehicles a year.

That destination is the part’s geopolitical punchline. China saw the battery age coming a decade before the West did, and built the middle of the supply chain while everyone else admired the ends: Chinese refineries convert the majority of the world’s lithium into battery-grade chemicals, and Chinese firms dominate cathodes, anodes, electrolytes, and cells — with CATL, a company few Westerners could name, the largest battery maker on earth, and BYD vertically integrated from lithium to finished car. The gigafactory — Tesla’s coinage for its Nevada plant — has become the unit of industrial ambition, and most of the world’s gigafactory capacity is Chinese. Conway’s closing arithmetic is the book’s thesis in miniature: an electric car requires roughly six times the mineral inputs of a conventional one (lithium, copper, nickel, cobalt, graphite, rare earths); a wind farm needs multiples of the metal of a gas plant per unit of power. The energy transition is not a dematerialization but a rematerialization — a one-time, civilization-scale replacement of fuel flows with metal stocks. His conclusion is neither doomy nor triumphant: the material world has always delivered when called, but only when noticed, invested in, and given the decade-plus lead times that mines and refineries demand. The first step, the book’s whole purpose, is simply to see it again.

Reading it critically

Three published lines of criticism are worth keeping beside the book. First, the six-materials frame is admittedly arbitrary at the edges: cobalt, nickel, graphite, and the rare earths — each with supply politics at least as fraught as lithium’s — appear only in asides, and reviewers noted that Conway’s selection criterion (“hard to imagine modern civilization without them”) quietly assumes a consuming reader in the global north rather than the producing communities his journeys pass through. Second, the book trades analytic depth for reportorial breadth: economists pointed out that it has relatively little to say about how prices, substitution, and innovation have historically dissolved exactly these scarcity panics (the lithium price collapse of 2023–24, right after publication, made the point for them), and that gripping travelogue sometimes stands in where a framework should be. Third, the framing is recognizably a product of its 2020–2022 moment — COVID shortages, the gas crisis, chip-war anxiety — and reads at times as Western supply-chain alarm rather than neutral description; Chinese industrial policy appears mostly as threat, rarely as the demand-creating force that made several of these industries investable at all. None of this undoes the reporting, which is the book’s durable core; it just dates the mood music.

If you remember ten things

  1. Six materials — sand, salt, iron, copper, oil, lithium — sit under essentially everything modern, and in 2019 humanity extracted more raw material than in all of history up to 1950.
  2. The “ethereal world” of services and software did not replace the material world; it just made it so cheap and remote that we stopped seeing it.
  3. A material can be globally common and chokepoint-rare in the grade that matters: desert sand can’t make concrete, and only Spruce Pine’s quartz can make semiconductor crucibles.
  4. The silicon chip’s journey — Appalachian quartz, nine-nines polysilicon, Czochralski crystals, ASML’s tin-plasma light, TSMC’s fabs — is the most refined supply chain humans have built, and nobody designed or fully comprehends it.
  5. Salt is the chemical industry’s mother substance: chlor-alkali chemistry touches roughly half of manufactured products, and the nitrate salts of the Atacama fed and armed the world until Haber–Bosch synthesized both bread and bombs from air.
  6. Steel is brutally hard to decarbonize because coke is the reductant, not just the fuel — the CO₂ is in the recipe — and steel plus cement account for roughly 15% of global emissions.
  7. Copper ore grades have fallen from 2–3% to around 0.6%, so every new tonne of electricity’s metal costs more rock, energy, and water than the last — just as electrification multiplies demand.
  8. Oil is a feedstock as much as a fuel: plastics, fertilizer (via natural gas and Haber–Bosch), and thousands of products flow from refineries that the no-burn future still needs.
  9. The energy transition is a rematerialization: EVs need roughly six times the minerals of conventional cars, and the lithium chain runs from Atacama brine and Australian spodumene through Chinese refineries that process most of the world’s supply.
  10. Mines and refineries take a decade or more to build; the material world delivers, but only on material timescales — and only if the ethereal world remembers it exists.

Concepts to keep

The ethereal world vs. the material world

Conway’s framing device: measured economic value has migrated to services, software, and finance, while the physical economy that makes them possible became cheap, consolidated, offshore, and invisible. The error is mistaking invisibility for irrelevance — every crisis since 2020 has been the material world reasserting itself.

Chokepoint geography

Advanced products depend on hyper-specialized inputs that only a handful of sites can supply: Spruce Pine’s quartz, ASML’s lithography, TSMC’s fabs, China’s refining. Sophistication concentrates; concentration creates systemic risk that no individual market participant prices.

Ore-grade decline

The best deposits get mined first, so average grades fall — copper from 2–3% a century ago to ~0.6% now — meaning more rock, energy, water, and waste per tonne of metal over time. The material world’s version of diminishing returns, running quietly underneath every electrification forecast.

Rematerialization

The energy transition replaces fuel flows (burn oil continuously) with material stocks (build batteries, turbines, and grids once, from several times the minerals). Net zero is therefore a mining and refining boom before it is anything else — the opposite of the dematerialization story the ethereal world tells itself.

The reductant problem

In steelmaking, carbon doesn’t just heat the furnace; it chemically strips oxygen from iron ore. You cannot decarbonize the process by swapping fuels — you must change the chemistry itself (hydrogen reduction, electrolysis) or recycle scrap. The template for why “just electrify it” fails for heavy industry.

The longest journey

Conway’s name for the sand-to-silicon chain, and a general lens: trace any modern artifact backward and you find a self-assembled, unplanned, multi-continent itinerary of refinement that no actor designed or fully understands — globalization’s deepest achievement and its least insurable risk.

“In 2019 we mined, dug and blasted more materials from the earth’s surface than the sum total of everything we extracted from the dawn of humanity all the way through to 1950.” — Ed Conway, Material World, Introduction
“Year after year, billions of tonnes of rock have been torn from the ground and refined into hundreds of thousands of tonnes of pure metal. From this hole which few in the world have heard of, let alone seen, came the copper that helped power the twentieth century.” — Ed Conway, on Chuquicamata, Part Four
Salt, in Conway’s telling, is “the magic ingredient” that helps us transform our world — not the seasoning on the table but the feedstock of half of industrial chemistry. — Ed Conway, Part Two, on salt as the mother substance
His recurring observation, stated in many forms: the more advanced our technology becomes, the more specialised the facilities needed to produce it — until a small number of mines, factories and labs hold up the whole edifice of modern capitalism. — close paraphrase of a theme running through the book

Threads to other books

Energy and Civilization — Vaclav Smil
Smil supplies the physics under Conway’s reporting: steel, cement, ammonia, and plastics are his four material pillars too, and his power-density and embodied-energy accounting explains why Conway’s six industries are so big, so dirty, and so slow to change.
Chip War — Chris Miller
Conway’s “longest journey” is the upstream half of Miller’s story: the quartz, polysilicon, and Czochralski crystals that exist before the fab. Read together, they trace one unbroken chain from a Carolina mountainside to EUV lithography — and show that the chip chokepoints start in the ground.
The Prize — Daniel Yergin
Yergin wrote the definitive biography of one of Conway’s six; Conway returns the favor by showing what Yergin’s epic leaves offstage — the barrel as feedstock rather than fuel, and oil as just one member of a family of strategic substances whose politics rhyme across centuries.
Austerity — Mark Blyth
Blyth shows how macroeconomic ideas can detach from reality and still govern policy; Conway shows the deepest version of that detachment — a discipline measuring GDP in services and flows while the tonnage that underwrites it all goes uncounted, until a gas crisis or a chip shortage forces the ledger open.
Seeing Like a State — James C. Scott
Scott’s legibility problem runs through Conway’s material world in reverse: states simplified what they could see and lost what they couldn’t, and the modern state’s map — prices, indices, national accounts — renders the supply chains it depends on almost perfectly illegible until they break.
For the AI researcher

Read this book as the bill of materials for your field. A frontier datacenter is, in Conway’s terms, a material object of the first order: tens of thousands of tonnes of steel and concrete (sand and iron), thousands of tonnes of copper in busbars, transformers, and cabling (the same cathodes the EV industry is bidding for), banks of lithium-ion UPS batteries, diesel generators, water for cooling in exactly the kinds of dry, cheap-land places that don’t have it — and, at its heart, silicon that passed through every chokepoint in the longest journey: Spruce Pine crucibles, nine-nines polysilicon, Czochralski ingots, ASML’s machines, TSMC’s fabs. The AI buildout of the 2020s is not cloud-shaped; it is mine-shaped, and the hyperscalers’ capex lines are increasingly claims on copper, turbines, and grid steel with decade-long lead times.

The transferable analytic is chokepoint thinking. Conway’s rule — sophistication concentrates supply — describes your dependency graph exactly: one quartz town (closed for weeks by Hurricane Helene in 2024), one lithography firm, one leading-edge foundry on one island, one country refining most of the world’s battery chemicals. AI researchers tend to model compute as a scalar that money buys; Conway’s book is the argument that compute is a supply chain with single points of failure, and that systemic risk lives precisely in the nodes nobody in the ethereal world is paid to watch. If you are thinking about AI timelines, the binding constraints he documents — ore grades, refinery permits, transformer backlogs — belong in the model alongside scaling laws.

Finally, take the rematerialization thesis personally. Your field’s story about itself is the ethereal world’s story in its purest form — intelligence as weightless abstraction. Conway’s ledger says every abstraction layer is purchased with tonnage, and the honest question his book leaves an AI researcher is the one Smil would also ask: what is the material cost per unit of capability, who is paying it, where — and would you notice if one small town in North Carolina flooded?
