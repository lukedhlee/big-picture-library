Capital & Venture

The Power Law: Venture Capital and the Making of the New Future

Sebastian Mallaby · 2022
~13 hours to read Condensed read below: ~35 min read The definitive history of the money behind technology Pairs with: Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital

Every technology you study, every lab you might one day spin out of, every startup that recruits your classmates exists inside a financial machine with its own strange logic. This book is the best account ever written of that machine — where it came from, why it works the way it does, and why it keeps producing both miracles and disasters.

Sebastian Mallaby is an unusual chronicler of Silicon Valley: a former Economist and Washington Post journalist and a senior fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations, he had already written the standard history of hedge funds (More Money Than God) and a doorstop biography of Alan Greenspan before turning to venture capital. He spent roughly five years on this book and conducted hundreds of interviews — with the partners of Sequoia, Kleiner Perkins, Accel, Benchmark, and Andreessen Horowitz, and with the founders they funded and sometimes fired. The result reads less like business journalism than like diplomatic history: careful about sources, skeptical of legends, and genuinely curious about a question most Valley hagiography never asks.

That question is whether venture capitalists actually possess skill, or whether they are lottery winners who have retroactively constructed a mythology of judgment. Mallaby’s answer is nuanced — luck matters enormously, and path dependence compounds early wins — but he concludes that the craft is real, and that it consists of something subtler than stock-picking: building networks, structuring incentives, and having the stomach to back ideas that sound ridiculous.

The book arrived in 2022, just as the longest venture bull market in history was cresting and breaking, and it has become the reference point for arguments about how innovation gets financed. If you want to understand why AI labs raise billions on no revenue, why founders now hold power that would have been unthinkable in 1980, and why governments from Brussels to Beijing keep trying and failing to clone Sand Hill Road, this is the place to start.

The core argument

Venture returns are not normally distributed; they follow a power law in which a tiny number of outlier wins generate nearly all the profit. This single statistical fact explains the entire culture of venture capital: its tolerance for failure, its obsession with grand-slam outcomes, its herd behavior, and its governance pathologies. And because VCs choose which improbable futures get funded, the power law has quietly shaped which technologies exist at all.

The mechanism is worth dwelling on, because it inverts the intuitions of ordinary finance. In a normal distribution — the returns of a bond portfolio, say — you make money by avoiding mistakes, and the worst outcome is bounded. In a power-law distribution, mistakes barely matter: an investment can only fall to zero, but a single Google can return ten thousand times its cost. A venture investor who avoids every bad deal but misses the one transcendent deal has failed; one who makes seven write-offs and one Genentech has triumphed. Rationality, in this world, means systematically courting what looks like recklessness.

From that seed, Mallaby grows an institutional history. The structures of venture capital — the time-limited fund, the equity-only stake, financing released in stages against milestones, the board seat, the recruiting network — were all invented, one by one, by identifiable people solving identifiable problems. None of it was inevitable. Boston had the first venture firm and lost the industry; Japan and Europe had more capital and never built one. The Valley won because its financiers stumbled into a structure that matched the statistics of innovation, and because each success enriched the network that sourced the next one.

The book’s second, quieter argument is about governance. For its first four decades, venture capital disciplined founders — coaching them, replacing them, tranching their money. After Google, the power balance flipped: capital became abundant, founders became scarce, and “founder-friendly” became a sales pitch. Mallaby treats the unicorn catastrophes of the 2010s — Uber, WeWork, Theranos — not as morality tales about individual bad actors but as predictable consequences of removing the adult supervision the power law had once funded.

Mallaby’s thesis is in the title: venture returns do not follow the bell curve that governs most of finance, where outcomes cluster around an average. They follow a power law, in which a tiny number of grand-slam investments return more than everything else combined, and the average outcome is meaningless. From that single statistical fact he derives the psychology, the contracts, and the history of the industry — why venture capitalists must chase improbable, even absurd, ideas; why they tolerate a strike-out rate that would end careers anywhere else; and why the firms that internalize the power law have, deal by deal, manufactured the modern technology economy. The book is a chronological history, from a 1957 mutiny in Mountain View to the WeWork implosion of 2019, told through the people who invented each new financial move. This condensation follows the book’s own fourteen-chapter arc, folding the introduction into the first section and keeping the deal mechanics — who put in how much, for what stake, with what governance — because for Mallaby the mechanics are the history. The appendix of quantitative evidence and some repeated minor episodes are omitted.

Introduction & Chapter 1

Unreasonable people: Arthur Rock and liberation capital

The book opens with a question: where do new futures come from? Mallaby’s answer is that they come from people unreasonable enough to attempt the improbable — and from a financial technology built to back them. He reaches for an old analogy: nineteenth-century New Bedford whaling, where agents pooled money from passive investors, recruited captains and crews with profit shares rather than wages, and accepted that a third of voyages would lose money because a few would return fortunes. Whaling returns, like venture returns, followed a power law. The deep structure of venture capital — outside capital, hands-on intermediaries, equity upside for the talent, a portfolio of long-shot bets — is that structure reborn for technology.

The founding act takes place in 1957. Eight young scientists at Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory in Mountain View — among them Robert Noyce, Gordon Moore, and Eugene Kleiner — could no longer endure William Shockley, a Nobel laureate who posted salaries on the wall, subjected staff to lie-detector tests, and steered the lab away from the silicon transistors they believed in. They wanted to leave together, but in the America of 1957 there was no mechanism for eight employees to simply become owners. Kleiner sent a letter to his father’s broker at the New York firm Hayden, Stone & Co., where it landed on the desk of a young analyst named Arthur Rock. Rock flew out, met the eight over dinner, and reframed their problem: don’t look for an employer, start a company. He and his colleague Bud Coyle pitched roughly thirty-five established corporations on funding the defectors; all declined — backing runaway employees was unseemly. Finally Sherman Fairchild, an inventor-heir who was IBM’s largest individual shareholder, agreed: his Fairchild Camera and Instrument would put up $1.38 million to launch Fairchild Semiconductor, with each of the eight founders buying in for $500, Hayden, Stone taking a block of shares, and Fairchild Camera holding an option to buy everyone out for $3 million.

Mallaby calls what Rock invented liberation capital: money whose purpose is to free talent from organizations that fail to value it, converting employees into founders. The “Traitorous Eight” built the company that seeded virtually the entire semiconductor industry — and when Fairchild Camera exercised its buyout in 1959, each founder cleared roughly a quarter of a million dollars on a $500 stake, a several-hundred-fold return that taught the Valley its founding lesson: defection pays. The deal’s flaw was equally instructive. Because Fairchild Camera owned everything after the buyout, the engineers reverted to salarymen, and they defected again — “Fairchildren” spun out across the Valley. In 1968 Noyce and Moore left to found Intel, and Rock, now operating in San Francisco, raised $2.5 million for them in roughly two days on the strength of a bare-bones prospectus and his own credibility. Intel spread stock options deep into the workforce, fixing Fairchild’s mistake. Equity for talent, capital for defectors: the operating system of Silicon Valley was written.

The power law

A distribution in which the biggest outcome captures a disproportionate, often majority, share of all value — “the rich get richer” dynamics of feedback and compounding. In venture portfolios, one investment routinely returns more than all the others combined, so rational venture investors optimize for the magnitude of wins, not the frequency of them. The cost of missing a Google dwarfs the cost of funding ten failures.

Chapter 2

Finance without finance: ARD, Davis & Rock, and the limited partnership

If Rock supplied the spirit, who supplied the structure? The first organized attempt was Boston’s American Research and Development (ARD), founded in 1946 by Georges Doriot, a French-born Harvard Business School professor with the bearing of a general. ARD made the single most spectacular early venture bet: in 1957 it put about $70,000 into Digital Equipment Corporation, the minicomputer startup of MIT engineers Ken Olsen and Harlan Anderson, for a 70 percent stake. By the early 1970s that position was worth on the order of $350 million — a return of several thousandfold, and a perfect, premature demonstration of the power law: DEC alone outweighed everything else ARD ever did.

Yet ARD was a dead end, because its structure fought its mission. Doriot had organized it as a publicly traded closed-end fund, which put it under SEC supervision: regulators second-guessed its valuations, and the structure made it nearly impossible to give staff stock in portfolio companies. Doriot preached service and sacrifice while his young investors watched the entrepreneurs they funded get rich; one by one the best of them quit — most consequentially to found Greylock. ARD also took 70 percent of a startup, founder incentives be damned. The East Coast had the capital and the prestige; it had the wrong contract.

The right contract was worked out in the West. In 1959, Draper, Gaither & Anderson in Palo Alto became the first venture firm organized as a limited partnership: passive limited partners supply the money for a fixed term, general partners invest it and keep a share of the profits. In 1961 Arthur Rock moved to San Francisco and, with the evangelical ex-broadcaster Tommy Davis, raised Davis & Rock: roughly $3.4 million, a seven-year life, and 20 percent carried interest for the partners — the profit share whose lineage runs back through whaling captains and the medieval shipping ventures from which the term “carry” descends. The time limit forced discipline (you cannot sit on positions forever); the carry aligned the partners with the upside; the partnership’s small size forced concentration. Davis preached backing intense, missionary people; Rock said he was investing in “intellectual book value” — talent not yet priced. Their defining bet was Scientific Data Systems, the computer company of the brash Max Palevsky, into which they put $257,000. When the fund wound up in 1968 it had multiplied its capital well over twentyfold — the best record in the young industry — and SDS, which Xerox soon bought for roughly $900 million, accounted for the overwhelming bulk of it. One grand slam; power law confirmed.

A second West Coast firm, Sutter Hill Ventures, contributed the next clause of the social contract. In 1973 it backed Qume, a maker of daisy-wheel printers founded by the engineer David Lee, on a template that became standard: the founder accepts an experienced outside CEO; both get serious equity; the venture firm takes convertible preferred stock, senior to the founders’ common, so investors are protected on the downside while founders are motivated on the upside. When ITT bought Qume in 1978 for $164 million, Lee got rich, his hired president got rich, and the “Qume model” — equity-sharing professionalization — entered the playbook. Mallaby’s label for all this is finance without finance: a system that evaluates people and markets rather than balance sheets and collateral, because startups have neither.

The venture limited partnership

A fixed-life fund (classically ten years) in which limited partners commit capital and general partners invest it, charging a management fee and keeping carried interest — typically 20 percent of profits. The structure escapes the regulation that strangled ARD, forces exits, and ties the investors’ wealth to outlier wins. Combined with stock options for employees and convertible preferred for investors, it is the contractual core of Silicon Valley.

Chapter 3

Sequoia, Kleiner Perkins, and activist capital

In 1972, two firms opened that would dominate the next half century, and each embodied a different answer to the question Rock had left open: should investors merely pick people, or actively build companies? Don Valentine — a blunt, intimidating ex-Fairchild and National Semiconductor sales chief — founded Sequoia Capital on the heretical view that markets matter more than résumés. Tom Perkins — a flamboyant Hewlett-Packard executive — founded Kleiner Perkins with Eugene Kleiner of the Traitorous Eight, on the view that venture capitalists should get inside the company and engineer the risk out of it. Both rejected Rock’s arms-length style; this was activist capital.

Valentine’s proof case was Atari. Nolan Bushnell’s video-game company was, by any banker’s standard, uninvestable: board discussions happened in a hot tub, the air was thick with marijuana smoke, and management was chaos. Valentine, who could tolerate any amount of disorder if the market was big enough, saw that Pong addressed an enormous consumer market. He invested, then acted: he helped steer Atari to the home version of Pong and to the distribution deal with Sears, Roebuck that put it under American Christmas trees, and in 1976 he engineered the sale of Atari to Warner Communications for $28 million. The return was modest by later standards, but the method was the point: a venture capitalist had taken a company too messy for anyone else and converted it into an outcome through hands-on intervention.

Perkins went further: he incubated. Convinced that the way to handle risk was to identify what he called the white-hot risk — the single uncertainty most likely to kill the company — and spend the first, smallest dollars eliminating it, he structured ventures as sequences of experiments. Tandem Computers (1974), which grew out of an idea developed inside Kleiner Perkins itself, built fault-tolerant computers for banks and became a major win. But the masterpiece was Genentech. In 1976, Robert Swanson — a twenty-eight-year-old who had been let go from Kleiner Perkins’s staff — cold-called the UCSF biochemist Herbert Boyer and proposed commercializing recombinant DNA. Perkins put in an initial $100,000 for a quarter of the company, but on the condition that Genentech own almost nothing: rather than build laboratories, it subcontracted the white-hot experiment — could a synthetic gene be made to express a human protein in bacteria? — to academic labs at UCSF, Caltech, and the City of Hope. Each time a milestone was hit, a risk died, the valuation stepped up, and the next round of capital bought the next experiment. Genentech synthesized somatostatin, then human insulin; at its October 1980 IPO the stock, priced at $35, touched $88 within an hour — the fastest first-day run-up Wall Street had seen — and Kleiner Perkins’s few hundred thousand dollars of early money compounded into one of the great venture positions of the era. Staged, milestone-priced financing became biotech’s standard, and the broader lesson became venture’s: capital is not just selected risk, it is managed risk.

Staged financing and the white-hot risk

Perkins’s doctrine: identify the riskiest assumption in the venture, spend the first and cheapest dollars killing it, and release further capital in tranches as milestones retire risks — each tranche at a higher price. The startup is financed as a series of experiments; investors buy information as much as equity; dilution falls as proof accumulates.

Chapter 4

The whispering of Apple

The Apple chapter answers a sly question: if individual venture capitalists are such brilliant pickers, why did nearly all of them hesitate over the best deal of the decade? In 1976–77 Steve Jobs was a barefoot, abrasive twenty-one-year-old with a sideline in fruitarian diets, and almost everyone fumbled him. Nolan Bushnell passed. Don Valentine visited the garage, was repelled, and demanded adult supervision before he would consider it — but, crucially, he passed the lead to Mike Markkula, a marketing-minded Intel alumnus who had retired rich at thirty-three on his stock options. Markkula became the company-builder: he put in $91,000, personally guaranteed a credit line of a quarter million dollars, wrote the business plan, and took roughly a third of Apple.

Then the network closed in, whisper by whisper. Arthur Rock — tipped by Markkula and by Intel connections — invested $57,600. Venrock, the Rockefeller family’s venture arm, put in around $300,000. Valentine’s Sequoia invested as well, though Valentine sold Sequoia’s position in 1979 for a quick low-multiple profit — a mistake he spent years metabolizing into doctrine about holding winners. When Apple went public in December 1980, in the most celebrated stock offering since Ford in 1956, the market valued it at roughly $1.8 billion, and everyone who had touched the deal looked like a genius.

Mallaby’s point cuts against the lone-genius mythology of his own protagonists. No single investor “called” Apple; several nearly missed it; one sold catastrophically early. What worked was the system: a dense, gossiping, deal-sharing network in which a fundable company gets many chances to be funded, and capital flows toward the signal through repeated, partially redundant judgments. The Valley’s superpower was never one picker’s eye — it was that the network, taken as a whole, was an efficient machine for matching unreasonable people with risk capital. This is also the book’s first explicit nod to path dependence: once Rock, Sequoia, and Venrock were stamped on Apple’s cap table, their brands compounded, whatever the wobbles of the underlying judgment.

Chapter 5

Cisco, 3Com, and the Valley ascendant

The early 1980s delivered venture capital’s first boom-bust cycle. Policy had opened the floodgates: the capital-gains tax was cut from 49 to 28 percent in 1978 and lower still in 1981, and a 1979 clarification of ERISA’s “prudent man” rule let pension funds invest in venture funds. Money under management exploded from a few hundred million dollars a year of new commitments to billions, and the new money chased fashion — most notoriously into disk drives, where dozens of nearly identical Winchester-drive startups were funded simultaneously, guaranteeing that most would die. The 1983 IPO froth (this was the era of Osborne’s collapse and Eagle Computer’s misadventures) gave way to a mid-decade slump in which venture returns sagged into single digits and commentators wondered if Japan, not the Valley, owned the future. Mallaby’s reading: the power law cuts both ways — crowded, copycat capital produces clustered failure — but busts purge tourists while the durable firms compound their advantages.

Two networking companies show what the durable firms had learned. Bob Metcalfe, co-inventor of Ethernet, founded 3Com and shopped it in 1981 with a self-confident insistence on a rich valuation. Venture capitalists balked at his price; Metcalfe spent months hunting for better terms, market sentiment shifted beneath him, and he ended up closing on terms little better than the first offers — having burned time, the one resource a startup cannot recover. He then accepted the Valley bargain in full: the seasoned HP manager Bill Krause came in as president, Metcalfe took the technical-visionary lane, and 3Com prospered. The episode is Mallaby’s tidy parable about the option value of capital and the price of founder vanity.

Cisco is the harder, more famous story. Sandy Lerner and Len Bosack, the husband-and-wife Stanford staffers whose multiprotocol router let incompatible networks talk to each other, were turned down by something like seventy-five investors — the product was brilliant, the founders were combustible, the company was already selling routers out the door with no real management. Don Valentine, the connoisseur of great-market/messy-people situations, did the deal in late 1987 on activist terms: Sequoia put in $2.5 million for roughly a third of the company, with the founders’ stock subject to vesting and with Valentine holding effective authority to shape the management team. He installed himself as chairman, hired the unflashy operator John Morgridge as CEO in 1988, and took Cisco public in February 1990 at a valuation around $224 million. Six months later, after Cisco’s vice presidents collectively threatened to quit over Lerner’s volcanic management style, she was fired; Bosack resigned in solidarity, and the two sold their stock — before Cisco’s decade-long run to become, briefly in 2000, the most valuable company on earth. It is the canonical case of founder replacement creating enormous value, the high-water mark of investor power — and, Mallaby notes, the kind of episode whose retelling among founders would eventually fuel the revolt against it.

Chapter 6

Planners and improvisers: Accel, UUNET, and Netscape

Can venture skill be systematized? Accel, founded in 1983 by Arthur Patterson and Jim Swartz, made the most deliberate attempt. Borrowing Pasteur’s dictum that chance favors only the prepared mind, Accel specialized — telecoms and software — and mapped its territories in advance so that when an opportunity surfaced, the partnership could move with conviction while generalists were still doing homework. Accel’s partners codified rules of thumb (among them the “90 percent rule”: even successful startups end up far from their founding plan, so back adaptable teams in deep markets), and treated the prepared mind as the answer to the unsettling possibility that venture returns are luck.

The chapter’s irony is that Accel’s greatest 1990s triumph proved both the power and the limits of planning. UUNET was a nonprofit supplying internet connectivity to researchers when its engineer-founder Rick Adams decided to commercialize it. The internet in 1993 was not an obvious commercial category — the U.S. government still ran the backbone, and Mosaic was brand new — but prepared-mind telecom investors could see what generalists could not. Accel and its co-investors (with NEA’s Peter Barris recruiting the operator John Sidgmore as CEO) financed UUNET’s buildout; when Microsoft chose UUNET to build the dial-up network for MSN, the company became the arterial system of the consumer internet. UUNET went public in 1995 and was acquired by MFS in 1996 in a deal valuing it around $2 billion — a grand slam, and a textbook case of specialization converting an unfashionable sector into proprietary insight. The improviser’s counterpoint was Netscape: Jim Clark, fresh from Silicon Graphics and bitter about how little of it he had owned, recruited Marc Andreessen and the Mosaic team, and John Doerr of Kleiner Perkins invested about $5 million in 1994 at a valuation other investors considered indefensible — there was no plan worth the name, only the fastest-spreading software artifact anyone had seen. On August 9, 1995, Netscape’s IPO priced at $28, opened at $71, and closed with the sixteen-month-old company worth over $2 billion. The internet era’s defining lesson — speed and audacity beat diligence when the wave is big enough — was set, for better and for worse.

The Internet had ushered in “the largest legal creation of wealth in the history of the planet.”— John Doerr, as quoted in the book

The prepared mind

Accel’s doctrine of specialization: study a sector continuously so that judgment is formed before the deal appears. Preparation converts apparent luck into recognizable pattern — the firm that has mapped networking sees UUNET’s significance in 1993. It is Mallaby’s first major piece of evidence that venture skill is real, repeatable practice rather than dice-rolling.

Chapter 7

Benchmark, SoftBank, and “everyone needs $100 million”

The late 1990s produced two opposite theories of the venture firm, and Mallaby stages them as a duel. Benchmark, founded in 1995, was venture as craft: five (initially four) equal partners, equal pay, a deliberately small fund — about $85 million — on the theory that judgment does not scale and that small funds keep incentives pure. Its monument is eBay. In 1997 Bob Kagle backed Pierre Omidyar’s auction site with $6.7 million for roughly a fifth of the company — a business that was, almost uniquely for the era, already profitable, its growth powered by Beanie Baby traders. Benchmark recruited Meg Whitman from Hasbro as CEO, eBay went public in September 1998, and within a year Benchmark’s stake was worth around $5 billion — at the time the most lucrative venture investment ever made, a roughly 700-fold return that became the industry’s benchmark grand slam and the subject of the book eBoys.

Masayoshi Son’s SoftBank was venture as artillery. Son, a Korean-Japanese outsider who had made a fortune in software distribution and tech publishing, grasped earlier than anyone that if the power law is true, the rational move is to force your way into the certain winners with overwhelming checks — and that capital itself could be a weapon, not just a vote. In 1995–96 he invested a couple of million dollars in Yahoo, then came back within months and put in more than $100 million for over 30 percent of the company on the eve of its IPO — a sum with no precedent in venture history. When Jerry Yang resisted, Son’s negotiating method was naked: he asked who Yahoo’s strongest competitors were and made clear he would fund Excite instead if refused. The chapter title is Son’s worldview compressed — in the internet land grab, everyone needs $100 million, and whoever supplies it owns the future. For a moment it worked beyond imagination: in February 2000 Son was, on paper, briefly richer than Bill Gates. Then the crash vaporized more than 90 percent of SoftBank’s value, and the Valley drew the comfortable conclusion that Son was a gambler who had gotten lucky once. Mallaby flags the conclusion as premature: Son had discovered something true — growth-stage capital deployed at blitz scale could bend outcomes — and the industry would spend the 2010s relearning it from him.

Chapter 8

Money for Google, kind of for nothing

Google marks the hinge of the whole book: the moment bargaining power flipped from capital to founders. Larry Page and Sergey Brin took their first money in 1998 with imperial nonchalance — Andy Bechtolsheim famously wrote a $100,000 check to “Google Inc.” before the company existed, and roughly a million dollars of angel money (Jeff Bezos among the angels) followed. By mid-1999 the founders wanted institutional money, but only on their terms: they summoned the Valley’s two most prestigious firms and demanded both. Kleiner Perkins and Sequoia — John Doerr and Michael Moritz, rivals who almost never shared deals — each invested about $12.5 million at a valuation around $100 million, an extraordinary price for a pre-revenue search engine with no business model. The founders kept voting control, conceding only a promise to hire an experienced CEO.

They then spent two years not honoring it. Page and Brin interviewed and dismissed candidates until Doerr, instead of forcing the issue Cisco-style, arranged for them to meet a procession of admired tech leaders — and the founders finally accepted Eric Schmidt in 2001, less as a boss than as a chaperone they had chosen. The 2004 IPO completed the inversion: Google went public via an unbankerly Dutch auction and, more consequentially, with dual-class stock that gave founder shares ten votes each, entrenching Page and Brin against public shareholders and investors alike.

Google is not a conventional company. We do not intend to become one.— Larry Page, “An Owner’s Manual” for Google’s Shareholders, 2004 IPO letter

Why did capital accept all this? Mallaby’s answer is structural, not sentimental. Money had become abundant — too many funds chasing the few companies that matter under a power law — while Google-class founders were scarce; the scarce party sets terms. And Google’s staggering success laundered the new arrangement into a norm: founder control stopped looking like a governance defect and started looking like the signature of greatness. Every founder who came after — Zuckerberg above all — would cite the precedent. The title’s joke is precise: Google got the Valley’s most prestigious money, kind of for nothing.

Chapter 9

Peter Thiel, Y Combinator, and the Valley’s youth revolt

What Google began as precedent, the PayPal generation turned into ideology. PayPal — formed in 2000 by the merger of Peter Thiel and Max Levchin’s Confinity with Elon Musk’s X.com — survived the dot-com crash by raising $100 million in March 2000, days before the window slammed shut, then endured a coup in which Musk was replaced as CEO by Thiel while flying off on his honeymoon. eBay bought PayPal in 2002 for $1.5 billion, and its alumni — the “PayPal mafia” of Thiel, Musk, Levchin, Reid Hoffman, the YouTube founders, and others — fanned out with money, networks, and a grudge. They had been jerked around by investors and by Sequoia’s preference for adult supervision, and they concluded that venture capital’s traditional power — especially its habit of firing founders — was not discipline but value destruction.

Thiel built the grudge into a firm. Founders Fund, launched in 2005, advertised that it had never removed a founder and never would; it let founders sell some personal stock early (taking the desperation out of the cap table), and it treated eccentricity as signal — the power law implies the biggest wins look crazy, so a fund optimized for consensus comfort is optimized for mediocrity. Thiel’s own record made the argument: a $500,000 angel check into Facebook in 2004 for roughly a tenth of the company, and a 2008 Founders Fund investment of $20 million into SpaceX when Musk’s rockets were exploding and consensus had him finished. Meanwhile Paul Graham and Jessica Livingston attacked from below. Y Combinator (2005) industrialized the seed stage: small standardized checks — on the order of five figures per startup — for single-digit equity, founders admitted in batches, trained by peers and alumni rather than gray-haired board members. Graham’s essays (one was titled “A Unified Theory of VC Suckage”) gave the youth revolt its scripture: founders should retain control, raise less, and treat VCs as service providers.

The incumbents’ flank was turned, and in 2009 Marc Andreessen and Ben Horowitz completed the rout from inside. Andreessen Horowitz raised a $300 million debut fund on an explicitly founder-friendly thesis: technical founders should run their companies, and the firm’s job is to supply what they lack — recruiting, marketing, sales introductions — through a large paid staff, an agency model borrowed from Hollywood’s CAA. a16z paid aggressive prices, marketed itself like a startup, and within three years managed $2.7 billion. The Cisco-era bargain — our money for your obedience — was dead; the new bargain was our money, our services, your company.

Chapter 10

To China, and stir

Could the model travel? The China chapter is Mallaby’s natural experiment, and it opens with the most painful trade in the book. In 1999 Shirley Lin of Goldman Sachs backed a charismatic former English teacher named Jack Ma: Goldman led a $5 million investment for half of Alibaba, brushing past Ma’s protests about control. In 2004 Goldman — which had no framework for power-law patience — sold the stake for about $22 million, a tidy sevenfold gain that forfeited what would have been one of the largest investment profits in history. The investor who understood what Goldman held was Masayoshi Son: after a brief meeting with Ma, Son invested $20 million in early 2000 for roughly a third of Alibaba, ignored every interim panic, and at Alibaba’s 2014 IPO held a stake worth on the order of $58 billion — the single greatest venture-style investment ever recorded, and the vindication of Son’s whole theory of concentrated, conviction-heavy capital.

Through the 2000s, U.S.-trained investors manufactured a Chinese venture industry from imported parts. SAIF’s Andy Yan put $40 million into the online-games company Shanda and saw it returned more than tenfold within two years of its 2004 IPO. Kathy Xu of Capital Today staked $10 million on Richard Liu’s JD.com in 2007 and rode it to billions. Qiming Venture Partners, co-founded in 2006 by the American Gary Rieschel with Duane Kuang, became Mallaby’s model transplant: U.S.-style limited partnerships and power-law discipline, but run by empowered local partners (JP Gan, Nisa Leung) making local judgments — an approach that produced hits like Xiaomi. The instructive failure was Kleiner Perkins, which entered China in 2007 by remote control from Sand Hill Road, imposed its U.S. enthusiasms (cleantech among them) on partners it churned through, and flopped. The lesson generalizes: the technology of venture capital — the partnership, the carry, the staged rounds, the equity culture — transplants beautifully, but only with full local autonomy.

Mallaby draws a larger conclusion that sets up the book’s ending. Nothing about Silicon Valley’s soil — Stanford, the weather, the defense contracts — explains China’s eruption of venture-backed giants; what traveled was a financial technology and the people trained in it. That is evidence that venture capital is a skill that can be learned and exported. It is also, he warns, evidence that America’s lead is contestable: by the late 2010s Chinese venture funding was, in some quarters, rivaling America’s.

Chapter 11

Accel, Facebook, and the decline of Kleiner Perkins

The Facebook deal is the book’s finest set piece of venture craft under the new founder-friendly rules. In early 2005 Facebook was a college network with a twenty-year-old CEO whose business cards read “I’m CEO … bitch,” and the hot social-network comparison was Friendster’s collapse and MySpace’s cheap sale. Accel’s prepared mind had been deliberately rebuilt for exactly this: after missing Google and the Web 2.0 turn, the firm had ordered its partners to find the next consumer-internet platform. Associate-turned-partner Kevin Efrusy hunted Facebook for weeks, was ignored, and finally walked into its Palo Alto office uninvited on a Friday afternoon. What he found — graffiti, twenty-somethings, and engagement metrics showing most users returning daily — was a power-law signature. Accel’s Jim Breyer closed the deal over dinner by doing what the era demanded: he paid up, massively. Accel led a $12.7 million Series A at a $98 million post-money valuation — a price most of the Valley considered insane for a company with trivial revenue — with Breyer adding roughly $1 million personally.

The deal nearly didn’t happen, because Zuckerberg had already shaken hands with Don Graham of the Washington Post Company on friendlier, lower-priced terms. Mallaby relishes the scene: Zuckerberg, tormented, was found by a colleague in the bathroom in tears, and called Graham to confess Accel’s richer offer. Graham, gracefully, released him — concluding that a founder who agonized that hard was worth admiring. Accel’s stake was worth on the order of $12 billion by the time of Facebook’s IPO era: the prepared mind, plus a willingness to pay what the power law says winners are worth, had produced one of the great venture outcomes of all time.

The same years broke Kleiner Perkins. Mallaby’s autopsy is unsentimental: the Valley’s most storied firm missed essentially the entire social-web generation — Facebook, Twitter, and their kin — because John Doerr had redirected the partnership into cleantech, declaring green technology the largest economic opportunity of the twenty-first century and raising dedicated green funds (with Al Gore aboard as partner). The thesis wasn’t dishonorable, but the execution violated venture’s own rules: capital-hungry, regulation-dependent, commodity-pricing businesses do not produce power-law software returns on venture timelines. Returns sagged for a decade; star partners left; the celebrity-hiring culture (Colin Powell, Gore) substituted for sourcing discipline; and the 2015 Ellen Pao discrimination trial, whatever its verdict, put the firm’s internal dysfunction on public display. The pair of stories is the chapter’s argument: in a power-law business, one prepared, decisive yes (Accel) and one thematic wrong turn (Kleiner) are the difference between dynasty and decline.

Chapter 12

A Russian, a Tiger, and the rise of growth equity

In May 2009, with the financial crisis still smoking and Facebook struggling to raise money at the valuation it wanted, an unknown Russian named Yuri Milner offered Mark Zuckerberg $200 million at a $10 billion valuation — no board seat, and his shares’ votes pledged to Zuckerberg’s proxy. The Valley assumed dumb money. Milner was the opposite of dumb: running Mail.ru in Moscow, he had assembled comparative data on Facebook clones around the world — vKontakte in Russia and others — and could see that their revenue per user ran far ahead of Facebook’s, because Facebook had barely begun to monetize. His model said $10 billion was cheap. He sweetened the structure with a second innovation: a tender offer buying employees’ common stock at a lower price than the preferred, blending down his effective entry price while giving staff liquidity without an IPO. Milner’s DST repeated the playbook — Zynga, Twitter, Spotify, Airbnb — and made billions.

The deal crystallized a new asset class. Companies were staying private for a decade or more; someone had to supply IPO-scale capital privately, and whoever did it on founder-friendly terms — high prices, no control, fast decisions — would win the allocation. The hedge-fund world had been converging on the same discovery from another direction. Tiger Global, run by Julian Robertson’s protégé Chase Coleman with Scott Shleifer, had bought the despised Chinese portals — Sina, Sohu, NetEase — after the crash for a few times earnings and ridden them up severalfold; when the best internet companies in China turned out to be private, Tiger created private funds in 2003 and invested as a sort of research-driven index of global internet leaders, applying public-market comparables across countries (the e-commerce leader in each market, the travel portal in each market) and moving at hedge-fund speed without seeking board seats. Mallaby treats Milner and Tiger as the second great power shift to founders: where a16z had made early-stage capital founder-friendly, growth capital now made the public market itself optional. Companies could reach world scale — and founders and employees could get rich — without ever facing the discipline of public shareholders. The consequences, good and bad, fill the last chapters.

Growth equity

Late-stage private capital — checks of $100 million and up — supplied to companies that in earlier eras would already be public, typically at high valuations, without board control, often paired with tenders that let employees and early investors cash out privately. Pioneered in spirit by Son, systematized by Milner and Tiger Global, it extended the power law’s logic past the IPO and dissolved the boundary between venture capital and public-market investing.

Chapter 13

Sequoia’s strength in numbers

If venture success were mostly luck, no firm could stay on top for five decades. Sequoia is Mallaby’s counterexample, and this chapter is his case study in deliberate, designed excellence. Don Valentine handed the firm to Michael Moritz — a Welsh ex-journalist with a killer instinct for narrative and people — and Doug Leone, a relentless Italian-immigrant salesman; the two institutionalized succession (rare in a business of ego) and rebuilt the team around hungry operators rather than financiers: Roelof Botha (PayPal’s CFO), Jim Goetz (a networking entrepreneur), and later Alfred Lin. Internally, Sequoia engineered against psychology: pre-mortems and structured memos to fight hype, public dissection of misses (passing on deals that became hits) rather than just losses, and team-first compensation. Its October 2008 all-hands warning to portfolio companies — the famous “R.I.P. Good Times” deck — and its March 2020 “Black Swan” memo typified the institutional reflex to act early and collectively.

The machine produced. Goetz’s preparation in mobile — he tracked app-store data systematically — led Sequoia to WhatsApp, where it became the sole venture investor across every round: roughly $60 million in for about a fifth of the company. When Facebook paid $19 billion for WhatsApp in 2014, Sequoia’s position was worth around $3 billion, a 50-fold return earned not by a lucky cocktail-party encounter but by a system built to see the anomaly first. Sequoia widened the funnel with its scout program — seeding trusted founders with Sequoia money to angel-invest, which both surfaced deals early and quietly extended the network — and stretched the duration of its capital with the Heritage endowment-style vehicle and a public-markets arm, so that it could hold compounding winners instead of distributing them.

The boldest experiment was Sequoia China. In 2005 Moritz and Leone recruited Neil Shen — co-founder of Ctrip and Home Inns, the rare person who was both a proven founder and a natural investor — and gave him genuine autonomy (after early friction proved remote control unworkable, exactly as it had for Kleiner). Shen built arguably the best venture franchise in the world. His signature conviction was Wang Xing, a serial founder he backed repeatedly until Wang’s Meituan — group-buying, then food delivery, then the everything-app of Chinese local services — emerged from the brutal “war of a thousand Groupons.” Sequoia China led Meituan’s roughly $12 million Series A in 2010 and kept investing; after Meituan merged with Dianping and went public in Hong Kong in 2018, it climbed past a $100 billion valuation, and Sequoia’s stake ranked among the most valuable venture positions ever built. Strength in numbers, in both senses: a deep team, and returns that compounded by design.

Chapter 14

Unicorn poker

By 2013, when Aileen Lee coined “unicorn” for private companies worth $1 billion, there were thirty-nine; within a few years there were hundreds, and the late-stage capital glut had created a game Mallaby calls unicorn poker — players shoving billion-dollar chips at founders, hoping to bluff or buy their way into the power law, with governance as the ante nobody wanted to post. The chapter is three governance failures, carefully distinguished.

Theranos, he argues, is the failure that proves the system’s residual sanity: Elizabeth Holmes raised her billions largely from outside the experienced venture world — family offices, media-dazzled magnates, a board of statesmen — while specialist health-tech venture investors, who asked for the data and the audited results that Holmes would not supply, declined. Uber is the harder case because the company was real and enormous. Benchmark’s Bill Gurley led its roughly $11–12 million Series A in 2011 at a valuation around $60 million — a brilliant bet that became a prison. Travis Kalanick’s combativeness, which had been an asset against taxi cartels, curdled into a culture of lawbreaking (Greyball) and harassment exposed by Susan Fowler’s February 2017 blog post; and because the unicorn era had stripped investors of leverage — founder-controlled shares, no IPO pressure, an infinite queue of replacement capital — Gurley had to fight with improvised weapons. In June 2017 Benchmark and allied investors delivered an ultimatum to Kalanick in a Chicago hotel and forced his resignation; Benchmark then sued him outright, and SoftBank’s subsequent tender offer — buying heavily at a roughly 30 percent discount to the headline valuation — recapitalized the board under Dara Khosrowshahi. Uber’s 2019 IPO, at a valuation barely above its last private rounds, quantified what governance failure had cost.

WeWork is the reductio. Masayoshi Son toured Adam Neumann’s office-rental company for all of twelve minutes in 2016, told him his problem was thinking too small — that being crazy was the point — and the Vision Fund, Son’s $100 billion vehicle bankrolled largely by Saudi Arabia, ultimately poured in billions (an initial $4.4 billion alone), pricing WeWork at $47 billion. Capital at that scale didn’t fund Neumann’s discipline; it dissolved it: super-voting shares, self-dealing (he personally trademarked “We” and sold it to the company for $5.9 million, later returned), tequila-soaked management. The 2019 S-1 exposed all of it to public-market eyes, the IPO died, the valuation collapsed toward $8 billion, and SoftBank paid Neumann lavishly to leave. Mallaby’s verdict: the unicorn disasters were not failures of venture capital’s core method but of its abandonment — the old craft of staged money and engaged boards displaced by passive megachecks chasing the power law without its discipline.

Unicorn poker

Mallaby’s name for late-2010s late-stage investing: with capital abundant, founders entrenched, and IPOs optional, investors competed by escalating check size and valuation rather than by diligence or governance — a game where the price of a seat at the table was surrendering the controls that traditionally protected the money.

Conclusion

Luck, skill, and the competition among nations

The conclusion confronts the skeptic’s case head-on. Venture returns are persistent across funds — unusual in finance — but the obvious explanation is path dependence, not skill: one early hit (Kleiner’s Genentech, Sequoia’s Apple) gives a firm a brand; the brand attracts the best founders; the best founders deliver the next hit, regardless of the partners’ acumen. Entrenched advantage masquerading as genius. Mallaby concedes the loop is real, then assembles three rebuttals. First, the entrenchment story cannot explain entry: Benchmark, Y Combinator, Founders Fund, a16z, DST, and Tiger Global all broke into the top tier from nothing within the era the book covers, each by inventing a genuinely new method — small-fund craft, batch seed investing, founder-aligned contrarianism, the services platform, data-driven growth checks. In a truly closed loop, newcomers don’t crack the cap tables of the decade’s best companies. Second, the methods themselves are observable, teachable practice — Perkins’s risk-staging, Accel’s prepared mind, Sequoia’s team engineering — and firms that abandon practice (Kleiner in the 2000s) visibly decay, which luck cannot explain. Third, the transplant test: the same financial technology, carried to China by trained practitioners and granted local autonomy, produced the same power-law results in alien soil.

Skill being real, the stakes are national. Mallaby closes as a policy writer: venture capital is a strategic asset in the U.S.–China competition, and history shows government shapes it mostly by getting the enabling conditions right — funded basic science, deep capital markets, favorable capital-gains treatment, the ERISA-style rules that let institutional money flow, and above all open immigration, since a startling share of America’s most valuable startups have immigrant founders. He is equally clear about venture’s defects: a monoculture of white and Asian men (women held only a small fraction of senior investing roles), herding into fashionable sectors, and the governance abdications of the unicorn era. But the closing argument is affirmative. Most of finance redistributes claims on existing assets; venture capital, almost alone, manufactures new ones — it is the financial system’s mechanism for converting unreasonable people into new industries. Societies that want new futures should want more of it, done better, and should understand the power law that drives it: the future is built by betting on long shots, and the long shots that hit pay for all the rest.

Reading it critically

Reviewers broadly admired the reporting — Mallaby interviewed hundreds of insiders over five years — but several criticisms recur. First, access bias: a book built on cooperation from Sequoia, Accel, and a16z is gentler toward its sources than a fully arms-length history would be, and skeptics (notably in left-leaning and academic reviews) argue Mallaby underweights the government’s role — DARPA, NASA, semiconductor procurement — in creating the demand and science that venture capital later harvested. Second, the luck-versus-skill verdict remains contested: the academic evidence he leans on shows persistence at top firms but cannot cleanly separate skill from access, and his three rebuttals are suggestive rather than decisive. Third, timing: the book appeared at the very top of the 2021–22 cycle. The subsequent crash hit its more bullish exhibits hard — Tiger Global suffered historic losses, SoftBank’s Vision Fund bled tens of billions, and the China chapters now read as a period piece, since Beijing’s post-2020 crackdown and U.S. outbound-investment restrictions dismantled much of the U.S.–China venture bridge (Sequoia itself split off its China arm as HongShan in 2023). None of this overturns the central argument about the power law, but it underlines the book’s own warning: the industry’s discipline is cyclical, and its geopolitics moved faster than its publisher.

If you remember ten things

  1. Venture returns follow a power law: one grand slam outweighs the rest of the portfolio, so rational VCs maximize the magnitude of wins and treat frequent failure as the cost of admission.
  2. Arthur Rock invented “liberation capital” with the Fairchild deal of 1957 — money that turns frustrated employees into founders — and the equity-for-talent norm it spawned is Silicon Valley’s real operating system.
  3. The limited partnership (Draper Gaither & Anderson 1959, Davis & Rock 1961) beat ARD’s public-fund model because fixed fund lives, 20 percent carry, and option-rich startups aligned everyone with the upside.
  4. Activist capital built companies, not just portfolios: Valentine turned chaotic Atari and unfundable Cisco into outcomes, and Perkins’s Genentech showed how staged, milestone-priced financing kills the “white-hot risk” first.
  5. Apple proved the system matters more than any single picker: most VCs fumbled the deal, but the Valley’s gossiping network gave a fundable company many chances to be funded.
  6. Accel’s “prepared mind” (UUNET) and Sequoia’s engineered teamwork (WhatsApp, succession, scouts) are Mallaby’s core evidence that venture skill is deliberate practice, not dice.
  7. Google’s 1999 double term sheet and dual-class IPO flipped bargaining power to founders; PayPal’s alumni, Y Combinator, Founders Fund, and a16z turned that flip into ideology and business model.
  8. Son’s Yahoo blitz, Milner’s no-board-seat $200 million Facebook deal at $10 billion, and Tiger Global created growth equity — IPO-scale private money that made public markets optional and founders nearly untouchable.
  9. The unicorn disasters — Theranos, Kalanick’s Uber, Son’s WeWork — were failures of abandoned venture discipline, not of the venture method: megachecks without staged money or engaged boards.
  10. Venture capital transplanted to China and worked, which proves it is exportable skill and makes it a strategic national asset — best cultivated through funded science, open immigration, and capital-market rules that let it flow.

Concepts to keep

The power law

Venture outcomes are radically skewed: most investments return little or nothing, while a handful return thousands of multiples and pay for everything. The rational strategy is therefore to maximize exposure to outliers, not to minimize error — a logic that feels insane from inside normal finance.

Liberation capital

Arthur Rock’s founding move: money whose purpose is to free talented people from institutions that own their work, converting employees into founder-owners. Most of Silicon Valley’s genealogy — Fairchild begat Intel begat everything — is liberation capital iterating on itself.

Stage-by-stage finance

Tom Perkins’s Genentech innovation: release money in tranches, each sized to kill the biggest remaining risk. It converts an unpriceable gamble into a sequence of experiments, and it was the original source of VC leverage over founders — which is why founder-friendly capital later abandoned it.

Grandstanding capital vs. disciplined tranches

Masayoshi Son’s counter-philosophy: a check so large it becomes a weapon, funding blitz-scaling and threatening rivals, with reputation-making boldness as the point. Yahoo, Uber, and WeWork trace the arc from genius to pathology; the tension with Perkins-style discipline is the book’s central dialectic.

Path dependence in networks

Early wins buy access to the best deal flow, which produces more wins: venture skill and venture luck are entangled because success is partly self-fulfilling. This is also why Silicon Valley has proven nearly impossible to replicate by policy — the asset is the accumulated network, not the capital.

The prepared mind

Accel’s discipline of studying a sector deeply enough that when the outlier appears — UUNET, Facebook — you recognize it in hours and move. A useful model for researchers too: serendipity rewards those who have pre-computed what a breakthrough would look like.

Governance vs. founder-friendliness

The pendulum of power between investors and founders. Too much investor control deters the irreverent founders who produce outliers; too little produces Uber, WeWork, and Theranos. Mallaby’s history suggests the pendulum is driven less by ideas than by the supply of capital.

In the author's words

“Most people think improbable ideas are unimportant, but the only thing that’s important is something that’s improbable.” — Vinod Khosla, as quoted by Mallaby in the book’s opening
“You succeed in venture capital by backing the right deals, not by haggling over valuations.” — Sebastian Mallaby, on the arithmetic of power-law investing
Most venture bets fail, but a few succeed at such scale that they more than make up for everything else — and that extreme ratio of failure to success is the power law that drives the whole business. — close paraphrase of Mallaby’s framing of the book’s thesis in the introduction

Threads to other books

Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital — Carlota Perez
Perez supplies the macro theory of which Mallaby is the micro history: her “frenzy” phase, where financial capital overshoots a new technology, is exactly the unicorn era Mallaby documents deal by deal.
The Innovator’s Dilemma — Clayton Christensen
Christensen explains why incumbents cannot fund disruption internally; Mallaby explains the external institution that evolved to fund it instead — the two books are halves of one mechanism.
Chip War — Chris Miller
Fairchild and the Traitorous Eight open both books: Mallaby follows the money that liberated them, Miller follows the silicon they made, and together they show finance and fabrication co-evolving into geopolitics.
Machines of Loving Grace — Dario Amodei
Amodei’s compressed-century optimism is precisely the kind of improbable, unbounded-upside story the power law selects for — reading Mallaby explains why such visions attract billions while incremental science struggles.
For the AI researcher

The power law is the hidden selection function on AI research itself. Venture and venture-like capital will not fund work whose best case is “solid contribution”; it funds work whose best case is a monopoly on a transformed industry. That is why foundation-model labs raise tens of billions while interpretability tooling, evaluation science, and dataset infrastructure scrape for grants — not because investors are foolish, but because the statistical logic Mallaby describes points capital at unbounded outcomes. When you choose a thesis topic or a startup idea, you are choosing where you sit on that distribution, and it is worth doing consciously: some excellent research is structurally unfundable by VC, and pretending otherwise wastes years.

The governance chapters are directly applicable to the lab you may someday join or found. The OpenAI board crisis of 2023, the strange nonprofit-capped-profit structures, the founder worship around AI CEOs — all of it is the founder-friendliness pendulum Mallaby traces from Google through WeWork, now swinging with far higher stakes. His evidence suggests the uncomfortable lesson that boards mostly lose against founders when capital is abundant, and that discipline returns only when money gets scarce. For anyone signing an offer letter heavy with equity, the book is also simply practical: it teaches you to read a cap table, to understand why stage-by-stage finance exists, and to recognize when a Vision-Fund-sized check is a gift versus a trap.

Finally, note Mallaby’s closing argument about national competition. The US–China venture entanglement he documents — Sequoia China’s rise and eventual split into HongShan — previewed the bifurcation of the global AI ecosystem you now work inside. The networks that fund your field are no longer politically neutral plumbing, and the power law now has a flag planted in it.
