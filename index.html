Further along the same threads — recommended next

  • The Structure of Scientific Revolutions — Thomas Kuhn (1962)How fields actually change their minds. Paradigm shifts are the science-internal version of Perez’s surges — essential self-knowledge for a PhD student.
  • Thinking in Systems — Donella Meadows (2008)Stocks, flows, feedback loops, and leverage points — the grammar that every book on this shelf is secretly written in.
  • Why Nations Fail — Daron Acemoglu & James Robinson (2012)Inclusive versus extractive institutions as the deep variable behind prosperity. The institutional complement to Scott’s warning about state power.
  • How Asia Works — Joe Studwell (2013)The actual recipe behind the East Asian miracles — land reform, export discipline, financial repression. Development economics with the abstraction stripped out; pairs with Chip War’s Taiwan and Korea chapters.
  • The Idea Factory — Jon Gertner (2012)Bell Labs and the conditions under which great research organizations produce transistors, information theory, and Unix. A field guide to the kind of institution you may one day build or join.
  • The Dream Machine — M. Mitchell Waldrop (2001)J.C.R. Licklider and the long, state-funded road to personal computing — the prehistory of your own field, and a case study in patient capital that VC cannot supply.
  • The Making of the Atomic Bomb — Richard Rhodes (1986)The last time a small group of scientists knowingly built a world-changing technology under state urgency. The closest historical analogue to frontier AI labs.
  • The Alignment Problem — Brian Christian (2020)The intellectual history of your own subfield — how machine learning’s technical choices became society’s problems, told through the researchers who noticed.
  • Where Is My Flying Car? — J. Storrs Hall (2021)The case that the Great Stagnation was an energy-ceiling choice, not fate — a provocative foil for both Smil’s gradualism and Amodei’s optimism.
