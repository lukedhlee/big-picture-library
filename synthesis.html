The Big Picture

A working model of how the world flows — assembled from seventeen works
Read after any two books Re-read after every book Revise it — this is a draft, like all models

Every book on this shelf is a partial map. Smil maps joules, Perez maps bubbles, Scott maps states, Pearl maps inference itself. Held separately they are interesting; held together they form something better — a layered model of the world in which each layer constrains the ones above it and is steered by the ones below.

The claim of this page is simple: the world is not a blur of unrelated events. It is a stack. Energy and matter set what is physically possible. Firms and capital decide what gets built within those limits. Money and states decide who pays and who is allowed. Information networks and human psychology decide what everyone believes is happening. And running through all of it is the question Pearl forces on us — do we actually know what causes what, or are we just curve-fitting on history?

AI is the rare event that touches every layer at once. That is why it feels confusing from inside, and why a researcher who can see the whole stack has an advantage that no amount of additional model-training intuition can substitute for.

The seven-layer stack

Read from the bottom up. Each layer is anchored by one or two books, and each constrains everything above it: you cannot fund what physics forbids, and you cannot govern what no one believes in.

1
Smil — Energy and Civilization  ·  Yergin — The Prize

Energy: the floor under everything

Civilization is a sequence of energy conversions — muscle to water-wheel to steam to electricity. Every leap in what societies could do was first a leap in how much useful energy they could command, and energy transitions are slow, capital-hungry, and stubbornly material. Whatever story is being told upstairs, the joules must exist. In 2026 this layer has reasserted itself rudely: the binding constraint on AI is increasingly gigawatts, not ideas.

Yergin supplies the political history of this layer: for a century, whoever controlled oil controlled the tempo of war and growth — navies converted, empires redrew the Middle East, embargoes broke presidencies. The Prize is what it looks like when one resource becomes the master variable of geopolitics. Read it once as history and once as a forecast with the noun swapped: compute-plus-energy is auditioning for oil’s old role.

2
Miller — Chip War  ·  Conway — Material World

Matter and compute: the made substrate

One step above raw energy sits the manufactured world, and at its apex the semiconductor — the most complex artifact humans produce, dependent on a supply chain so specialized that single companies (TSMC, ASML) are chokepoints of world-historical importance. This layer teaches that the substrate of intelligence is not abstract: it is etched in specific fabs, on a specific island, under specific export-control regimes. Geography did not stop mattering; it concentrated.

Conway widens the lens below the chip: six materials — sand, salt, iron, copper, oil, lithium — carry the whole modern edifice, mined and refined through chokepoints as obscure as a single quartz mine in North Carolina. His sharpest correction to the “ethereal” digital story: the energy transition and the AI buildout are rematerializations — more copper, more concrete, more refined silicon — not an escape from stuff.

3
Christensen — The Innovator’s Dilemma  ·  West — Scale

Firms: where new technology is born and old firms die

Technologies enter the world through organizations, and organizations have predictable failure modes. Incumbents listen to their best customers, chase margins upmarket, and are eaten from below by products that start as toys. The mechanism is not stupidity — it is rational resource allocation inside an existing value network. Keep this layer in mind whenever an established player dismisses a cheap, “worse” alternative: the dismissal is the syndrome.

West gives the same fact a physicist’s derivation: companies scale sublinearly — efficiency-dominated, bureaucratically rigid, mortal, with a half-life around a decade — while cities scale superlinearly on the open-ended social networks inside them, which is why cities outlive every firm founded in them. Where Christensen explains the mechanism of a particular corporate death, West shows the actuarial table: bigger is never just more of the same.

4
Mallaby — The Power Law  ·  Perez — Technological Revolutions and Financial Capital

Capital: what gets funded, and the rhythm of funding

Two clocks tick here. Mallaby’s is micro: venture returns follow a power law, so rational investors chase outliers, tolerate failure, and systematically over-fund whatever might be the next outlier. Perez’s is macro: every technological revolution for 250 years has run the same arc — irruption, frenzy, a crash at the turning point, then a golden age of deployment once the institutions catch up. The frenzy is not a malfunction; it is how society over-installs infrastructure it later grows into. Railways, electricity, dot-com fiber — and, plausibly, GPU datacenters.

5
Blyth & Fraccaroli — Inflation

Money: the plumbing and its politics

Beneath every investment boom is a monetary regime, and money is never neutral. Inflation — the headline variable of the 2020s — is best understood as distributional conflict: somebody’s price is somebody else’s income, and the story told about inflation’s cause (greedy workers? supply shocks? corporate margins?) determines who is made to pay for the cure. This layer explains why interest rates, capex cycles, and political resentment move together, and why an AI investment boom is also a macroeconomic event.

6
Scott — Seeing Like a State

States: the governors and their blind spots

States see the world through simplifications — maps, censuses, standard measures, last names — because they must. The catastrophes come when four ingredients combine: administrative simplification, high-modernist confidence, authoritarian power, and a civil society too weak to resist. Scott’s deepest concept is metis, the local, practical knowledge that no central plan can capture and every central plan destroys. This is simultaneously a theory of states, a theory of why top-down optimization fails, and — read carefully — a warning label for objective functions.

7
Harari — Nexus  ·  Cialdini — Influence  ·  Hutchins — Cognition in the Wild  ·  Clark & Chalmers — The Extended Mind

Information and minds: the narrative layer

At the top of the stack, societies are held together by information networks that optimize for order, not truth — and by human minds running ancient firmware. Cialdini catalogues the firmware: reciprocity, social proof, authority, scarcity — automatic levers that fire before deliberation. Harari shows what happens at network scale: stories and bureaucracies bind millions, self-correcting mechanisms (or their absence) decide whether errors compound, and AI is the first participant in the network that is not human. The narrative layer feels soft, but it steers all the hard layers below: it decides what gets funded, regulated, feared, and built.

And the “minds” in this layer are not where we think they are. Hutchins shows that real-world cognition is distributed across teams, instruments, and routines — a navy ship’s navigation is computed by a socio-technical system, not by anyone’s brain. Clark & Chalmers push further: when an external tool is reliably available, automatically trusted, and easily consulted, it is not an aid to your mind — it is part of it. Which means AI entering the network is not an alien intrusion into human thinking; it is the newest medium in a cognitive fabric that was never skull-bound to begin with. The design question is what kind of extended minds we are building.

+
Pearl — The Book of Why  ·  Moravec — The Role of Raw Power in Intelligence  ·  Amodei — Machines of Loving Grace

Two meta-layers: how to know, and where to aim

Pearl is not a layer of the world but a lens for all of them: seeing, doing, imagining — the ladder of causation. Most of what passes for knowledge about the stack is rung-one correlation; real intervention requires rung-two causal models; real wisdom requires rung-three counterfactuals. And Amodei supplies the other missing piece — a destination. If powerful AI arrives, the upside is concrete: compressed decades of progress in biology, mental health, development, governance. A map needs a direction of travel to be worth steering by.

Between those two sits the shelf’s oldest bet: Moravec, writing in 1975, argued that AI’s embarrassments proved nothing about its methods — the machines simply had insect-grade compute — and that intelligence would arrive roughly when affordable hardware crossed brain-scale. Half a century later that wager, restated by Sutton as the Bitter Lesson and priced daily in GPU markets, is the live hypothesis connecting the energy and matter layers at the bottom of this stack to the minds at the top.

One story through every book

Here is the whole shelf as a single narrative, told about the present.

A new general-purpose technology — machine intelligence — has irrupted, exactly the kind of event Perez describes as the start of a great surge. Financial capital has decoupled from production capital and flooded in, because Mallaby’s power law makes it rational for every fund to overpay for a shot at the one outlier that returns everything. The frenzy is visible in any week’s headlines.

But the surge is hitting the lower layers. Smil’s constraint binds: datacenters now negotiate for gigawatts, and energy transitions move at the speed of concrete and transmission lines, not software. Miller’s constraint binds too: the entire boom rests on a chip supply chain with single points of failure, which states have begun to treat as the strategic terrain of the century. The frenzy, in other words, is colliding with physics and geopolitics simultaneously.

Inside the industry, Christensen’s dynamics play out in compressed time: incumbents with the best customers and biggest models watch cheap, open, “worse” alternatives improve along trajectories that may overshoot what most users actually need. Meanwhile the boom is a macroeconomic event — Blyth reminds us that the capex wave, the electricity prices, and the eventual question of who pays will be fought out as competing inflation stories, with winners and losers chosen partly by narrative.

Above it all, the governors struggle. States try to make AI legible — licensing regimes, benchmarks, compute thresholds — and Scott predicts both the necessity and the failure modes of those simplifications. The information network through which all of this is understood is itself being transformed by the technology it is trying to describe, which is Harari’s central alarm: the self-correcting mechanisms we rely on — peer review, journalism, democratic feedback — are being re-plumbed mid-flight by systems optimized with Cialdini’s levers built in. And our claims to understand any of this rest, as Pearl insists, mostly on rung-one evidence: correlations from a single historical run, with no counterfactual.

Which is why Amodei’s essay matters as more than optimism. It is an attempt to give the surge a deployment-phase vision worth steering toward — the golden age that Perez says only arrives when institutions, not just technologies, catch up. Whether this surge gets its golden age is not predetermined. It is the live question of your career.

Cross-cutting principles

Patterns that recur on every layer — the actual “lessons” of the shelf.

1. Hard constraints are physical; soft constraints are narrative — and both bind.

Smil and Miller describe limits that no story can talk away; Harari, Blyth, and Cialdini describe stories that allocate trillions. The common error is category confusion: treating energy like a narrative (it will yield to enthusiasm) or treating narratives like physics (they cannot be changed). Know which kind of constraint you are facing.

2. The bubble is part of the mechanism.

Perez’s deepest insight, confirmed by Mallaby: over-funding is how societies install infrastructure ahead of demand. Crashes transfer assets, not possibility. If the AI frenzy ends in a correction, that is the historical norm on the way to deployment — railways, electricity, and fiber all said the same.

3. Medians mislead; tails decide.

Venture returns, research impact, persuasion cascades, pandemic spread, model capabilities: power-law worlds reward different behavior than normal-distribution worlds. In a power-law domain, the rational strategy is more attempts at the tail, not better averages. A PhD is a power-law domain.

4. Top-down simplification destroys the local knowledge it depends on.

Scott’s scientific forests died because the map deleted the understory. The same mechanism appears in Christensen (headquarters metrics delete the low-end market), in Blyth (aggregate indices delete distributional reality), and in machine learning (objective functions delete everything not measured). The fix is the same everywhere: keep channels open to metis — the knowledge of practitioners that resists formalization.

5. Information networks optimize for order, not truth. Self-correction is a design choice.

Harari’s dichotomy generalizes: institutions survive by coordinating people, and coordination tolerates convenient falsehood. Science is the great exception only because it institutionalized error-finding. Any system you build — a model, a lab, a benchmark, a company — should be judged by one question: what is its mechanism for discovering it is wrong?

6. Correlation is rung one. Most arguments about the world never climb higher.

Pearl’s ladder is a portable epistemic audit. History gives us one run, no controls; most macro claims (including several on this page) are rung-one patterns dressed as rung-two mechanisms. Hold the whole model with appropriate looseness — and notice that this critique applies with full force to systems trained on observational data alone.

7. Incumbents fail rationally. Newcomers win at the neglected low end.

Christensen at firm scale, Perez at epoch scale, Mallaby at fund scale, and arguably Scott at state scale: established players over-serve their existing constituency and cannot reallocate toward what looks small. The opportunity is always where the serious people are not looking — which is, encouragingly, actionable advice for a graduate student choosing problems.

8. Bigger is systematically different — and quantity has a quality of its own.

West proves it for organisms, cities, and companies: scaling is sublinear or superlinear, never neutral, so growth always changes the thing growing. Moravec applied the same instinct to intelligence in 1975 — enough raw compute is not a faster version of the same machine but a different kind of machine — and Yergin and Conway show the converse at civilization scale: when a resource’s volume crosses a threshold, it stops being an input and becomes a geopolitics. Before asking whether a system is smart, efficient, or stable, ask what regime of scale it is in.

Reading 2026 with this map

A mental model earns its keep by generating questions about the present. Some it generates now:

Where is the surge? By Perez’s chronology, the AI boom shows classic installation-phase features: capital decoupled from revenue, infrastructure built ahead of demand, paper fortunes. Watch for turning-point signatures — a financial correction plus a regulatory settlement — and remember that deployment golden ages historically follow them.

Which constraint binds first? Energy (Smil), advanced packaging and fab capacity (Miller), capital-market patience (Mallaby/Blyth), or political legitimacy (Scott/Harari)? The binding constraint determines who has leverage. Today the smart money worries about electricity; the smart geopolitics worries about Taiwan; the smart sociology worries about trust.

Is the incumbent set stable? Christensen suggests watching the low end: small open models, cheap inference, and “good-enough” capability for most tasks. If most economic value needs far less than frontier capability, the disruption pattern predicts margin collapse at the top — with the value migrating to whoever owns deployment, not training.

Who pays? Blyth’s question. The capex is real, the electricity is real, and the bill will be narrated onto someone — ratepayers, taxpayers, shareholders, or workers. The inflation story told about AI in the late 2020s will shape its politics as much as any benchmark.

Where one person can push

You asked not just how the world flows but how to contribute. The stack suggests where individual leverage actually lives:

  • Work at a binding constraint. Leverage concentrates where the bottleneck is. Energy-efficient computation, inference economics, evaluation science, causal methods for high-stakes deployment — these sit at constraints the whole stack is pressing against.
  • Build self-correcting mechanisms. Harari’s lesson and science’s secret. Benchmarks that resist gaming, interpretability that lets systems be audited, institutions that find their own errors — this is infrastructure for the deployment phase, and it is underbuilt relative to capability work.
  • Preserve metis. Scott’s lesson applied to AI: the most dangerous systems will be the ones that impose thin simplifications on rich domains — medicine, education, governance. Researchers who deeply know one messy domain and one technical stack are rare and disproportionately useful.
  • Climb the ladder. Pearl’s program — getting machine reasoning from correlation to intervention to counterfactual — remains substantially open, and it is the difference between systems that predict the world and systems that can be trusted to act in it.
  • Aim at the upside. Amodei’s essay is, among other things, a research agenda: biology, mental health, development economics as AI application domains. The frenzy funds capability; the golden age will be made by people who pointed capability at the right problems.

How to use this library

A suggested path, though every order works: start with Smil to get the floor, then Perez for the rhythm — those two are the load-bearing walls. Add Christensen and Mallaby for the engine room, Miller and Blyth for the hard and soft plumbing, Scott and Harari for the governors, Pearl and Cialdini for the lenses, Hutchins and Clark & Chalmers for where minds actually live, and finish with Amodei to point the whole apparatus at the future.

Practical cadence: one book per two or three weeks alongside research. Read the study page here first as a scaffold, read the book itself slowly, then come back to this synthesis and ask what it gets wrong — the moment you can argue with this page, it has done its job. Models of the world are like research taste: built by repeated cycles of prediction, error, and revision.

Questions to carry

Open questions this shelf raises but cannot settle — good companions for a decade of research:

Does the Perez cycle still apply when the technology can improve itself? Is energy a hard ceiling on AI or a solvable engineering problem on a twenty-year horizon? Can a state make AI legible without strangling the metis that makes it work? What is the self-correcting mechanism for a civilization whose information network is partly non-human? Can rung-three reasoning emerge from rung-one data at sufficient scale — or is Pearl’s hierarchy a permanent wall? And the one that contains the others: who, exactly, decides what the golden age is for?

This page is a draft of a worldview, not a conclusion. Revise it every time a book — or an experiment — proves it wrong.
